Writing Module Overview (B2)

The ISE II Writing Module is part of the 2-hour Reading & Writing exam. It consists of 2 writing tasks (Task 3 and Task 4), each requiring you to write 150-180 words in 40 minutes. The tasks test your ability to produce clear, well-structured, and detailed text at B2 level.

Key Differences from ISE I (B1):
  • Longer word count: 150-180 words vs. 100-130 words
  • More complex topics: Abstract subjects requiring evaluation and analysis
  • More bullet points: Task 3 has 3 content points; Task 4 has 2 content points
  • Higher language expectations: B2 grammar structures (conditionals, passives, relative clauses)
  • Synthesis required: Task 3 requires combining information from multiple texts
Important: You must complete both reading tasks (Task 1 and Task 2) before starting the writing tasks. The information from Task 2 is essential for Task 3. Plan your time carefully: 20 minutes for Task 1, 20 minutes for Task 2, 40 minutes for Task 3, and 40 minutes for Task 4.

Task 3: Reading into Writing (40 minutes)
Task Overview: Write 150-180 words using information from the four texts in Task 2. You must include three content points specified in the prompt and use your own words (copying from texts results in penalties).

Task Requirements:

  • Word count: 150-180 words (strictly enforced)
  • Source material: Use information from all four Task 2 texts
  • Content points: Address all three bullet points in the prompt
  • Own words: Paraphrase and synthesise - don't copy phrases
  • Genre: Article, essay, formal/informal email, review, or report

Sample Task 3 Prompt:

Task 3 - Reading into Writing

Write an article (150-180 words) for an environmental website about sustainable urban living. Use the information from texts A-D to:
¬ª explain the environmental challenges facing modern cities
¬ª evaluate the effectiveness of current recycling initiatives
¬ª suggest practical solutions for individuals to reduce their carbon footprint

Step-by-Step Approach:

1 Analyse the Prompt (2 minutes)

Identify the three bullet points. Note the genre (article) and register (formal/semi-formal for a website). Underline key task words: "explain," "evaluate," "suggest."

2 Review Task 2 Texts and Select Information (5 minutes)

Go back to your Task 2 texts. Find relevant information for each bullet point. Make notes in your own words:

  • Text A (Statistics): Cities produce 70% of global CO2 emissions, air pollution affects 9/10 urban residents
  • Text B (Recycling data): Participation rates vary by district (40-85%), contamination is a major problem
  • Text C (Individual actions): Transport choices and energy consumption are key factors
  • Text D (Expert recommendations): Systemic change requires both policy and personal commitment
3 Plan Your Structure (3 minutes)
  • Introduction (25-30 words): Hook + context + thesis statement
  • Body Paragraph 1 (40-45 words): Address bullet point 1 (challenges) with evidence from Text A
  • Body Paragraph 2 (40-45 words): Address bullet point 2 (recycling evaluation) using Text B
  • Body Paragraph 3 (30-35 words): Address bullet point 3 (solutions) with ideas from Texts C and D
  • Conclusion (15-20 words): Summary + forward-looking statement
4 Write and Monitor Word Count (25 minutes)

Write your response, keeping track of word count. Use linking words and B2 grammar structures.

5 Check and Revise (5 minutes)

Verify all bullet points are addressed, check word count, correct grammar/spelling errors.

Sample Answer (Task 3):

The Challenge of Sustainable Urban Living

As cities continue to expand worldwide, the environmental impact of urban living has become impossible to ignore. This article examines the challenges we face and practical steps towards sustainability.

Modern cities are responsible for approximately 70% of global carbon dioxide emissions, despite covering only 2% of the Earth's surface. Furthermore, recent studies indicate that air pollution affects nine out of ten urban residents, leading to significant health concerns. These statistics demonstrate the urgent need for comprehensive environmental policies in urban areas.

Current recycling initiatives show mixed results. While participation rates vary dramatically between districts - from 40% to 85% - contamination remains a significant obstacle. If residents were better educated about proper sorting procedures, these programmes would undoubtedly be more effective. Nevertheless, recycling alone cannot solve our environmental crisis.

Individuals can make meaningful contributions through transport choices and energy consumption. Walking, cycling, or using public transport instead of private vehicles significantly reduces personal carbon footprints. Additionally, experts emphasise that systemic change requires both policy reform and personal commitment from every citizen.

Creating sustainable cities demands collective action at every level of society.

Word count: 178 words

Critical Success Factors for Task 3:
  • ‚úì Synthesis: Combine information from multiple texts, don't just list facts from each
  • ‚úì Critical thinking: Evaluate, compare, and contrast information (especially for "evaluate" prompts)
  • ‚úì Paraphrasing: Use your own words - avoid copying phrases longer than 3 words
  • ‚úì All texts: Include information from ALL four texts, not just 2 or 3
  • ‚úì Bullet points: Address all three content points explicitly
  • ‚úì Linking devices: Use sophisticated connectors (furthermore, however, consequently, in contrast)
Task 4: Extended Writing (40 minutes)
Task Overview: Write 150-180 words using your own ideas and knowledge (no reading texts provided). You must include two content points specified in the prompt. This task tests your ability to produce independent, well-structured writing.

Task Requirements:

  • Word count: 150-180 words (10% tolerance allowed)
  • Source material: Use your own ideas and knowledge only
  • Content points: Address both bullet points in the prompt
  • Genre: Descriptive/discursive/argumentative essay, article, email, review, or report

Sample Task 4 Prompt:

Task 4 - Extended Writing

Write an article (150-180 words) for your college magazine with the title "Someone I admire".
Choose a public figure from the past or present, giving reasons for your choice.

You should: ¬ª describe the person's achievements ¬ª explain why you find them inspiring

Step-by-Step Approach:

1 Analyse the Prompt (2 minutes)

Identify the genre (article), register (semi-formal for college magazine), and two bullet points. Note the title requirement.

2 Brainstorm Ideas (3 minutes)

Choose a public figure you know well. List their achievements and reasons for admiration. Select the strongest points that you can explain clearly.

3 Plan Your Structure (3 minutes)
  • Introduction (25-30 words): Hook + introduce the person
  • Body Paragraph 1 (50-60 words): Describe achievements (bullet point 1)
  • Body Paragraph 2 (50-60 words): Explain inspiration (bullet point 2)
  • Conclusion (20-30 words): Summary + final thought
4 Write (25 minutes)

Write your response, using B2 language and maintaining consistent register.

5 Check and Revise (7 minutes)

Verify bullet points, check word count, correct errors, ensure title is included.

Sample Answer (Task 4):

Someone I Admire: Malala Yousafzai

In a world full of celebrities and sports stars, the person I truly admire is Malala Yousafzai, the Pakistani education activist. Her courage and determination to fight for girls' education, even in the face of extreme danger, make her an extraordinary role model for young people everywhere.

Malala's achievements are remarkable. Despite being shot by the Taliban at the age of fifteen for speaking out about girls' right to education, she survived and became even more determined. She founded the Malala Fund, which has helped thousands of girls access education worldwide. Furthermore, she became the youngest-ever Nobel Peace Prize laureate in 2014, using her platform to advocate for the 130 million girls globally who are denied schooling.

What I find most inspiring about Malala is her unwavering commitment to peaceful activism. She could have chosen anger and revenge, but instead she chose education and dialogue. Her famous quote, "One child, one teacher, one book and one pen can change the world," reminds us that individual action can create global impact.

Malala proves that age is no barrier to making a difference.

Word count: 176 words

Critical Success Factors for Task 4:
  • ‚úì Original ideas: Use your own knowledge and opinions
  • ‚úì Both bullet points: Ensure both are explicitly addressed
  • ‚úì Consistent register: Match the genre and audience throughout
  • ‚úì B2 language: Use complex sentences, conditionals, passives, modal verbs
  • ‚úì Cohesion: Link ideas logically with appropriate connectors
  • ‚úì Evidence: Support opinions with examples and reasons
Genre-Specific Guidance

1. Essays (Descriptive, Discursive, Argumentative)

Feature Descriptive Discursive Argumentative
Purpose Create vivid picture Explore multiple views Persuade to your view
Structure Spatial/chronological Point-counterpoint Thesis-evidence-conclusion
Language Sensory details, imagery Balanced, objective Strong, persuasive
Example opening "The ancient city stands majestically..." "There are valid arguments on both sides..." "It is undeniable that..."

2. Articles

Key Features:
  • Title: Engaging and relevant (can be provided or you create)
  • Opening: Hook the reader with something interesting or surprising
  • Body: Mix of facts, opinions, examples, and personal experience
  • Closing: Memorable final thought or call to action
  • Register: Semi-formal, accessible but intelligent

3. Formal Emails/Letters

Structure:
  • Opening: Dear Sir/Madam, Dear Editor, Dear Manager,
  • Introduction: State purpose clearly and immediately
  • Body: Develop points logically with supporting evidence
  • Conclusion: State desired outcome or next steps
  • Closing: Yours faithfully (if Dear Sir/Madam), Yours sincerely (if named)

4. Reviews

Structure:
  • Introduction: What you're reviewing, basic information
  • Description: What it's about/plot/what happens
  • Evaluation: Strengths and weaknesses with specific examples
  • Recommendation: Who would enjoy it and why (or why not)

5. Reports

Structure:
  • Title: Clear and descriptive
  • Introduction: Purpose and scope of the report
  • Findings: Present facts objectively (use bullet points if appropriate)
  • Conclusions: Summarise main findings
  • Recommendations: Specific, actionable suggestions
B2 Language for Writing

Essential Grammar Structures:

Structure Example Use
Second Conditional "If people recycled more, the environment would improve." Hypothetical situations
Third Conditional "If the government had acted sooner, the crisis could have been avoided." Past hypotheticals
Passive Voice "It has been suggested that..." / "The report was written..." Formal, objective tone
Relative Clauses "The city, which has a population of 10 million, faces serious challenges." Add information concisely
Modal Verbs (speculation) "This might indicate..." / "It could be argued that..." Tentative conclusions
Participle Clauses "Having considered all options, we recommend..." Sophisticated linking

Academic Vocabulary:

Discourse Markers: Furthermore, Moreover, However, Nevertheless, Consequently, In contrast, On the other hand, Similarly

Reporting Verbs: claim, argue, suggest, acknowledge, emphasise, deny, admit

Evaluation Language: significant, notable, problematic, effective, beneficial, detrimental, substantial, considerable
Assessment Criteria

Both writing tasks are assessed using four criteria. Understanding these helps you focus on what examiners value most.

Organisation Text organisation, paragraphing, idea development, format, signposting Effective organisation, clear presentation, logical development, appropriate format
Language Control Grammar range/accuracy, vocabulary range/accuracy, errors, spelling/punctuation Wide range, good accuracy, errors don't impede, excellent spelling/punctuation

Task 4 (Extended Writing) Assessment:

Criterion What Examiners Look For Score 4 (Excellent)
Task Fulfilment Communicative aim, writer-reader relationship, topic coverage Excellent achievement, excellent awareness of reader, all requirements met
Organisation Text organisation, idea development, format, signposting Effective organisation, very clear presentation, effective signposting
Language Control Grammar range/accuracy, vocabulary range/accuracy, errors, spelling/punctuation Wide range, good accuracy, errors don't impede, excellent spelling/punctuation
Common Mistakes to Avoid:
  • Copying from texts (Task 3): Heavy lifting from source texts results in low scores for Reading for Writing
  • Missing bullet points: Each missed point significantly reduces Task Fulfilment score
  • Inconsistent register: Mixing formal and informal language
  • Poor paragraphing: No clear structure or very long/short paragraphs
  • Basic linking: Overusing "and," "but," "so" instead of sophisticated connectors
  • Spelling errors: At B2, spelling should be largely accurate
  • Word count issues: Too short (under 150) or too long (over 180 significantly)

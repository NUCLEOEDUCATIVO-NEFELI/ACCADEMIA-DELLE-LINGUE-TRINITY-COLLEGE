ISE II Interview Overview (B2)

The ISE II Speaking & Listening Interview is a 20-minute one-to-one assessment with a Trinity examiner. It consists of 4 tasks designed to test your ability to communicate effectively in English at B2 level.

Key Differences from ISE I (B1):
  • Longer duration: 20 minutes vs. 18 minutes
  • Collaborative Task: New task requiring you to lead the interaction (not present at B1)
  • Topic preparation: Mind map instead of Topic Form
  • Listening complexity: 8 facts to report + 4 inference questions (vs. 6 facts at B1)
  • Abstract topics: Society, values, environmental concerns, public figures
  • More language functions: Speculating, giving advice, establishing common ground
Important: This is a student-centred exam. You are expected to take an active role in all speaking tasks, asking questions and maintaining interaction. Do not wait for the examiner to lead.
Assessment Criteria

All speaking tasks are assessed using these four criteria:

Criterion What Examiners Look For Weight
Communicative Effectiveness Task fulfilment, initiating and maintaining interaction, appropriate turn-taking, elaborating responses 30%
Interactive Listening Understanding examiner questions, responding appropriately, asking for clarification when needed 20%
Language Control Range and accuracy of grammar, vocabulary precision, B2 structures (conditionals, passives, modals) 30%
Delivery Pronunciation, intonation, fluency, appropriate speed, clear articulation 20%

Topic Task (4 minutes)
Task Overview: You prepare a topic before the exam and may bring notes or a mind map (no topic form required at B2). You can also bring a small object or picture to support the discussion. The examiner discusses your topic with you, and you must take control of the conversation, ask questions, and demonstrate B2 language functions.

Preparing Your Topic:

1 Choose Wisely

Select something you know well and can discuss in depth. Avoid the 10 subject areas listed for the Conversation task. Good topics include: hobbies, sports, a book you've read, a place you've visited, your studies, career plans, a personal project.

2 Create a Mind Map

Prepare a mind map with 4-5 branches covering different aspects of your topic. This helps you organise your thoughts and ensures you can discuss various angles.

3 Prepare Questions for the Examiner

You MUST ask the examiner questions during this task. Prepare 3-4 questions in advance about their opinion, experience, or knowledge related to your topic.

Sample Mind Map: "Learning Photography"

Central Topic: My Journey with Photography

Branch 1 - Origins: How I got interested (3 years ago) ‚Ä¢ First camera (birthday gift) ‚Ä¢ Early challenges

Branch 2 - Development: Types I enjoy (landscape, street) ‚Ä¢ Technical skills learned ‚Ä¢ Influential photographers I admire

Branch 3 - Recent Experience: Difficult project last month ‚Ä¢ Wedding photography ‚Ä¢ Unexpected problems

Branch 4 - Future: Exhibitions I'm planning ‚Ä¢ Professional aspirations ‚Ä¢ Question for examiner: "Have you ever tried photography?"

Function Example Phrases

Practical Example: Topic Task Dialogue

Examiner: "Good morning. I see you've brought a mind map. What would you like to talk about today?"
Candidate: "I'd like to discuss my experience learning photography over the past three years. Shall we start with how I first became interested?"
Examiner: "Yes, tell me about that."
Candidate: "It started when my grandfather gave me his old film camera for my birthday. At first, I found it incredibly frustrating because I couldn't see results immediately. However, that delay actually taught me patience and composition. I'm convinced that starting with film rather than digital made me a better photographer. Have you ever used a film camera?"
Examiner: "Yes, when I was younger. Do you prefer digital now?"
Candidate: "I use both, actually. Digital is more convenient for professional work, but film has a certain quality that's hard to replicate. Moving on to my recent work, I photographed a wedding last month which was quite challenging. The lighting conditions were terrible, but I managed to get some good shots. What would you have done in that situation?"
Success Strategies for Topic Task:
  • ‚úì Start with a brief introduction, then immediately ask the examiner a question
  • ‚úì Speak in sustained turns (30-40 seconds) with examples and details
  • ‚úì Use your mind map flexibly - don't read from it
  • ‚úì Ask at least 2-3 questions throughout the 4 minutes
  • ‚úì Show enthusiasm and genuine interest in the examiner's responses
  • ‚úì Use a range of tenses: past experiences, current habits, future plans
Collaborative Task (4 minutes)
Task Overview: The examiner reads you a prompt describing a situation, problem, or dilemma. You must initiate, lead, and maintain the interaction by asking questions and making suggestions. This is the most interactive task and requires you to take control immediately.

Task Structure:

1 Listen to the Prompt

The examiner reads a scenario (e.g., planning an event, solving a work problem, making a difficult decision). Listen carefully - you won't see it written down.

2 Take Immediate Control

Do NOT wait for the examiner to ask you questions. Start immediately with: "Shall we start by discussing...?" or "I'd like to find out more about..."

3 Ask Questions to Gather Information

Ask 6-8 questions to understand the situation fully. Start with basic details (who, what, when, where) before moving to opinions and advice.

4 Offer Advice and Reach Conclusions

Only give your recommendations after gathering sufficient information. Build towards a solution together with the examiner.

Sample Prompts:

Prompt 1 (Problem): "I'm doing quite well at work, but I'd like to do even better and I'm not sure how."
Prompt 2 (Dilemma): "I've been invited to go on holiday with my best friend's family to Barbados. It would be an amazing experience but I'd miss an important business meeting."
Prompt 3 (Opinion): "I think work experience counts for more than qualifications, so when I'm hiring staff, I don't even look at the qualifications section of their CV."

Practical Example: Collaborative Task Dialogue

Examiner: [Reads prompt] "I'm doing quite well at work, but I'd like to do even better and I'm not sure how."
Candidate: "Shall we start by discussing what area you'd most like to improve? Is it your technical skills, leadership abilities, or something else?"
Examiner: "I think leadership is my weakness. I'm good at my job technically but I find it hard to manage people."
Candidate: "I see. What specific challenges do you face when managing your team? Do you find it difficult to delegate tasks or give feedback?"
Examiner: "Both, actually. I tend to do things myself because it's faster, and I avoid difficult conversations."
Candidate: "That sounds familiar. Have you considered taking a management course? Many companies offer leadership training that might help you develop those skills. What does your company provide?"
Examiner: "They do have some training, but it's quite basic."
Candidate: "In that case, you might want to look for external courses. The downside is the cost, but the investment could pay off in promotions. Would you be willing to fund it yourself?"

Function Phrases
Opening "Shall we start by...?" / "Where shall we begin?" / "I'd suggest we first consider..."
Making Suggestions "How about...?" / "Why don't we...?" / "Perhaps we could..." / "One option would be..."
Asking for Opinions "What do you think about...?" / "Do you think...would work?" / "What's your view on...?"

Common Mistakes to Avoid:
  • ‚úó Waiting for the examiner to ask you questions (you must lead)
  • ‚úó Giving long speeches without involving the examiner
  • ‚úó Not asking enough questions (aim for 6-8 questions in 4 minutes)
  • ‚úó Solving the problem too quickly (build up to solutions gradually)
  • ‚úó Agreeing to everything without adding your own ideas
  • ‚úó Not reaching any conclusions or decisions
Conversation Task (2 minutes)
Task Overview: The examiner chooses one subject area from the 10 listed areas and initiates conversation. You must respond with opinions, ask questions, and maintain interaction. This is shorter than other tasks but requires the same level of engagement.

Key Requirements:

  • Respond to questions with extended answers and reasons
  • Demonstrate knowledge of the subject area
  • Ask the examiner at least one question about the subject area
  • Use appropriate B2 language functions
  • Maintain natural turn-taking

Practical Example: Conversation Task Dialogue

Examiner: "Let's talk about The World of Work. How do you think remote working has changed the modern workplace?"
Candidate: "I believe it's fundamentally transformed our work culture. On one hand, employees enjoy greater flexibility and save commuting time, which improves work-life balance. However, I suspect it might reduce team cohesion in the long run. What has your experience been with remote working?"
Examiner: "I find it convenient but sometimes miss office interaction. Do you think companies should mandate return-to-office policies?"
Candidate: "That's a complex issue. While some collaboration requires face-to-face contact, forcing everyone back seems counterproductive. A hybrid model might be the most sensible compromise, wouldn't you agree? In my opinion, two or three days in the office maintains connection while preserving flexibility."

Language for Conversation Task:

Function Example Language
Extending Answers "That's an interesting question. I think..." / "There are several aspects to consider..."
Giving Examples "For instance,..." / "To give you an example,..." / "A case in point would be..."
Contrasting Views "On the one hand... on the other hand..." / "While some people argue..., I believe..."
Asking Questions "What's your perspective on...?" / "Do you think...?" / "I'd be interested to hear your view..."
Subject Area Preparation Tips:
  • Prepare vocabulary for each of the 10 subject areas
  • Think of 2-3 opinions you could express about each area
  • Consider current events related to these topics
  • Practice explaining your views with reasons and examples
Independent Listening Task (8 minutes)
Task Overview: Listen twice to a complex recording (approximately 3 minutes 30 seconds). First, report the gist (main idea). Second, report 8 facts from the recording. Finally, answer 4 inference questions from the examiner.

Task Structure:

1 First Listening - Gist (2-3 minutes)

Listen for overall understanding. The examiner will ask: "What is the speaker talking about?" or "What is the main topic?"

Your response: A comprehensive summary in 2-3 sentences showing understanding of the main argument and perspective.
Example: "The speaker is discussing the impact of artificial intelligence on employment, arguing that while AI will eliminate some jobs, it will also create new opportunities in different sectors."
2 Second Listening - Facts (3-4 minutes)

Listen for specific facts. Take detailed notes. After listening, report 8 facts to the examiner. These should be specific and accurate.

Example facts:
"The speaker stated that 40% of current jobs could be automated within 15 years."
"He mentioned that retraining programs would be essential for displaced workers."
"According to the speaker, AI is already being used in healthcare for diagnosis."
3 Inference Questions (2-3 minutes)

The examiner asks 4 questions requiring you to read between the lines and interpret the speaker's attitude or implications.

Example questions:
"What is the speaker's attitude toward government intervention?"
"What conclusion can we draw about the speaker's view on education?"
"Why do you think the speaker mentioned...?"

Assessment Criteria:

Score Description
4 Identifies gist accurately, reports 7-8 facts correctly, answers inference questions promptly and accurately
3 Identifies gist, reports 5-6 facts, answers most inference questions with minor hesitation
2 Identifies partial gist, reports 3-4 facts, answers some questions with difficulty
1 Identifies minimal information, reports 1-2 facts, struggles with inference questions
0 No performance to assess or task not attempted

Note-Taking Strategy:

Effective Note-Taking:
  • Use abbreviations and symbols (&, +, ‚Üí, ‚Üë, ‚Üì)
  • Write keywords only - don't try to write full sentences
  • Organise notes spatially (left side for main ideas, right for details)
  • Number your facts 1-8 as you hear them
  • Use arrows to show relationships (cause ‚Üí effect)
  • Notes are not assessed - only your oral responses count
B2 Listening Strategies:
  • Listen for signposting language: "First," "However," "In conclusion"
  • Pay attention to stress and intonation - they indicate importance
  • Inference clues come from tone of voice, word choice, and emphasis
  • Prepare for abstract topics: economics, technology, environment, education
  • Don't panic if you miss a fact - focus on the next one

Interview Preparation Checklist

Use this checklist to prepare for your ISE II interview:

Before the Exam:

  • I have chosen a Topic that is NOT from the 10 subject areas
  • I have prepared a mind map with 4-5 branches for my Topic
  • I have prepared 3-4 questions to ask the examiner about my Topic
  • I have practised all 8 B2 language functions
  • I have practised 10+ Collaborative Task prompts with a partner
  • I can ask 6-8 questions in 4 minutes for the Collaborative Task
  • I have prepared vocabulary for all 10 subject areas
  • I have practised listening to recordings and reporting 8 facts
  • I have practised answering inference questions

During the Exam:

  • I will speak clearly and at a natural pace
  • I will ask the examiner questions in every speaking task
  • I will take the lead in the Collaborative Task immediately
  • I will use my mind map flexibly (not read from it)
  • I will show enthusiasm and interest in the examiner's responses
  • I will use a range of B2 grammar and vocabulary
  • I will take notes during the Listening Task
  • I will report the gist before the facts in the Listening Task

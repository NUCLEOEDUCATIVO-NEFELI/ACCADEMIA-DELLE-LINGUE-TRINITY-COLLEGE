ISE II Complete Task Breakdown

Reading, Writing, Speaking & Listening at B2 Level

ISE II Exam Overview (B2)

ISE II is a four-skills exam at CEFR B2 level (Independent User - Upper Intermediate). The exam demonstrates you can understand the main ideas of complex text on both concrete and abstract topics, interact with a degree of fluency and spontaneity, and produce clear, detailed text on a wide range of subjects.

Key Differences from ISE I (B1):
  • Longer texts: 500-900 words vs. 400-700 words
  • More complex writing: 150-180 words vs. 100-130 words
  • Abstract topics: Society, values, environmental concerns, public figures
  • Collaborative Task: New interactive task requiring initiative and leadership
  • Extended speaking time: 20 minutes vs. 18 minutes
  • Single Independent Listening: One complex task with gist, facts, and inferences

Task 1: Long Reading (20 minutes)
Task Overview: Read one complex text of approximately 500 words (divided into 5 paragraphs) and answer 15 questions testing different reading skills.

Question Types Breakdown:

1 Questions 1-5: Title Matching

Match the correct title to each of the 5 paragraphs. You have 6 titles to choose from (one is a distractor). Tests understanding of main ideas.

Example: Paragraph discusses economic inequality. Correct title: "The Wealth Gap Challenge"
2 Questions 6-10: True Statements Selection

Choose 5 true statements from a list of 8. Three statements are false according to the text. Tests detailed comprehension.

Example: "Remote work has increased productivity in most sectors" (True/False based on text evidence)
3 Questions 11-15: Summary Gap-Fill

Complete a summary with exact words from the text (maximum 4 words per gap). Tests precise understanding.

Example: "The author suggests that _______ policies are necessary to address climate change."
Answer from text: "comprehensive environmental"
B2 Reading Strategies:
  • Identify tone and attitude: Is the author critical, supportive, or neutral?
  • Recognize implicit meaning: What is suggested but not directly stated?
  • Track argument development: How does the writer build their case?
  • Watch for hedging language: "may," "could," "potentially" indicate uncertainty
  • Understand discourse markers: "however," "furthermore," "consequently"
Task 2: Multi-text Reading (20 minutes)
Task Overview: Read 4 short texts (approximately 900 words total) on the same theme. One text is an infographic (diagram, table, or chart with text). Answer 15 questions.

Question Types Breakdown:

1 Questions 16-20: Text Matching

Match descriptions to the correct text (A, B, C, or D). Tests understanding of purpose, tone, and main argument.

Example: "Which text presents statistical evidence?" ‚Üí Match to correct letter
2 Questions 21-25: True Statements Selection

Choose 5 true statements from 8 options based on information across all four texts. Requires synthesizing information.

3 Questions 26-30: Summary Notes Completion

Complete summary notes using exact words from the texts (maximum 4 words per gap). The notes synthesize key information from all four texts.

Important: The information in these texts is used for Task 3 (Reading into Writing). Pay attention to statistics, expert opinions, case studies, and contrasting viewpoints.
Task 3: Reading into Writing (40 minutes)
Task Requirements:
  • Write 150-180 words 150-180 words
  • Use information from all four texts in Task 2
  • Include three content points specified in the prompt
  • Use your own words - copying from texts results in penalties
  • Genre: Article, essay, formal/informal email, review, or report

Sample Task Structure:

Example Prompt:
"Write an article (150-180 words) for an environmental website about sustainable urban living. Use the information from texts A-D to:
¬ª explain the environmental challenges facing modern cities
¬ª evaluate the effectiveness of current recycling initiatives
¬ª suggest practical solutions for individuals to reduce their carbon footprint"
1 Analyze the Prompt

Identify the three bullet points. Note the genre and register. Recognize the evaluative nature of B2 tasks ("evaluate," "suggest," "explain").

2 Synthesize and Adapt Information

Find relevant information across all texts. Make notes in your own words, combining information where appropriate:

  • Text A: Statistics on urban pollution ‚Üí "cities produce 70% of global CO2 emissions"
  • Text B: Recycling program results ‚Üí "participation rates vary significantly by district"
  • Text C: Individual actions ‚Üí "transport choices and energy consumption are key factors"
  • Text D: Expert recommendations ‚Üí "systemic change requires both policy and personal commitment"
3 Plan Your Structure
  • Introduction (25-30 words): Hook + context + thesis statement
  • Body Paragraph 1 (40-45 words): Point 1 with synthesized evidence
  • Body Paragraph 2 (40-45 words): Point 2 with evaluation
  • Body Paragraph 3 (30-35 words): Point 3 with practical suggestions
  • Conclusion (15-20 words): Summary + forward-looking statement
Critical Success Factors:
  • ‚úì Demonstrate synthesis: Combine information from multiple texts, don't just list facts
  • ‚úì Show critical thinking: Evaluate, compare, and contrast information
  • ‚úì Use sophisticated linking devices: "Consequently," "In contrast," "Furthermore"
  • ‚úì Maintain consistent register throughout (formal for essays/articles)
  • ‚úì Include complex sentences with subordinate clauses
Task 4: Extended Writing (40 minutes)
Task Requirements:
  • Write 150-180 words 150-180 words
  • No reading texts provided - use your own ideas and knowledge
  • Include two content points specified in the prompt
  • Genre: Discursive essay, article, formal/informal email/letter, review, or report

B2 Genre Requirements:

1. Discursive Essay

Balanced argumentation with clear thesis. Consideration of multiple perspectives. Evidence-based reasoning.

Example: "Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of remote working for society."

2. Article (Magazine/Online)

Engaging headline, subheadings, mix of formal and accessible language, rhetorical questions, direct address.

Example: "Write an article about whether social media has improved or damaged communication."

3. Formal Email/Letter

Professional tone, clear purpose, formal conventions (Yours sincerely/faithfully), persuasive or informative purpose.

Example: "Write a letter to your local council proposing improvements to public transport."

4. Review

Balanced evaluation, specific examples, recommendation, awareness of target audience.

Example: "Review a recent documentary about climate change, evaluating its effectiveness."

5. Report

Objective tone, headings and subheadings, factual information, conclusions and recommendations.

Example: "Write a report on the state of education in your country."

B2 Language Requirements:
  • Complex sentences: Relative clauses, participle clauses, conditionals (2nd, 3rd, mixed)
  • Passive voice: "It has been suggested that..."
  • Modal verbs of speculation: "must have," "might have," "could be"
  • Academic vocabulary: "significant," "consequently," "furthermore," "nevertheless"
  • Nominalization: "The implementation of the policy resulted in..."
Topic Discussion Task (4 minutes)
Task Overview: You prepare a topic before the exam and may bring notes or a mind map (no topic form required at B2). The examiner discusses your topic with you. You must maintain interaction and ask the examiner questions.

Preparing Your Topic:

  • Choose something you know well and can discuss in depth
  • Ensure it allows demonstration of B2 language functions
  • Do NOT choose from the 10 subject areas (these are for the Conversation task)
  • Prepare a mind map with 4-5 discussion branches
  • Bring a small object or photo to support discussion (optional)
Sample Mind Map: "My Experience Learning Photography"

Branch 1: How I became interested + first camera
Branch 2: Types of photography + technical challenges
Branch 3: A difficult project I completed recently
Branch 4: Future plans + asking examiner about their experience

B2 Language Functions to Demonstrate:

Function Example Language
Initiating and maintaining "Shall we start by discussing...?" / "Moving on to..." / "That leads me to..."
Expressing and expanding opinions "I'm convinced that..." / "What I find particularly interesting is..." / "My view is that..."
Highlighting advantages/disadvantages "One major benefit is..." / "The downside is..." / "On the plus side..."
Speculating "If I were in that situation..." / "It might be that..." / "Presumably..."
Giving advice "If I were you, I'd..." / "You might want to consider..." / "My recommendation would be..."
Expressing agreement/disagreement "I see your point, but..." / "That's exactly what I think" / "I'm not sure I agree..."
Eliciting information "What's your take on...?" / "Have you ever experienced...?" / "I'd be interested to know..."
Establishing common ground "We seem to agree that..." / "That's something we have in common" / "Like you, I..."
Key Strategies:
  • Start with a brief introduction, then ask the examiner an opening question
  • Respond to examiner questions with elaboration and examples
  • Use your mind map flexibly - don't read from it
  • Ask at least 2-3 questions throughout the 4 minutes
  • Show enthusiasm and genuine interest in the examiner's responses
Collaborative Task (4 minutes)
Task Overview: The examiner reads you a prompt. You must initiate, lead, and maintain the interaction by asking questions and making suggestions. This tests your ability to collaborate and take control of a conversation.

Task Structure:

Phase 1 - Prompt: Examiner reads a scenario (e.g., planning an event, solving a problem, organizing something)

Phase 2 - Your Role: You must immediately start asking questions and making suggestions. Do NOT wait for the examiner to ask you questions.

Phase 3 - Development: Build on the examiner's responses, offer alternatives, reach conclusions.
Sample Prompt:
"We're organizing a charity event to raise money for a local environmental project. We need to decide on the type of event, the venue, how to promote it, and what activities to include."

Your opening: "That sounds like a great initiative. Shall we start by discussing what type of event would attract the most people? I'm thinking either a concert or a food festival - what are your thoughts?"

Useful Collaborative Language:

  • Opening: "Shall we start by...?" / "Where shall we begin?" / "I'd suggest we first consider..."
  • Making suggestions: "How about...?" / "Why don't we...?" / "Perhaps we could..."
  • Asking for opinions: "What do you think about...?" / "Do you think...would work?" / "What's your view on...?"
  • Responding: "That's a good point" / "I hadn't thought of that" / "I'm not sure that would work because..."
  • Negotiating: "We could compromise by..." / "What if we combined both ideas?" / "Let's settle on..."
  • Closing: "So we've agreed on..." / "To summarize our decisions..." / "Shall we move on to...?"
Common Mistakes to Avoid:
  • Waiting for the examiner to ask you questions (you must lead)
  • Giving long speeches without involving the examiner
  • Not asking enough questions (aim for 6-8 questions in 4 minutes)
  • Agreeing to everything without adding your own ideas
  • Not reaching any conclusions or decisions
Conversation Task (2 minutes)
Task Overview: The examiner chooses one subject area from the 10 listed areas and initiates conversation. You must respond with opinions, ask questions, and maintain interaction.

Key Requirements:

  • Respond to questions with extended answers and reasons
  • Demonstrate knowledge of the subject area
  • Ask the examiner at least one question about the subject area
  • Use appropriate B2 language functions
Sample Conversation about "The World of Work":

Examiner: "How do you think working from home has changed the modern workplace?"
Candidate: "I believe it's fundamentally transformed our work culture. On one hand, employees enjoy greater flexibility and save commuting time. However, I suspect it might reduce team cohesion in the long run. What has your experience been with remote working?"
Examiner: "I find it convenient but sometimes miss office interaction. Do you think companies should mandate return-to-office policies?"
Candidate: "That's a complex issue. While some collaboration requires face-to-face contact, forcing everyone back seems counterproductive. A hybrid model might be the most sensible compromise, wouldn't you agree?"
Speaking Assessment Criteria
Criterion B2 Requirements
Communicative Effectiveness Initiates and maintains interaction, uses appropriate turn-taking, elaborates spontaneously, handles repair strategies smoothly
Interactive Listening Understands without repetition, responds promptly and relevantly, asks for clarification appropriately
Language Control Uses range of B2 structures (conditionals, passives, modals, relative clauses), precise vocabulary, minor errors don't impede communication
Delivery Clear pronunciation, natural intonation, fluent speech with minimal hesitation, appropriate pace
Independent Listening Task (10 minutes)
Task Overview: Listen twice to a complex recording (approximately 3 minutes 30 seconds). First, report the gist (main idea). Second, report 8 facts from the recording. Finally, answer 4 inference questions from the examiner.

Task Structure:

Phase 1 - First Listening:
Listen for overall understanding. The examiner will ask: "What is the speaker talking about?" or "What is the main topic?"

Your response: A comprehensive summary in 2-3 sentences showing understanding of the main argument and perspective.
Example: "The speaker is discussing the impact of artificial intelligence on employment, arguing that while AI will eliminate some jobs, it will also create new opportunities in different sectors."
Phase 2 - Second Listening:
Listen for specific facts. Take detailed notes.

After listening: Report 8 facts to the examiner. These should be specific and accurate.
Example: "The speaker stated that 40% of current jobs could be automated within 15 years." / "He mentioned that retraining programs would be essential for displaced workers."
Phase 3 - Inference Questions:
The examiner asks 4 questions requiring you to read between the lines.

Example: "What is the speaker's attitude toward government intervention?" / "What conclusion can we draw about the speaker's view on education?"

Assessment Criteria:

Score Description
4 Identifies gist accurately, reports 7-8 facts correctly, answers inference questions promptly and accurately
3 Identifies gist, reports 5-6 facts, answers most inference questions with minor hesitation
2 Identifies partial gist, reports 3-4 facts, answers some questions with difficulty
1 Identifies minimal information, reports 1-2 facts, struggles with inference questions
0 No performance to assess or task not attempted
B2 Listening Strategies:
  • Gist listening: Focus on the speaker's main argument, not details
  • Note-taking system: Use abbreviations, symbols, and layout to organize information
  • Listen for signposting: "First," "However," "In conclusion" indicate structure
  • Inference clues: Tone of voice, word choice, and emphasis reveal attitude
  • Prepare for abstract topics: Economics, technology, environment, education

ISE I Exam Overview (B1)

ISE I is a four-skills exam at CEFR B1 level (Independent User). The exam demonstrates you can understand the main points of clear standard English on familiar matters, deal with most situations while travelling, and produce simple connected text on familiar topics.

Key Differences from ISE Foundation (A2):
  • Longer texts: 400-700 words vs. 300 words
  • More complex writing: 100-130 words vs. 70-100 words
  • Abstract topics: Can discuss opinions, predictions, and hypothetical situations
  • Extended speaking time: 18 minutes vs. 13 minutes
  • Two Independent Listening tasks: Both require detailed information extraction

Task 1: Long Reading (20 minutes)
Task Overview: Read one factual/descriptive text of approximately 400 words (divided into 5 paragraphs) and answer 15 questions testing different reading skills.

Question Types Breakdown:

1 Questions 1-5: Title Matching

Match the correct title to each of the 5 paragraphs. You have 6 titles to choose from (one is a distractor).

Example: Paragraph 1 discusses how tour guides need patience. Correct title: "The Challenges of Guiding"

Skill tested: Reading for gist and main ideas

2 Questions 6-10: True Statements Selection

Choose 5 true statements from a list of 8. Three statements are false according to the text.

Example: "Tour guides often work long hours" (True/False based on text evidence)

Skill tested: Reading for specific factual information

3 Questions 11-15: Sentence Completion

Complete sentences with exact words from the text (maximum 3 words per gap).

Example: "Successful tour guides must be able to _______ difficult questions quickly."
Answer from text: "answer"

Skill tested: Reading for detail and precise information

Strategies for Success:
  • Skim first: Read quickly for general meaning before looking at questions
  • Analyze paragraph structure: Topic sentences often contain main ideas for title matching
  • Scan for keywords: Look for specific names, numbers, or phrases from questions
  • Check context: For sentence completion, read the whole sentence to ensure grammar fits
  • Watch for synonyms: The text may use different words with similar meanings
Task 2: Multi-text Reading (20 minutes)
Task Overview: Read 4 short texts (approximately 700 words total) on the same theme. One text is an infographic (diagram, table, or chart with text). Answer 15 questions.

Question Types Breakdown:

1 Questions 16-20: Text Matching

Match descriptions to the correct text (A, B, C, or D). Tests understanding of main idea or purpose of each text.

Example: "Which text gives advice on saving money?" ‚Üí Match to correct letter
2 Questions 21-25: True Statements Selection

Choose 5 true statements from 8 options based on information across all four texts.

3 Questions 26-30: Summary Notes Completion

Complete summary notes using exact words from the texts (maximum 3 words per gap). The notes summarize key information from all four texts.

Important: The information in these texts is used for Task 3 (Reading into Writing). Pay attention to facts, statistics, opinions, and examples you can use in your writing.
Task 3: Reading into Writing (40 minutes)
Task Requirements:
  • Write 100-130 words 100-130 words
  • Use information from all four texts in Task 2
  • Include three content points specified in the prompt
  • Use your own words - copying from texts results in penalties
  • Genre: Article, essay, formal/informal email, or review

Sample Task Structure:

Example Prompt:
"Write an article (100-130 words) for your school magazine about why more students should learn languages. Use the information from texts A-D to:
¬ª explain how language learning will help students in the future
¬ª suggest how learning a foreign language could help their performance at school
¬ª advise students on useful methods for learning foreign languages"
1 Analyze the Prompt

Identify the three bullet points you must address. Note the genre (article, essay, email) and register (formal/informal).

2 Select and Adapt Information

Go back to Task 2 texts. Find relevant information for each bullet point. Make notes in your own words:

  • Text A: Career benefits ‚Üí "better job opportunities abroad"
  • Text B: School performance ‚Üí "improves memory and concentration"
  • Text C: Learning methods ‚Üí "use apps and watch films with subtitles"
3 Plan Your Structure
  • Introduction (20-25 words): Hook the reader and introduce the topic
  • Body Paragraph 1 (30-35 words): Address bullet point 1 with evidence from texts
  • Body Paragraph 2 (30-35 words): Address bullet point 2 with evidence from texts
  • Conclusion (20-25 words): Address bullet point 3 + closing thought
Critical Success Factors:
  • ‚úì Paraphrase - don't copy phrases longer than 3 words from the source texts
  • ‚úì Include information from ALL four texts (not just 2 or 3)
  • ‚úì Address all three bullet points explicitly
  • ‚úì Use appropriate linking words (furthermore, however, in addition)
  • ‚úì Maintain consistent register throughout
Task 4: Extended Writing (40 minutes)
Task Requirements:
  • Write 100-130 words 100-130 words
  • No reading texts provided - use your own ideas and knowledge
  • Include two content points specified in the prompt
  • Genre: Descriptive essay, discursive essay, article, formal/informal email/letter, or review

Genre-Specific Requirements:

1. Descriptive Essay

Vivid descriptions using adjectives and sensory details. Clear spatial or chronological organization.

Example: "Describe a festival in your country and explain why it is important."

2. Discursive Essay

Balanced discussion of different viewpoints. Clear thesis statement and logical argumentation.

Example: "Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of online shopping."

3. Article (Magazine/Online)

Engaging title, informal but appropriate register, direct address to reader, personal opinion.

Example: "Write an article about healthy eating habits for young people."

4. Formal Email/Letter

Appropriate opening (Dear Sir/Madam) and closing (Yours faithfully/sincerely). Clear purpose stated early. Formal vocabulary.

Example: "Write a letter to a hotel manager complaining about service and requesting compensation."

5. Informal Email/Letter

Friendly opening (Hi/Hello + name), conversational tone, phrasal verbs acceptable, appropriate closing (Best wishes/Take care).

Example: "Write to a friend describing your new job and explaining your future plans."

6. Review

Description of item (film/book/restaurant), balanced evaluation, recommendation, specific examples.

Example: "Review a film you have seen recently, saying what you liked and disliked about it."

Assessment Criteria (All writing tasks):
  • Task fulfilment: Did you answer all parts? Is the word count correct? Is the genre appropriate?
  • Organisation: Clear paragraphs, logical flow, appropriate openings/closings
  • Language control: Grammar accuracy, vocabulary range, spelling and punctuation
Topic Discussion Task (4 minutes)
Task Overview: You prepare a topic before the exam and complete a Topic Form with 4 discussion points. The examiner asks questions about these points in random order. You must ask the examiner at least one question about your topic.

Preparing Your Topic:

  • Choose something you know well and can discuss in depth
  • Ensure it allows demonstration of B1 language functions
  • Do NOT choose from the 12 subject areas (these are for the Conversation task)
  • Prepare to discuss past, present, and future aspects
Sample Topic Form: "My Experience Learning Photography"

Point 1: How I became interested in photography and my first camera
Point 2: The types of photography I enjoy and my typical routine
Point 3: A challenging photo shoot I experienced recently
Point 4: My future plans - exhibitions or professional goals

B1 Language Functions to Demonstrate:

Function Example Language
Describing past actions (indefinite & recent) "I started photography three years ago..." / "I've recently begun using..."
Describing future & expressing intention "I'm planning to..." / "I intend to..." / "My goal is to..."
Predicting & expressing certainty "I think photography will become..." / "I'm sure that..." / "It might..."
Giving reasons & opinions "The reason I enjoy this is..." / "In my opinion..." / "I believe..."
Expressing obligation "I have to practice every day..." / "You need to understand light..."
Making comparisons "Digital cameras are more convenient than film..." / "It's easier to..."
Key Strategies:
  • Speak in sustained turns (don't give one-word answers)
  • Elaborate with examples and details
  • Refer back to previous points and forward to upcoming ones
  • Ask the examiner a genuine question about your topic
  • Use a range of tenses: past experiences, current habits, future plans
Conversation Task (4 minutes)
Task Overview: Two parts: (1) 2 minutes discussing your portfolio with examiner questions, and (2) 2 minutes on one subject area chosen by the examiner. You must ask at least one question about the portfolio and one about the subject area.

Part A: Portfolio Discussion (2 minutes)

The examiner asks about your written work (completed before the exam). Be prepared to:

  • Explain what you wrote and why
  • Discuss challenges you faced
  • Talk about what you learned
  • Ask the examiner a question about your portfolio (e.g., "What do you think about my conclusion?")

Part B: Subject Area Conversation (2 minutes)

The examiner chooses one of the 12 subject areas and initiates conversation. You must:

  • Respond to questions with opinions and reasons
  • Initiate and maintain the conversation
  • Ask the examiner at least one question about the subject area
Sample Conversation about "Travel":

Examiner: "What's the most interesting place you've visited?"
Candidate: "Last year I went to Japan, which was fascinating because the culture is so different from mine. I particularly enjoyed visiting traditional temples in Kyoto. The architecture was incredible - much more detailed than I expected. Have you ever been to Japan, examiner?"
Examiner: "Yes, I went there five years ago. Did you try the food?"
Candidate: "Definitely! I was surprised by how healthy it was. I think Japanese cuisine is much lighter than European food. In fact, I'm planning to learn how to make sushi because I enjoyed it so much..."
Mandatory Requirements:
  • Ask at least ONE question about your portfolio
  • Ask at least ONE question about the subject area
  • Initiate conversation (don't just respond)
  • Use repair strategies if you don't understand ("Sorry, do you mean...?")

Criterion What the Examiner Looks For
Communicative Effectiveness Task fulfilment, appropriate turn-taking, initiating and maintaining interaction, elaborating responses
Interactive Listening Understanding examiner questions, responding appropriately, asking for clarification when needed
Language Control Range and accuracy of grammar, vocabulary precision, B1 structures (conditionals, passives, modals)
Delivery Pronunciation, intonation, fluency, appropriate speed, clear articulation
Independent Listening Task 1 (4.5 minutes)
Task Overview: Listen twice to a recording (approximately 1 minute) of basic information (descriptive or narrative). Answer 6 questions with one or two words. You may take notes.

Task Structure:

  • Recording type: Descriptive or narrative (e.g., describing a process, telling a short story)
  • Length: Approximately 1 minute
  • Plays: Twice
  • Questions: 6 factual questions requiring specific information
  • Answers: 1-2 words only (phrases not accepted)
  • Scoring: 1 mark per correct answer (total 6 marks)
Sample Questions:
1. "What time did the event start?" ‚Üí "Seven o'clock" (2 words)
2. "How many people attended?" ‚Üí "Fifty" (1 word)
3. "What was the weather like?" ‚Üí "Very rainy" (2 words)
4. "Where did they go first?" ‚Üí "The museum" (2 words)
1 First Listening - Get the Gist

Listen for overall understanding. Don't worry about details. Identify the topic and general context.

2 Second Listening - Extract Details

Focus on specific information: numbers, times, dates, names, places, adjectives. Write key words quickly.

Key Strategies:
  • Listen for content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives, numbers) - ignore grammar words
  • Write abbreviations during listening (e.g., "7pm" instead of "seven o'clock")
  • Check spelling if you write full words
  • Answer all 6 questions - no penalty for wrong answers
  • Stick to 1-2 words - longer answers may not be accepted
Independent Listening Task 2 (5.5 minutes)
Task Overview: Listen twice to a factual exposition (approximately 1 minute 15 seconds). First, report the gist (main idea). Then report 6 facts from the recording. Finally, answer 4 follow-up questions from the examiner.

Task Structure:

Phase 1 - First Listening:
Listen for gist. The examiner will ask: "What is the speaker talking about?" or "What is the main topic?"

Your response: A general statement in 1-2 sentences (e.g., "The speaker is talking about the benefits of learning foreign languages and how it affects brain development.")
Phase 2 - Second Listening:
Listen for specific facts. Take notes on the provided paper.

After listening: Report 6 facts to the examiner. These can be simple statements.
Example: "The speaker said that learning languages improves memory." / "He mentioned that bilingual people are better at multitasking."
Phase 3 - Follow-up Questions:
The examiner asks 4 questions about the recording content.

Example: "According to the speaker, why do children learn languages faster than adults?"

Assessment Criteria (Task 2 only):

Score Description
4 Identifies main points and specific details accurately. Responds promptly and accurately to questions.
3 Identifies main points and most specific details. Responds to questions with minor hesitation or inaccuracy.
2 Identifies some main points and details. Requires repetition of questions or provides incomplete answers.
1 Identifies few points. Has difficulty responding to questions even when repeated.
0 No performance to assess or task not attempted.
Note-Taking Tips:
  • Use abbreviations and symbols (&, +, ‚Üí, ‚Üë)
  • Write keywords only - don't try to write full sentences
  • Organize notes spatially (left side for main ideas, right for details)
  • Number your facts 1-6 as you hear them
  • Notes are not assessed - only your oral responses count

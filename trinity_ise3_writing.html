ISE III Writing Exam Overview

The Writing component of ISE III (C1) consists of two tasks to be completed in 80 minutes (40 minutes per task). You must write 200-230 words for each task, demonstrating sophisticated language control, coherent organisation, and the ability to write for different purposes and audiences at C1 level.

Key Features at C1 Level:
  • Longer texts: 200-230 words (vs. 150-180 at B2)
  • Complex arguments: Nuanced discussion with multiple perspectives
  • Abstract topics: Ambitions, economic issues, rights of the individual
  • Sophisticated vocabulary: Academic collocations, abstract nouns
  • Advanced grammar: Inversion, subjunctive, mixed conditionals, nominalisation
  • Genre flexibility: Essays, reports, articles, reviews, formal correspondence
Important: You must write in pen (not pencil) in the answer booklet provided. Plan your time carefully - 40 minutes per task including planning, writing, and checking.

C1 Writing Assessment Criteria

Your writing is assessed against four criteria at C1 level:

1 Task Fulfilment

Addressing all content points, appropriate genre and register, target reader awareness, word count.

2 Organisation and Structure

Clear paragraphing, logical progression, cohesive devices, appropriate format.

3 Language Control

Range and accuracy of grammar, vocabulary precision, spelling and punctuation.

4 C1 Language Features

Complex sentence structures, nominalisation, sophisticated discourse markers, abstract vocabulary.

Task 3: Reading into Writing (40 minutes)
Task Overview: Write 200-230 words using information from the four texts in Task 2 (Multi-text Reading). You must include three content points specified in the prompt and use your own words (copying from texts results in penalties).

Task Requirements:

  • Write 200-230 words 200-230 words
  • Use information from all four texts in Task 2
  • Include three content points specified in the prompt (usually bullet points)
  • Use your own words - do not copy directly from the texts
  • Genre: Article, essay, formal/informal email, review, or report
Sample Task 3 Prompt:

"Write an article (200-230 words) for a political magazine about economic inequality in your country. Use the information from texts A-D to:
¬ª explain the causes of economic inequality
¬ª evaluate the effectiveness of current government policies
¬ª suggest practical solutions for reducing the wealth gap"

Step-by-Step Approach:

1 Analyse the Prompt (2 minutes)

Identify the three bullet points you must address. Note the genre (article) and register (formal/academic). Underline key requirements.

2 Review Task 2 Texts (5 minutes)

Go back to the four texts from Task 2. Identify relevant information for each bullet point. Make quick notes in your own words:

  • Text A: Causes ‚Üí "globalisation and technological change"
  • Text B: Current policies ‚Üí "progressive taxation with mixed results"
  • Text C: Solutions ‚Üí "investment in education and infrastructure"
  • Text D: Expert opinion ‚Üí "minimum wage increases and wealth taxes"
3 Plan Your Structure (3 minutes)
  • Introduction (30-35 words): Hook the reader and introduce the topic
  • Body Paragraph 1 (50-60 words): Address bullet point 1 with evidence from texts
  • Body Paragraph 2 (50-60 words): Address bullet point 2 with evaluation
  • Body Paragraph 3 (40-50 words): Address bullet point 3 + examples
  • Conclusion (30-35 words): Summary + forward-looking statement
4 Write (25 minutes)

Write your response using sophisticated C1 language. Synthesise information from multiple texts.

5 Check (5 minutes)

Verify word count, check all bullet points are addressed, correct grammar and spelling errors.

Critical Success Factors for Task 3:
  • ‚úì Synthesise - combine information from multiple texts
  • ‚úì Include information from ALL four texts
  • ‚úì Address all three bullet points explicitly
  • ‚úì Use your own words - do not copy phrases from the reading texts
  • ‚úì Use sophisticated linking words (furthermore, however, consequently)
  • ‚úì Maintain consistent formal register throughout
  • ‚úì Use C1 grammar structures (inversion, subjunctive, advanced conditionals)
Penalties Apply For:
  • Copying directly from the reading texts (plagiarism)
  • Not addressing all three bullet points
  • Not using information from all four texts
  • Writing significantly under or over the word count (200-230 words)
Task 4: Extended Writing (40 minutes)
Task Overview: Write 200-230 words using your own ideas and knowledge (no reading texts provided). You must include two content points specified in the prompt. This task tests your ability to produce sophisticated, well-organised writing independently.

Task Requirements:

  • Write 200-230 words 200-230 words
  • No reading texts provided - use your own ideas and knowledge
  • Include two content points specified in the prompt
  • Genre: Descriptive essay, discursive essay, argumentative essay, article, formal/informal email/letter, review, or report
Sample Task 4 Prompt:

"Write an essay (200-230 words) for your tutor discussing the impact of artificial intelligence on the workplace.
¬ª evaluate the benefits and drawbacks of AI in employment
¬ª suggest how workers can prepare for an AI-driven future"

Approach for Task 4:

1 Analyse the Prompt (3 minutes)

Identify the two bullet points. Determine the genre and register. Consider your target reader.

2 Brainstorm Ideas (5 minutes)

Generate ideas for both bullet points. Consider different perspectives and examples.

  • Bullet 1 - Benefits: efficiency, automation of dangerous jobs, new job creation
  • Bullet 1 - Drawbacks: unemployment, deskilling, inequality
  • Bullet 2 - Preparation: lifelong learning, soft skills development, adaptability
3 Plan Structure (2 minutes)
  • Introduction: Contextualise the topic and outline your approach
  • Body 1: Address bullet point 1 with balanced evaluation
  • Body 2: Address bullet point 2 with practical suggestions
  • Conclusion: Summarise main arguments and provide final thought
4 Write (25 minutes)

Produce your response with C1 language features.

5 Check (5 minutes)

Verify word count, bullet points, grammar, vocabulary, and spelling.

Key Differences from Task 3:
  • No source texts - all ideas must be your own
  • Two bullet points instead of three
  • May require more original analysis and evaluation
  • Greater freedom in argument development
  • Same word count and time allocation
Writing Genres at ISE III

At ISE III, you may be asked to write in various genres. Each has specific conventions, register requirements, and structural expectations.

1. Essay (Descriptive/Discursive/Argumentative)

Characteristics:
  • Formal, academic register
  • No contractions (don't ‚Üí do not)
  • No colloquialisms or idioms
  • Objective tone (avoid "I think" - use "It could be argued that")
  • Clear thesis statement
  • Balanced argumentation with evidence

Structure: Introduction ‚Üí Body (2-3 paragraphs) ‚Üí Conclusion

Example task: "To what extent has social media improved communication? Discuss both sides and give your opinion."

2. Article (Magazine/Online)

Characteristics:
  • Engaging title
  • Semi-formal to formal register (depending on publication)
  • Rhetorical questions allowed
  • Direct address to reader ("you") may be appropriate
  • Sophisticated vocabulary and varied sentence structures

Structure: Title ‚Üí Introduction (hook) ‚Üí Body ‚Üí Conclusion

Example task: "Write an article about the future of renewable energy."

3. Formal Email/Letter

Characteristics:
  • Highly formal register
  • Complex sentence structures
  • Formal conventions (Dear Sir/Madam, Yours faithfully)
  • Persuasive or informative purpose
  • Clear paragraphing with topic sentences

Structure: Address ‚Üí Date ‚Üí Salutation ‚Üí Opening ‚Üí Body ‚Üí Closing ‚Üí Sign-off

Example task: "Write a letter to a government minister proposing policy changes."

4. Report

Characteristics:
  • Objective, analytical tone
  • Headings and subheadings (optional but helpful)
  • Evidence-based analysis
  • Recommendations section
  • Impersonal style (passive voice acceptable)

Structure: Title ‚Üí Introduction ‚Üí Findings/Analysis ‚Üí Recommendations ‚Üí Conclusion

Example task: "Write a report on the impact of remote working on productivity."

5. Review

Characteristics:
  • Balanced evaluation (positive and negative aspects)
  • Cultural context
  • Nuanced opinion with justification
  • Recommendation
  • Engaging but sophisticated style

Structure: Introduction (what reviewed) ‚Üí Summary ‚Üí Evaluation ‚Üí Conclusion/Recommendation

Example task: "Review a recent documentary about climate change, evaluating its impact."

Feature Formal (Essay/Report/Formal Letter) Semi-Formal (Article/Review)

Phrasal verbs Avoid (use Latin-based alternatives) Limited use acceptable

C1 Language Features

At ISE III (C1), you must demonstrate sophisticated language control. Here are the key features examiners look for:

1. Complex Sentence Structures

Structure Example Usage
Inversion "Never before has technology transformed society so rapidly." Emphasis, formal writing
Cleft sentences "What concerns policymakers most is the long-term impact." Emphasis
Participle clauses "Having considered all options, the committee decided..." Formal, concise
Passive voice "It has been suggested that..." Impersonal, academic

2. Advanced Conditionals

  • Mixed conditional: "If the government had acted sooner, unemployment would be lower now."
  • Inverted conditional: "Were the policy to be implemented, significant changes would follow."
  • Subjunctive: "It is essential that measures be taken to address this issue."

3. Nominalisation

Turning verbs into nouns for more formal, abstract writing:

  • "The implementation of the policy" (not "When they implemented the policy")
  • "A thorough examination of the evidence" (not "When we examine the evidence thoroughly")
  • "The rapid expansion of urban areas" (not "Urban areas expanded rapidly")

4. Sophisticated Discourse Markers

Function Basic (B2) Sophisticated (C1)
Addition Also, Furthermore Moreover, In addition, What is more
Contrast But, However Nevertheless, Conversely, Notwithstanding
Result So, Therefore Consequently, As a result, Thus
Example For example For instance, By way of illustration
Concession Although While it is true that..., Despite the fact that...

5. Hedging Language

At C1, show nuance by not being too absolute:

  • "It could be argued that..." (not "This proves that...")
  • "This suggests that..." (not "This means that...")
  • "To some extent..." / "In certain circumstances..."
  • "Arguably..." / "Potentially..." / "It appears that..."

6. Abstract Vocabulary

Basic C1 Abstract
problem issue, challenge, dilemma, predicament
important crucial, pivotal, paramount, instrumental
big substantial, considerable, significant, profound
change transformation, shift, transition, modification
think contend, posit, hypothesise, postulate
Common Errors to Avoid at C1
Do Not:
  • Use contractions in formal writing (don't, can't, won't)
  • Use phrasal verbs when formal alternatives exist (put up with ‚Üí tolerate, look into ‚Üí investigate)
  • Use "I think" or "In my opinion" repeatedly (use "It could be argued," "Evidence suggests")
  • Write overly simple sentences - combine ideas using subordination
  • Use "very" + adjective (very big ‚Üí substantial, very good ‚Üí exemplary)
  • Start sentences with "And" or "But"
  • Use informal connectors (Besides, Anyway)
Do:
  • Vary your sentence structures (simple, compound, complex)
  • Use passive voice appropriately for impersonal tone
  • Include specific examples to support arguments
  • Use nominalisation for formal register
  • Employ sophisticated discourse markers
  • Show awareness of counterarguments
  • Edit carefully for grammar and spelling errors

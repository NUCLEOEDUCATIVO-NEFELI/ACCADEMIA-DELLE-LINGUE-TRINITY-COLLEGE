ISE I Reading Complete Guide

Task 1: Long Reading & Task 2: Multi-text Reading

Reading Module Overview

The ISE I Reading module consists of two tasks completed in 40 minutes (20 minutes per task). These tasks test your ability to understand main ideas, specific details, and factual information at B1 level.

Key Information:
  • Total Time: 40 minutes (advised: 20 mins per task)
  • Total Questions: 30 (15 per task)
  • Total Texts: 5 (1 long text + 4 short texts)
  • Total Word Count: ~1,100 words (400 + 700)
  • Scoring: 1 mark per question (30 marks total)
Component Task 1: Long Reading Task 2: Multi-text Reading
Texts 1 text, ~400 words, 5 paragraphs 4 texts, ~700 words total, 1 infographic
Time 20 minutes 20 minutes
Questions 15 questions (3 types) 15 questions (3 types)
Question Types Title matching, True statements, Gap-fill Text matching, True statements, Summary notes
Skills Tested Gist, specific info, detail Main ideas, specific info, synthesis
Important: Task 2 texts are used for Task 3 (Reading into Writing). The information you extract from these texts will be needed for your writing task. Pay attention to facts, opinions, and examples you can use later.

Task 1: Long Reading 20 minutes | 15 questions
Task Overview: Read one factual/descriptive text of approximately 400 words (divided into 5 paragraphs) and answer 15 questions testing different reading skills.

Sample Text Structure:

1Paragraph 1: Introduction to topic - often defines the subject and provides background information.

2Paragraph 2: First main point or aspect of the topic - develops the discussion.

3Paragraph 3: Second main point - may provide examples or contrast information.

4Paragraph 4: Third main point - often includes specific examples, case studies, or expert opinions.

5Paragraph 5: Conclusion - summarizes main points or provides final thoughts/recommendations.

Question Types Breakdown:

Questions 1-5: Title Matching

Match the correct title to each of the 5 paragraphs. You have 6 titles to choose from (one is a distractor).

Example:
Paragraph discusses how tour guides need patience and deal with problems.
Correct title: "The Challenges of Guiding" (not "The Perfect Job?")

Skill tested: Reading for gist and main ideas

Strategy: Read the first sentence of each paragraph (topic sentence) to identify the main idea.

Questions 6-10: True Statements Selection

Choose 5 true statements from a list of 8. Three statements are false or not given according to the text.

Example:
A) Tour guides often work long hours.
B) All tourists enjoy visiting famous monuments.
C) Step-on guides replace regular tour guides.

(Check text to determine which are true/false)

Skill tested: Reading for specific factual information

Strategy: Scan for keywords from statements. Watch for synonyms and paraphrases.

Questions 11-15: Sentence Completion

Complete sentences with exact words from the text (maximum 3 words per gap).

Example:
"Successful tour guides must be able to _______ difficult questions quickly."
Answer from text: "answer" (1 word)

"Tourists nowadays prefer to be called '_______' rather than tourists."
Answer: "travellers" (1 word)

Skill tested: Reading for detail and precise information

Strategy: Scan for keywords, check grammar fits, copy exactly from text.

Sample Task 1: Tour Guides

1For a lot of young people, being a tour guide seems to be an ideal way to make money. You visit wonderful places and meet interesting people ‚Äî it's almost like a paid holiday with no expenses. In reality, of course, it can be tiring, boring and stressful having to solve all the problems and deal with the demands of the people who have paid money for your services.

2And it seems that tourists these days demand more and more. This is partly because of the internet: before they go away on holiday, they do their research, so they already know a lot of the basic information that guides used to tell them. What they want is something different and special. It isn't enough any more just to take them round the famous buildings, monuments and tourist sites and tell them a few dates and interesting stories. In fact, tourists nowadays don't like to think they are tourists at all. Today's well-informed 'traveller' prefers culture, seeing something of people's real way of life in the place they're visiting.

3This need has led to a new kind of tour guide becoming popular. These people, sometimes called 'step-on guides', live in the area and have local knowledge. They don't take the place of the normal guide; their job is just temporary, 'stepping onto' the tour bus and taking visitors to unusual places and providing the kind of information that regular tour guides can't offer.

4Being a step-on guide can give you all the fun of being a tour guide without all the problems. "I really enjoy it," says Enrique, a university student who became a step-on guide in his home city of Valencia in Spain two years ago. "It's best not to think of it as just a job. Of course the extra money is useful but mainly it's a great way to meet people and give them an introduction to your town or city."

5Enrique has noticed how tourists have changed. "When I started, I talked to the visitors like a teacher, but I soon realised they wanted a conversation, not a lecture. These days I always try to give them a more personal experience."

Task 2: Multi-text Reading 20 minutes | 15 questions
Task Overview: Read 4 short texts (approximately 700 words total) on the same topic. One text is an infographic (diagram, table, chart, or map with text). Answer 15 questions testing synthesis and comparison skills.

Text Types in Task 2:

  • Text A: Article or blog post (opinion or experience)
  • Text B: Informational text or report
  • Text C: Interview or personal account
  • Text D: Infographic with visual information (diagram, chart, table, map)

Question Types Breakdown:

Questions 16-20: Text Matching

Match descriptions to the correct text (A, B, C, or D). Tests understanding of main idea or purpose of each text.

Example:
"Which text describes a more relaxing activity?" ‚Üí Match to correct letter
"Which text mentions the importance of diet?" ‚Üí Match to correct letter

Note: You can use the same letter more than once (up to 2 questions may have the same answer).

Skill tested: Understanding main ideas and purposes across multiple texts

Questions 21-25: True Statements Selection

Choose 5 true statements from 8 options based on information across all four texts.

Example:
"Swimming is good for people with joint problems." (Check if mentioned in any text)
"All four texts recommend swimming as the best exercise." (Check if true across all texts)

Skill tested: Cross-referencing information across multiple sources

Questions 26-30: Summary Notes Completion

Complete summary notes using exact words from the texts (maximum 3 words per gap). The notes summarize key information from all four texts.

Example:
Notes about different exercises:
"Swimming exercises all _______ and is good for _______."
"Cycling is _______ but can be dangerous in parks."

Skill tested: Synthesizing information from multiple texts into summary form

Critical: The information in these notes is exactly what you need for Task 3 (Reading into Writing). Pay attention to facts, statistics, opinions, and examples you can use in your essay.

Step-by-Step Reading Strategies

For Task 1 (Long Reading):

1 Skim the Text First (2 minutes)

Read quickly for general meaning. Look at:

  • Title and any headings
  • First sentence of each paragraph (topic sentences)
  • Last paragraph (conclusion)

Don't worry about unknown words at this stage.

2 Analyze the Questions (2 minutes)

Read through all 15 questions before detailed reading. Identify:

  • Question types (title matching, true/false, gap-fill)
  • Keywords to scan for
  • What information you need to find
3 Tackle Title Matching First (Q1-5)

While the text is fresh in your memory:

  • Read each paragraph again carefully
  • Identify the main idea (usually in first sentence)
  • Match to the most appropriate title
  • Remember: 6 titles, 5 paragraphs (one distractor)
4 True Statements (Q6-10)

For each statement:

  • Scan for keywords or synonyms
  • Find the relevant sentence in the text
  • Check if the statement matches the text exactly
  • If not mentioned ‚Üí False/Not given
5 Gap-fill (Q11-15)

For each gap:

  • Read the sentence with gap carefully
  • Identify grammar needed (noun, verb, adjective?)
  • Scan text for keywords from the sentence
  • Copy exact words from text (max 3 words)
  • Check grammar fits in the sentence

For Task 2 (Multi-text Reading):

1 Survey All Texts (3 minutes)

Quickly identify:

  • Text types (article, conversation, information, infographic)
  • Main topic (all texts are related)
  • Key features of the infographic
2 Read Questions Before Detailed Reading

Know what you're looking for before diving into texts.

3 Text Matching (Q16-20)

For each question:

  • Identify the key description in the question
  • Scan all four texts quickly
  • Match to the most appropriate text
  • Same text can be used for multiple questions
4 True Statements Across Texts (Q21-25)

Check information across all four texts. A statement might be true based on information combined from multiple texts.

5 Summary Notes (Q26-30)

These notes summarize all texts. Look for:

  • Information that appears in multiple texts (consensus)
  • Contrasting information between texts
  • Specific facts and statistics
Tip: The completed summary notes are essentially a plan for Task 3 (Reading into Writing). Note which text provides which information.

Vocabulary Strategies

Dealing with Unknown Words:

1 Context Clues

Look at the sentences before and after the unknown word. The context often explains the meaning.

Example: "The serpentine lake winds through the park like a snake."
Context suggests it's a winding, snake-like shape.
2 Word Formation

Look for familiar roots, prefixes, or suffixes.

"Unbelievable" = un (not) + believe + able (can be)
"Step-on guide" = compound noun (guides who step on buses)
3 Don't Panic

You don't need to understand every word to answer questions. Focus on the words you do know and the overall meaning.

Common B1 Vocabulary Areas:

  • Travel: destination, accommodation, itinerary, sightseeing
  • Health: fitness, nutrition, exercise routine, well-being
  • Education: curriculum, assignment, assessment, revision
  • Technology: device, application, download, update
  • Environment: pollution, recycling, sustainable, conservation
Practice Tips & Resources

How to Practice Effectively:

1 Use Official Materials First

Start with Trinity sample papers to understand the exact format. These are available at trinitycollege.com/ISEI

2 Time Yourself

Always practice with a timer. Stick to 20 minutes per task strictly, even if you haven't finished.

3 Read Widely

Read articles on the 12 subject areas. Good sources:

  • BBC Learning English
  • Simple Wikipedia
  • News websites (BBC, The Guardian)
  • Travel blogs and magazines
4 Build Vocabulary

Keep a vocabulary notebook. Note:

  • New words with example sentences
  • Synonyms (important for True/False questions)
  • Topic-specific vocabulary
5 Analyze Mistakes

When you get questions wrong, ask:

  • Did I misread the question?
  • Did I find the wrong part of the text?
  • Did I copy words incorrectly?
  • Did I misunderstand the vocabulary?
Exam Day Checklist
  • Read the instructions carefully before starting
  • Check how many questions there are (15 per task)
  • Look at the question types before reading texts
  • Write answers clearly on the answer sheet
  • For gap-fill: Copy exact words from text (max 3 words)
  • For true statements: Write exactly 5 answers (never leave blank)
  • Guess if unsure - no penalty for wrong answers
  • Watch the time - 20 minutes maximum per task
  • Transfer answers carefully if using rough work
  • Check spelling in gap-fill answers
  • Never use correction fluid - cross out mistakes clearly

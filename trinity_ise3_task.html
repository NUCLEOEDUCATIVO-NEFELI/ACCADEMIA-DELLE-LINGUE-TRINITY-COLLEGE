Reading, Writing, Speaking & Listening at C1 Level

ISE III Exam Overview (C1)

ISE III is a four-skills exam at CEFR C1 level (Proficient User). The exam demonstrates you can understand a wide range of demanding, longer texts, express yourself fluently and spontaneously without much obvious searching for expressions, and use language flexibly and effectively for social, academic, and professional purposes.

Key Differences from ISE II (B2):
  • Longer texts: 700 words vs. 500-900 words
  • More complex writing: 200-230 words vs. 150-180 words
  • Formal presentation: Topic task includes 4-minute presentation with handout
  • Extended speaking time: 25 minutes vs. 20 minutes
  • Abstract topics: Ambitions, stereotypes, role models, economic issues
  • C1 language functions: Deducing, inferring, hypothesising, staging

Task 1: Long Reading (20 minutes)
Task Overview: Read one complex text of approximately 700 words (divided into 5 paragraphs) and answer 15 questions testing different reading skills at C1 level.

Question Types Breakdown:

1 Questions 1-5: Title Matching

Match the correct title to each of the 5 paragraphs. You have 6 titles to choose from (one is a distractor).

Example: Paragraph discusses economic inequality solutions. Correct title: "Addressing the Wealth Gap"

Skill tested: Reading for gist, main ideas, and implicit meaning

2 Questions 6-10: True Statements Selection

Choose 5 true statements from a list of 8. Three statements are false according to the text.

Example: "Economic inequality has decreased in developed nations" (True/False based on text evidence)

Skill tested: Reading for specific factual information and inference

3 Questions 11-15: Sentence Completion

Complete sentences with exact words from the text (maximum 3 words per gap).

Example: "The author suggests that _______ policies are necessary to address climate change."
Answer from text: "comprehensive environmental"

Skill tested: Reading for detail and precise information

C1 Reading Strategies:
  • Identify tone and attitude: Is the author critical, supportive, or neutral?
  • Recognise implicit meaning: What is suggested but not directly stated?
  • Track argument development: How does the writer build their case?
  • Watch for hedging language: "may," "could," "potentially" indicate uncertainty
  • Understand discourse markers: "however," "furthermore," "consequently"
Task 2: Multi-text Reading (20 minutes)
Task Overview: Read 4 short texts (approximately 700 words total) on the same theme. One text is an infographic (diagram, table, or chart with text). Answer 15 questions.

Question Types Breakdown:

1 Questions 16-20: Text Matching

Match descriptions to the correct text (A, B, C, or D). Tests understanding of main idea or purpose of each text.

Example: "Which text presents statistical evidence?" ‚Üí Match to correct letter
2 Questions 21-25: True Statements Selection

Choose 5 true statements from 8 options based on information across all four texts.

3 Questions 26-30: Summary Notes Completion

Complete summary notes using exact words from the texts (maximum 3 words per gap). The notes synthesise key information from all four texts.

Important: The information in these texts is used for Task 3 (Reading into Writing). Pay attention to facts, statistics, opinions, and examples you can use in your writing.
Task 3: Reading into Writing (40 minutes)
Task Requirements:
  • Write 200-230 words 200-230 words
  • Use information from all four texts in Task 2
  • Include three content points specified in the prompt
  • Use your own words - copying from texts results in penalties
  • Genre: Article, essay, formal/informal email, review, or report

Sample Task Structure:

Example Prompt:
"Write an article (200-230 words) for a political magazine about economic inequality in your country. Use the information from texts A-D to:
¬ª explain the causes of economic inequality
¬ª evaluate the effectiveness of current government policies
¬ª suggest practical solutions for reducing the wealth gap"
1 Analyse the Prompt

Identify the three bullet points you must address. Note the genre (article) and register (formal/academic).

2 Select and Adapt Information

Go back to Task 2 texts. Find relevant information for each bullet point. Make notes in your own words:

  • Text A: Causes ‚Üí "globalisation and technological change"
  • Text B: Current policies ‚Üí "progressive taxation with mixed results"
  • Text C: Solutions ‚Üí "investment in education and infrastructure"
  • Text D: Expert opinion ‚Üí "minimum wage increases and wealth taxes"
3 Plan Your Structure
  • Introduction (30-35 words): Hook the reader and introduce the topic
  • Body Paragraph 1 (50-60 words): Address bullet point 1 with evidence from texts
  • Body Paragraph 2 (50-60 words): Address bullet point 2 with evaluation
  • Body Paragraph 3 (40-50 words): Address bullet point 3 + examples
  • Conclusion (30-35 words): Summary + forward-looking statement
Critical Success Factors:
  • ‚úì Synthesise - combine information from multiple texts
  • ‚úì Include information from ALL four texts
  • ‚úì Address all three bullet points explicitly
  • ‚úì Use sophisticated linking words (furthermore, however, consequently)
  • ‚úì Maintain consistent formal register throughout
  • ‚úì Use C1 grammar structures (inversion, subjunctive, advanced conditionals)
Task 4: Extended Writing (40 minutes)
Task Requirements:
  • Write 200-230 words 200-230 words
  • No reading texts provided - use your own ideas and knowledge
  • Include two content points specified in the prompt
  • Genre: Descriptive essay, discursive essay, argumentative essay, article, formal/informal email/letter, review, or report

C1 Genre Requirements:

1. Argumentative Essay

Clear thesis, sophisticated argumentation, counterarguments, refutation, persuasive language.

Example: "To what extent has social media improved communication? Discuss both sides and give your opinion."

2. Discursive Essay

Balanced discussion of different viewpoints, objective tone, weighing evidence.

Example: "Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of artificial intelligence in the workplace."

3. Article (Magazine/Online)

Engaging title, sophisticated vocabulary, rhetorical devices, authoritative tone.

Example: "Write an article about the future of renewable energy."

4. Formal Email/Letter

Highly professional tone, complex sentence structures, formal conventions, persuasive purpose.

Example: "Write a letter to a government minister proposing policy changes."

5. Review

Sophisticated evaluation, nuanced opinion, cultural context, recommendation.

Example: "Review a recent documentary about climate change, evaluating its impact."

6. Report

Objective tone, analytical approach, data interpretation, recommendations.

Example: "Write a report on the impact of remote working on productivity."

C1 Language Requirements:
  • Complex sentences: Inversion, cleft sentences, participle clauses
  • Advanced conditionals: Mixed conditionals, subjunctive mood
  • Sophisticated vocabulary: Abstract nouns, academic collocations
  • Nominalisation: "The implementation of the policy resulted in..."
  • Discourse markers: "Notwithstanding," "Conversely," "Furthermore"
Topic Task (8 minutes)
Task Overview: You prepare a topic before the exam and deliver a formal presentation (4 minutes) with a handout. This is followed by a discussion (4 minutes) where the examiner asks questions about your presentation. You must ask the examiner for their comments.

Preparing Your Topic:

  • Choose a discursive topic (arguments for/against, benefits/drawbacks)
  • Prepare a formal presentation with clear structure
  • Create a handout for the examiner with brief notes
  • Bring brief notes for yourself (not a full script)
  • Do NOT choose from the 12 subject areas (these are for Conversation task)
Sample Topic: "To what extent has social media benefited society?"

Structure:
1. Introduction - define social media and its prevalence
2. Arguments for - connectivity, information access, business opportunities
3. Arguments against - privacy concerns, mental health, misinformation
4. Evaluation - balanced view with evidence
5. Conclusion - personal stance with justification

Handout content: Main arguments, key statistics, sources

C1 Language Functions to Demonstrate:

Function Example Language
Developing and justifying arguments "The primary argument in favour is..." / "This is supported by the fact that..."
Summarising "To summarise the key points..." / "In essence, the evidence suggests..."
Evaluating options/standpoints "From one perspective... however, conversely..." / "The merits of X are..."
Deducing and inferring "This implies that..." / "It can be deduced from this that..."
Staging "Having considered X, I shall now examine Y..." / "Turning to the next point..."
Hypothesising "Were this situation to continue..." / "Should X occur, the consequences would be..."
Key Strategies:
  • Structure your presentation clearly with signposting
  • Use formal, academic language throughout
  • At the end, ask: "Do you have any questions or comments?"
  • Be prepared to defend your arguments and consider alternatives
  • Use your handout to guide the discussion
Collaborative Task (4 minutes)
Task Overview: The examiner reads you a prompt. You must initiate, lead, and maintain the interaction by asking questions and making suggestions. This tests your ability to collaborate and take control of a conversation at C1 level.

Task Structure:

Phase 1 - Prompt: Examiner reads a scenario (e.g., ethical dilemma, complex problem, policy decision)

Phase 2 - Your Role: You must immediately start asking questions and making suggestions. Do NOT wait for the examiner to ask you questions.

Phase 3 - Development: Build on the examiner's responses, offer alternatives, reach conclusions.
Sample Prompt:
"A friend of mine has been offered a promotion at work which would mean moving to another country. She's excited about the opportunity but worried about leaving her elderly parents behind. She's asked me for advice but I'm not sure what to suggest."

Your opening: "That's a complex situation with no easy answer. Shall we start by considering what factors she should weigh up? For instance, how serious are her parents' health concerns, and what support do they currently have?"

Useful Collaborative Language:

  • Opening: "Shall we start by...?" / "Where shall we begin?" / "I'd suggest we first consider..."
  • Making suggestions: "How about...?" / "Why don't we...?" / "Perhaps we could..."
  • Asking for opinions: "What do you think about...?" / "Do you think...would work?" / "What's your view on...?"
  • Responding: "That's a good point" / "I hadn't thought of that" / "I'm not sure that would work because..."
  • Negotiating: "We could compromise by..." / "What if we combined both ideas?" / "Let's settle on..."
  • Closing: "So we've agreed on..." / "To summarise our decisions..." / "Shall we move on to...?"
Mandatory Requirements:
  • Take control immediately - don't wait for examiner questions
  • Ask multiple questions to gather information
  • Make suggestions and offer alternatives
  • Respond to examiner contributions
  • Work towards a conclusion or decision
Conversation Task (3 minutes)
Task Overview: The examiner chooses one subject area from the 12 listed areas and initiates conversation. You must respond with opinions, ask questions, and maintain interaction at C1 level.

Key Requirements:

  • Respond to questions with extended, sophisticated answers
  • Demonstrate knowledge of the subject area
  • Ask the examiner questions about the subject area
  • Use C1 language functions (deducing, evaluating, hypothesising)
Sample Conversation about "The Media":

Examiner: "How do you think the media influences public opinion?"
Candidate: "The media's influence is profound and multifaceted. On one hand, it serves an essential democratic function by informing citizens. However, the concentration of media ownership raises concerns about bias. Were we to have truly independent media, public discourse might be more balanced. What is your perspective on media ownership regulations?"
Examiner: "I think there should be more diversity in media ownership."
Candidate: "I concur. The current consolidation limits viewpoint diversity. This implies that policy intervention may be necessary. Would you support stricter antitrust measures in the media sector?"
Speaking Assessment Criteria
Criterion What the Examiner Looks For
Communicative Effectiveness Task fulfilment, appropriate turn-taking, initiating and maintaining interaction, elaborating responses, using C1 functions
Interactive Listening Understanding examiner questions, responding appropriately, asking for clarification when needed
Language Control Range and accuracy of grammar, vocabulary precision, C1 structures (inversion, subjunctive, advanced conditionals)
Delivery Pronunciation, intonation, fluency, appropriate speed, clear articulation
Independent Listening Task (8 minutes)
Task Overview: Listen twice to a complex recording (approximately 3 minutes 30 seconds). First, report the gist (main idea). Second, answer specific questions from the examiner. Finally, engage in discussion about the content.

Task Structure:

Phase 1 - First Listening:
Listen for gist. The examiner will ask: "What is the speaker talking about?" or "What is the main topic?"

Your response: A comprehensive summary in 2-3 sentences showing understanding of the main argument and perspective.
Example: "The speaker is examining the ethical implications of artificial intelligence in healthcare, arguing that while AI offers significant benefits for diagnosis, it raises serious concerns about patient privacy and the dehumanisation of care."
Phase 2 - Second Listening:
Listen for specific information. Take detailed notes.

After listening: Answer examiner's questions about specific content, opinions, and implications.
Example: "According to the speaker, what are the main advantages of AI in healthcare?" / "What concerns does the speaker raise about privacy?"
Phase 3 - Discussion:
The examiner asks follow-up questions requiring you to discuss the implications and relate to broader issues.

Example: "To what extent do you think the benefits of AI outweigh the risks?" / "How might these issues affect your country specifically?"

Assessment Criteria (Independent Listening):

Score Description
4 Identifies gist accurately, understands detailed information, responds to questions with sophistication, shows critical engagement
3 Identifies gist, understands most details, responds appropriately with minor hesitation
2 Identifies partial gist, understands some details, requires repetition of some questions
1 Identifies minimal information, struggles with detailed questions
0 No performance to assess or task not attempted
Note-Taking Tips:
  • Use abbreviations and symbols (&, +, ‚Üí, ‚Üë, ?)
  • Write keywords only - don't try to write full sentences
  • Organise notes spatially (left for main ideas, right for details)
  • Notes are not assessed - only your oral responses count

ISE IV Exam Overview (C2 - Mastery)

ISE IV is the pinnacle of the Trinity ISE suite, at CEFR C2 level (Mastery). This exam demonstrates you can understand with ease virtually everything heard or read, summarise information from different spoken and written sources, reconstruct arguments and accounts in a coherent presentation, and express yourself spontaneously, very fluently and precisely, differentiating finer shades of meaning even in more complex situations.

üéì C2 Mastery Characteristics:
  • Precision: Can express subtle nuances and shades of meaning
  • Complexity: Handles highly abstract, technical, and academic content
  • Fluency: Speaks without hesitation or searching for expressions
  • Accuracy: Maintains consistent grammatical control even in complex structures
  • Sophistication: Uses idiomatic expressions and colloquialisms appropriately
  • Critical Analysis: Evaluates complex arguments and counter-arguments

Key Differences from ISE III (C1):
  • Longer, denser texts: 900+ words with complex argumentation

  • Abstract subject areas: Society and Living Standards, The Nature of Knowledge, etc.
  • C2 language functions: Persuading, advocating, hypothesising with nuance
  • Critical evaluation: Must demonstrate independent critical thinking

Task 1: Long Reading (25 minutes)
Task Overview: Read one highly complex text of approximately 900+ words with sophisticated argumentation, multiple perspectives, and abstract concepts. Answer 15 questions testing C2-level reading skills.

Question Types Breakdown:

1 Questions 1-5: Title Matching

Match the correct title to each of the 5 paragraphs. You have 6 titles to choose from (one is a distractor). Texts contain complex rhetorical structures and implicit arguments.

Example: Paragraph discusses epistemological approaches to knowledge construction. Correct title: "Deconstructing Truth: Postmodern Perspectives"

Skill tested: Understanding implicit argumentation, rhetorical purpose, and sophisticated discourse markers

2 Questions 6-10: True Statements Selection

Choose 5 true statements from a list of 8. Three statements are false or not mentioned. Requires distinguishing between explicit claims, implied meanings, and inferences.

Example: "The author implies that positivist epistemology is fundamentally flawed" (True - requires inference from critical tone)

Skill tested: Critical evaluation, distinguishing fact from opinion, recognising authorial stance

3 Questions 11-15: Sentence Completion

Complete sentences with exact words from the text (maximum 3 words per gap). Tests precise understanding of complex academic vocabulary and collocations.

Example: "The author contends that _______ discourse has obscured the _______ nature of knowledge production."
Answer: "hegemonic" / "provisional"

Skill tested: Academic vocabulary recognition, understanding of collocations, precise comprehension

C2 Reading Strategies:
  • Deconstruct argumentation: Identify premises, evidence, warrants, and conclusions
  • Recognise rhetorical devices: Irony, understatement, hyperbole, rhetorical questions
  • Track authorial voice: Distinguish between author's views and reported views
  • Understand hedging: "It could be argued," "tends to suggest" indicate epistemic stance
  • Identify intertextuality: References to other works, traditions, or schools of thought
  • Evaluate evidence quality: Statistical vs. anecdotal, primary vs. secondary sources
Task 2: Multi-text Reading (25 minutes)
Task Overview: Read 4 complex texts (approximately 900+ words total) on a sophisticated theme. One text is an infographic with complex data representation. Answer 15 questions requiring synthesis and critical evaluation.

Question Types Breakdown:

1 Questions 16-20: Text Matching

Match descriptions to the correct text (A, B, C, or D). Tests understanding of rhetorical purpose, methodology, and theoretical stance.

Example: "Which text adopts a phenomenological approach to the research question?" ‚Üí Requires knowledge of research methodologies
2 Questions 21-25: True Statements Selection

Choose 5 true statements from 8 options based on information across all four texts. Requires synthesising conflicting viewpoints.

3 Questions 26-30: Summary Notes Completion

Complete summary notes using exact words from the texts (maximum 3 words per gap). The notes synthesise complex theoretical positions and empirical evidence.

Important: The information in these texts is used for Task 3 (Reading into Writing). You must synthesise academic perspectives, empirical data, and theoretical frameworks in your writing.
Task 3: Reading into Writing (50 minutes)
Task Requirements:
  • Write 250-300 words 250-300 words
  • Use information from all four texts in Task 2
  • Include three content points specified in the prompt
  • Demonstrate critical synthesis - evaluate and compare sources, not just summarise
  • Use your own words with sophisticated academic register
  • Genre: Academic essay, critical article, formal report, or analytical review

Sample Task Structure:

Example Prompt:
"Write an academic article (250-300 words) for a journal of political philosophy examining the relationship between individual autonomy and social responsibility. Use the information from texts A-D to:
¬ª critically evaluate competing conceptions of liberty
¬ª analyse the tension between negative and positive freedom
¬ª propose a synthesis that reconciles individual rights with collective obligations"
1 Analyse the Prompt Critically

Identify the three bullet points and the required critical stance. Note the genre (academic article) and sophisticated register (formal, objective, analytical).

2 Select and Critically Evaluate Sources

Go back to Task 2 texts. Evaluate the theoretical positions and empirical evidence. Make analytical notes:

  • Text A: Libertarian perspective (Isaiah Berlin) - strong on negative liberty
  • Text B: Communitarian critique (Charles Taylor) - positive freedom essential
  • Text C: Empirical study - Scandinavian model balances both effectively
  • Text D: Critical synthesis (Amartya Sen) - capability approach reconciles tension
3 Plan Your Argument Structure
  • Introduction (40-50 words): Contextualise the debate, state thesis, outline analytical approach
  • Body Paragraph 1 (60-70 words): Evaluate competing conceptions with evidence from texts
  • Body Paragraph 2 (60-70 words): Analyse tension using theoretical frameworks
  • Body Paragraph 3 (50-60 words): Propose synthesis based on critical evaluation
  • Conclusion (40-50 words): Restate thesis, implications, future research directions
Critical Success Factors:
  • ‚úì Synthesise sources critically - compare, contrast, and evaluate theoretical positions
  • ‚úì Include information from ALL four texts with explicit attribution
  • ‚úì Address all three bullet points with sophisticated argumentation
  • ‚úì Use C2 discourse markers: "Notwithstanding," "Be that as it may," "Conversely"
  • ‚úì Maintain consistent academic register with nominalisation and passive constructions
  • ‚úì Deploy C2 grammar: Inversion, subjunctive, mixed conditionals, cleft sentences
  • ‚úì Demonstrate critical stance: evaluate evidence quality and theoretical coherence
Task 4: Extended Writing (50 minutes)
Task Requirements:
  • Write 250-300 words 250-300 words
  • No reading texts provided - demonstrate independent critical thinking
  • Include two content points specified in the prompt
  • Genre: Academic essay, critical review, formal report, or discursive article

C2 Genre Requirements:

1. Academic Essay

Thesis-driven argument with literature engagement, methodological awareness, and theoretical sophistication.

Example: "Critically examine the proposition that artificial intelligence will fundamentally alter human consciousness. Evaluate competing theoretical perspectives and propose your own analytical framework."

2. Critical Review

Evaluation of a book, policy, or cultural phenomenon with theoretical framework and evidence-based judgment.

Example: "Review recent developments in climate policy, evaluating the effectiveness of international agreements from a game-theoretic perspective."

3. Formal Report

Analytical, evidence-based investigation with recommendations. Objective tone with sophisticated data interpretation.

Example: "Write a report analysing the socio-economic implications of the gig economy, drawing on relevant theoretical frameworks and empirical evidence."

4. Discursive Article

Sophisticated exploration of complex issues with rhetorical flair and analytical depth.

Example: "Write an article exploring the philosophical implications of quantum mechanics for our understanding of reality and free will."

C2 Language Requirements:
  • Complex syntactic structures: Embedded clauses, fronting, inversion ("Rarely has such a profound shift...")
  • Subjunctive mood: "Were this hypothesis to be confirmed..." / "It is essential that research be conducted..."
  • Sophisticated modality: Epistemic ("may well," "must necessarily") and deontic ("ought to," "is bound to")
  • Abstract nominalisation: "The commodification of knowledge" / "The instrumentalisation of education"
  • Academic collocations: "empirical evidence," "theoretical framework," "analytical approach"
  • Discourse markers: "Admittedly," "Conversely," "Be that as it may," "Notwithstanding"
Topic Task (8 minutes)
Task Overview: Deliver a formal academic presentation (4 minutes) with a handout on a sophisticated discursive topic. Followed by a critical discussion (4 minutes) where you defend your arguments, respond to challenges, and engage with examiner's perspectives.

Preparing Your Topic:

  • Choose a highly abstract, discursive topic requiring sophisticated argumentation
  • Prepare a formal academic presentation with theoretical framework
  • Create a handout with key arguments, evidence, and theoretical references
  • Bring brief notes (not a full script) with key points and evidence
  • Do NOT choose from the 12 subject areas (reserved for Conversation task)
Sample Topic: "The Instrumentalisation of Higher Education: A Critical Examination of Neoliberal Paradigms"

Structure:
1. Introduction - Define instrumentalisation and neoliberal context
2. Theoretical Framework - Drawing on Habermas (lifeworld colonisation) and Ball (performativity)
3. Critical Analysis - Marketisation effects on knowledge production, academic autonomy, student subjectivity
4. Counter-arguments - Economic necessity, accessibility, accountability
5. Synthesis - Propose alternative model based on capabilities approach
6. Conclusion - Invite critical questions

Handout content: Theoretical framework, key empirical studies, conceptual diagram, discussion questions

C2 Language Functions to Demonstrate:

Function Example Language
Advocating and persuading "It is imperative that..." / "One cannot overstate the significance of..."
Speculating with nuance "Were this trend to continue..." / "Should such policies be implemented..."
Evaluating critically "While compelling, this argument overlooks..." / "The evidence is not unequivocal..."
Defending and justifying "This contention is substantiated by..." / "The theoretical coherence is evident in..."
Conceding and countering "Admittedly, X is valid; however, Y remains problematic because..."
Hypothesising with precision "In the hypothetical scenario where..." / "Presupposing that X obtains..."
Key Strategies:
  • Structure with sophisticated signposting and theoretical framing
  • Use academic register consistently with appropriate terminology
  • At the end, explicitly invite critical engagement: "I welcome your critical reflections on..."
  • Defend arguments with evidence while acknowledging limitations
  • Engage with examiner's challenges using concession-counter patterns
  • Demonstrate metacognitive awareness: "Reflecting on my own position..."
Collaborative Task (4 minutes)
Task Overview: The examiner presents a complex, abstract scenario. You must immediately initiate, lead, and sustain sophisticated interaction, demonstrating critical thinking and collaborative problem-solving at C2 level.

Task Structure:

Phase 1 - Complex Prompt: Examiner presents an abstract dilemma (e.g., ethical paradox, policy trade-off, philosophical tension)

Phase 2 - Your Leadership: You must immediately demonstrate critical engagement and lead the exploration

Phase 3 - Sophisticated Development: Analyse multiple perspectives, evaluate trade-offs, propose nuanced solutions
Sample Prompt:
"A renowned university is considering implementing a policy where admission decisions are made entirely by an AI algorithm that claims to eliminate human bias. The algorithm has demonstrated higher predictive validity for academic success than human admissions officers. However, critics argue this represents a form of 'algorithmic governmentality' that fundamentally alters the nature of educational judgment and may reproduce hidden biases. The university has asked for your consultation on this policy."

Your opening: "This presents a profound tension between epistemic and ethical rationality. Shall we begin by interrogating the assumption that predictive validity equates to educational justice? I would suggest we first examine the theoretical framework - are we conceiving of education as credentialing or capability development?"

Sophisticated Collaborative Language:

  • Critical opening: "This raises fundamental questions about..." / "Shall we interrogate the premise that...?"
  • Analytical suggestions: "Perhaps we might conceptualise this as..." / "I propose we distinguish between X and Y..."
  • Evaluative questioning: "To what extent does...?" / "How might we weigh...against...?"
  • Critical response: "That's a compelling perspective; however, one might counter that..." / "While valid, that overlooks..."
  • Sophisticated negotiation: "We might reconcile these positions by..." / "A synthesis could be achieved through..."
  • Meta-discursive closing: "To recapitulate our analytical trajectory..." / "We have established that..."
Mandatory Requirements:
  • Demonstrate immediate critical engagement with abstract concepts
  • Lead with sophisticated questions that probe assumptions
  • Propose analytical frameworks for understanding the problem
  • Evaluate multiple perspectives with evidence
  • Negotiate toward nuanced, qualified conclusions
  • Maintain academic register throughout
Conversation Task (3 minutes)
Task Overview: The examiner chooses one subject area from the 12 listed areas. You must engage in sophisticated, spontaneous discussion demonstrating deep knowledge, critical thinking, and C2 language control.

Key Requirements:

  • Respond with extended, sophisticated, theoretically-informed answers
  • Demonstrate deep knowledge of abstract concepts and current debates
  • Ask examiner questions that demonstrate critical engagement
  • Use C2 language functions (advocating, speculating, evaluating with nuance)
  • Handle topic shifts and abstract questions with ease
Sample Conversation about "The Nature of Knowledge":

Examiner: "To what extent can we claim that scientific knowledge is objective?"
Candidate: "This touches on fundamental epistemological debates. From a realist perspective, scientific knowledge aspires to objective truth through methodological rigour. However, the strong programme in sociology of science compellingly demonstrates how social factors shape scientific consensus. Kuhn's paradigm shifts suggest that scientific progress is not purely cumulative. I would contend that while methods aim at objectivity, the interpretation of data is invariably theory-laden. What is your position on the demarcation between science and other knowledge systems?"
Examiner: "I think there's a distinction between the method and the social practice."
Candidate: "Precisely. That distinction is crucial yet problematic. The method's normative ideal of objectivity is often undermined by the institutional practices of science - funding priorities, publication biases, disciplinary gatekeeping. This suggests that epistemic norms and social structures are dialectically related. Would you accept that the sociology of scientific knowledge has undermined the authority of science, or merely complicated our understanding of it?"

Independent Listening Task (8 minutes)
Task Overview: Listen twice to a complex, academic recording (approximately 3 minutes 30 seconds) with sophisticated argumentation. First, report the gist with critical evaluation. Second, answer analytical questions. Finally, engage in critical discussion evaluating the content and implications.

Task Structure:

Phase 1 - First Listening:
Listen for argument structure and theoretical stance. The examiner will ask: "What is the speaker's main argument and theoretical position?"

Your response: A sophisticated summary identifying the thesis, methodology, and critical stance.
Example: "The speaker advances a post-colonial critique of development economics, arguing that mainstream GDP-centric models perpetuate epistemic violence by marginalising indigenous knowledge systems. Drawing on Escobar and Spivak, the speaker advocates for pluriversal approaches to development that recognise ontological diversity."
Phase 2 - Second Listening:
Listen for specific arguments, evidence, and rhetorical strategies. Take analytical notes.

After listening: Answer examiner's questions about theoretical frameworks, evidence quality, and implications.
Example: "What evidence does the speaker provide for the claim that GDP measures are epistemically violent?" / "How does the speaker distinguish between development and post-development approaches?"
Phase 3 - Critical Discussion:
The examiner asks follow-up questions requiring critical evaluation of the speaker's arguments and their implications.

Example: "To what extent do you find the speaker's critique of development economics persuasive?" / "How might these arguments be applied to policy in your context?" / "What are the potential limitations of the pluriversal approach the speaker advocates?"

Assessment Criteria (Independent Listening - C2):

Score Description
4 Identifies complex argument structure, understands nuanced theoretical positions, responds with critical sophistication, evaluates speaker's stance independently
3 Identifies main argument and most details, responds appropriately with minor analytical gaps
2 Identifies partial argument, understands some details, requires clarification of complex points
1 Identifies minimal information, struggles with theoretical content
0 No performance to assess or task not attempted
Critical Listening Strategies:
  • Identify the speaker's theoretical framework and methodological approach
  • Track the argument structure: thesis, evidence, counter-arguments, conclusion
  • Note hedging language that indicates epistemic stance ("arguably," "tentatively")
  • Recognise rhetorical devices: irony, understatement, appeals to authority
  • Evaluate evidence quality: statistical, anecdotal, theoretical, empirical
  • Prepare to critique: identify strengths, limitations, and alternative perspectives

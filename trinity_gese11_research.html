Grade 11 Advanced Grammar and Discourse

Justifying Arguments, Inferring, Expressing Caution and Empathy

Grade 11 Overview (C1.2 Level)

Grade 11 of the Trinity College London GESE exam corresponds to level C1.2 (advanced) of the CEFR. At this level, candidates demonstrate sophisticated mastery of complex grammatical structures, nuanced language use, and the ability to engage in abstract discourse.

Key Features of Grade 11:
  • Duration: 25 minutes (fully candidate-led discussion)
  • Topic Presentation: Formal presentation on a discursive topic (5 minutes)
  • Topic Discussion: Challenging ideas and defending arguments (5 minutes)
  • Interactive Task: Controlling the interaction through questioning (5 minutes)
  • Listening Task: Inferring meaning and predicting outcomes (3 minutes)
  • Conversation: Two subject areas from selected lists (6 minutes)
Exam Tip: At Grade 11, you must demonstrate the ability to justify arguments, infer meaning, express caution, show empathy, challenge opinions, evaluate standpoints, and express reservations using sophisticated linguistic devices.

Advanced Language Functions
Grade 11 Language Functions:

  • Expressing empathy and sympathy
  • Challenging arguments and opinions respectfully
  • Evaluating different standpoints critically

Complex Passive Structures with Modals

At C1.2 level, candidates must demonstrate mastery of complex passive constructions combined with modal verbs to express degrees of certainty, obligation, and speculation.

Structure Function Example
should/must + have been + pp Deduction about past It must have been destroyed by the fire.
could/might + have been + pp Possibility in past It could have been prevented with better planning.
ought to + have been + pp Unfulfilled obligation He ought to have been informed earlier.
needn't + have been + pp Unnecessary past action It needn't have been mentioned at the meeting.
can't/couldn't + have been + pp Negative deduction It can't have been written by him.
Advanced Examples:
• The report should have been submitted before the deadline, but the system failed.
• These measures might have been implemented earlier had the risks been recognized.
• The data is believed to have been manipulated for political purposes.
Hedging and Cautious Language

Expressing caution is essential at C1.2. Hedging allows you to soften statements, express degrees of certainty, and acknowledge limitations in your arguments.

Modal Verbs for Hedging:
• may/might/could + infinitive (possibility)
• seems/appears to + infinitive (tentative observation)
• tends to + infinitive (general tendency)
• is likely/unlikely to + infinitive (probability)
Examples of Hedging:
• This might suggest that economic factors play a significant role.
• It could be argued that traditional methods remain effective.
• The evidence tends to indicate a correlation between the variables.
• This appears to be the most viable solution in most cases.
Advanced Hedging Phrases:
• To a certain extent... / Up to a point...
• With certain reservations...
• Subject to verification...
• Barring unforeseen circumstances...
• All things being equal...
Verbs Followed by Gerund and/or Infinitive

Grade 11 requires precise control of verb patterns, particularly verbs where meaning changes depending on whether they are followed by gerund or infinitive.

Verb + Gerund (-ing) + Infinitive (to)
forget Forget posting the letter. (forget that you did it) Forget to post the letter. (fail to do it)
remember Remember meeting him. (past memory) Remember to meet him. (future obligation)
stop Stop smoking. (quit an activity) Stop to smoke. (pause in order to smoke)
regret Regret telling him. (past action) Regret to tell you... (formal bad news)
try Try eating less. (experiment) Try to eat less. (make an effort)
mean Doing that means leaving early. (involves) I mean to leave early. (intend)
go on Go on talking. (continue) Go on to talk about... (change subject)
Advanced Reporting and Discourse Markers
Signposting Expressions for Formal Discourse:
Function Expressions
Opening I'd like to begin with..., To start with..., First and foremost...
Adding points Furthermore, Moreover, In addition, What is more...
Contrasting Conversely, On the contrary, Having said that, That being said...
Conceding Admittedly, While it is true that..., I accept that..., Notwithstanding...
Emphasizing It is worth noting that..., Significantly, Crucially...
Concluding To conclude, In light of the above, Taking all factors into account...
Vague and Imprecise Language (C1.2 Feature):
• Sort of / Kind of (softening)
• And so on / And the like (etc.)
• More or less / Give or take (approximately)
• Things like that / That sort of thing (generalization)
• A bit / Somewhat (slightly)
Wish and Hope - Correct Patterns
Wish + Past Perfect: Regrets about the past
• I wish I had studied harder. (but I didn't)
Wish + Past Simple: Present/future desires (unreal)
• I wish I knew the answer. (but I don't)
Hope + Present/Past: Real possibilities
• I hope you feel better soon.
• I hope you managed to finish it.
Common Error: Do not use "wish" + "would" for your own actions when you have control:
❌ I wish I would study more.
✅ I wish I studied more. / I wish I had studied more.

How to Write the Grade 11 Discursive Essay

Requirements: At Grade 11, you must prepare a Formal Topic Presentation - a discursive essay (approximately 400-500 words) that presents arguments, evaluates different standpoints, and reaches a balanced conclusion. You must provide a written copy to the examiner.

Step 1: Choose a Discursive Topic

Your topic must be discursive (argumentative/debatable), not descriptive. Good topics present two or more valid perspectives.

Examples:

  • "To what extent does social media enhance or hinder interpersonal communication?"
  • "Is competitiveness in education beneficial or detrimental to student wellbeing?"
  • "Should governments prioritize economic growth over environmental protection?"

Avoid: "My family," "My holiday," "My favorite book" (these are descriptive, not discursive)

Step 2: Structure Your Essay

Introduction (50-60 words):

  • Introduce the topic and its relevance
  • Present the two/main sides of the argument
  • Include a thesis statement outlining your position

Body Paragraph 1 - Counter-argument (100-120 words):

  • Present the opposing view fairly
  • Use hedging language: "It could be argued that...", "Some might suggest..."
  • Provide evidence or examples

Body Paragraph 2 - Your Argument (100-120 words):

  • Present your main argument with strong connectors
  • Use signposting: "Conversely...", "However, a more compelling view is..."
  • Support with logical reasoning and examples

Body Paragraph 3 - Synthesis/Evaluation (80-100 words):

  • Evaluate both positions critically
  • Acknowledge limitations: "While the former argument has merit..."
  • Express reservations where appropriate

Conclusion (50-60 words):

  • Summarize key points without repeating verbatim
  • Restate position with qualification if necessary
  • End with a thought-provoking final statement

Step 3: Use Advanced Connectors

Replace basic connectors with sophisticated alternatives:

  • Instead of "but" → nevertheless, conversely, having said that
  • Instead of "also" → furthermore, moreover, in addition
  • Instead of "so" → consequently, as a result, therefore
  • Instead of "I think" → it could be argued that, the evidence suggests

Step 4: Practice Delivery

  • Time yourself: Maximum 5 minutes speaking
  • Maintain eye contact while referring to notes
  • Use intonation to emphasize key points
  • Prepare for challenging questions from the examiner
Sample Essay Opening:
"While it is undeniable that technological advancement has revolutionized educational practices, the extent to which digital learning platforms enhance academic outcomes remains a subject of considerable debate. This presentation will evaluate both the purported benefits of increased accessibility and the potential drawbacks regarding student engagement, ultimately arguing that a hybrid approach may offer the most viable solution."
Language for Challenging and Justifying
Function Phrases
Challenging politely "With respect, that assumes...", "That may be the case, however...", "I'm not entirely convinced that..."
Justifying strongly "The evidence clearly indicates...", "It stands to reason that...", "There is compelling evidence to suggest..."
Expressing reservations "I have certain reservations about...", "That being said, one must acknowledge...", "With certain caveats..."
Expressing empathy "I appreciate that position, given that...", "One can certainly understand why...", "It is understandable that..."

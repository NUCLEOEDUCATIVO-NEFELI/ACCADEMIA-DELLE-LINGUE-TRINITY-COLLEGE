Grade 9 Intermediate Grammar and Discourse

Expressing Abstract Ideas, Regrets, Wishes, and Evaluating Options

Grade 9 Overview (B2.3 Level)

Grade 9 of the Trinity College London GESE exam corresponds to level B2.3 (upper-intermediate) of the CEFR. At this level, candidates demonstrate the ability to communicate with spontaneity, engage in extended conversation, and handle complex language structures.

Key Features of Grade 9:
  • Duration: 15 minutes
  • Topic Discussion: Candidate-led discussion of prepared topic (5 minutes)
  • Interactive Task: Controlling interaction through questioning (4 minutes)
  • Conversation: Two subject areas from the prescribed list (5 minutes)
Exam Tip: At Grade 9, you must demonstrate the ability to express abstract ideas, hypothesise, evaluate options, express regrets and wishes, and maintain discourse using paraphrasing and cohesive devices.
Subject Areas for Conversation

The examiner will select two subject areas from the following list. You must not choose your topic from these areas.

Dreams and nightmares — Sleep experiences, interpretations, recurring themes
Crime and punishment — Justice system, rehabilitation, deterrence
Technology — Innovation, dependence, future implications
Habits and obsessions — Daily routines, compulsive behaviors, breaking habits
Global environmental issues — Climate change, conservation, sustainability
Design — Architecture, product design, aesthetics vs. function
Language Functions at Grade 9
You must demonstrate these communicative functions:
  • Expressing abstract ideas — Discussing concepts beyond concrete experience
  • Expressing regrets, wishes and hopes — Using correct verb patterns
  • Expressing assumptions — Making educated guesses about situations
  • Paraphrasing — Rephrasing when communication breaks down
  • Evaluating options — Weighing advantages and disadvantages
  • Hypothesising — Discussing hypothetical situations
  • Evaluating past actions — Assessing decisions and consequences
Mixed Conditionals

At Grade 9, you must demonstrate mixed conditionals — combining different time references in conditional sentences.

Type Structure Example
Mixed 2nd/3rd If + past perfect, would + base If I had studied harder, I would be at university now.
Mixed 3rd/2nd If + past simple, would have + pp If I were more confident, I would have applied for that job.
Advanced Examples:
• If we had invested in renewable energy earlier, we wouldn't be facing this climate crisis now.
• If I didn't live in the city, I would have bought a car years ago.
• If she hadn't missed that flight, she would be sitting in the meeting right now.
Complex Passive with Modals

Grade 9 requires passive constructions with modal verbs to express possibility, obligation, and speculation.

Function Structure Example
Obligation/Advice should/ought to + be + pp The report should be submitted by Friday.
Possibility may/might/could + be + pp The problem could be solved with better funding.
Speculation (past) must/can't + have been + pp The theft must have been planned in advance.
Expectation (unfulfilled) should + have been + pp The issue should have been addressed sooner.
Common Error: Don't confuse active and passive voice in modal perfect constructions.
❌ The criminal must have caught. (Active meaning but passive construction needed)
✅ The criminal must have been caught by now.
Gerunds vs. Infinitives: Meaning Changes

At B2.3 level, you must control verbs where the meaning changes depending on the form that follows.

Verb + Gerund (-ing) + Infinitive (to)
forget Forget meeting him. (past memory) Forget to meet him. (future obligation)
remember Remember visiting Paris. (past experience) Remember to visit grandma. (don't forget)
stop Stop smoking. (quit the habit) Stop to smoke. (pause in order to smoke)
regret I regret telling him. (past action) I regret to inform you... (formal bad news)
try Try eating less sugar. (experiment) Try to eat less. (make an effort)
go on Go on talking. (continue) Go on to explain... (change to new subject)
Wish and Hope: Correct Patterns
Wish + Past Perfect: Regrets about the past
• I wish I had studied medicine. (but I didn't)
Wish + Past Simple: Present/future desires (unreal)
• I wish I knew the answer. (but I don't)
Hope + Present/Future: Real possibilities
• I hope you feel better soon.
• I hope it doesn't rain tomorrow.
Common Error: Do not use "wish" + "would" for your own actions when you have control:
❌ I wish I would study more.
✅ I wish I studied more. / I wish I had studied more.
Modal Perfect: Should/Must/Might/Could + Perfect Infinitive
Structure Meaning Example
should have + pp Regret/criticism (didn't happen) You should have told me earlier!
must have + pp Strong deduction about past He must have forgotten the meeting.
might have + pp Possibility in past She might have missed the train.
could have + pp Past possibility/ability unused We could have won if we'd tried harder.
can't have + pp Negative deduction He can't have stolen it — he wasn't there.
Discourse Connectors and Cohesive Devices

At Grade 9, you must use sophisticated connectors to organize discourse and recover from interruptions.

Function Expressions
Recapping As I was saying..., To return to my point..., Where was I?
Adding information Furthermore, Moreover, In addition to that, What's more
Contrasting Nevertheless, However, On the other hand, Having said that
Giving examples For instance, To illustrate this, A case in point is
Concluding To sum up, All in all, Taking everything into account
Hedging To some extent, Up to a point, In a way, Sort of
Handling Interruptions:
"That's an interesting point. As I was saying earlier, the main issue is..."
"To return to what I was saying, we need to consider..."
"Where was I? Oh yes, I was talking about..."

How to Prepare the Grade 9 Topic Discussion

Requirements: At Grade 9, you must prepare a Topic Form with six discussion points. The examiner will select from these points to facilitate a spontaneous, authentic discussion. You must NOT read from a script — this is a conversation, not a presentation.

Step 1: Choose an Appropriate Topic

Your topic should be:

  • Personal and meaningful — something you genuinely care about
  • Complex enough to allow abstract discussion and evaluation
  • NOT from the subject area list (Dreams, Crime, Technology, Habits, Environment, Design)
  • Capable of generating six distinct discussion points

Good topics: "My transition from high school to university," "How learning guitar changed my perspective on discipline," "The impact of moving countries on my identity"

Avoid: "My favorite hobby" (too simple), "My family" (too descriptive), "My country" (too general)

Step 2: Create Your Topic Form

Prepare a form with six discussion points. These should not be questions, but areas for exploration.

Sample Topic Form: "My Decision to Become Vegan"
Initial skepticism and misconceptions about plant-based diets
The documentary that triggered my research
Challenges faced when changing eating habits
How this choice affected my social relationships
Evaluating the environmental vs. health benefits debate
Regrets about not making this change earlier
Important: The examiner will choose which points to discuss. You must be prepared to talk about ALL six points spontaneously. Do not write full sentences — just key words or short phrases.

Step 3: Structure Your Discussion

Opening (30 seconds):

  • Briefly introduce your topic
  • Explain why you chose it
  • Hand the Topic Form to the examiner

Development (4-5 minutes):

  • Respond to the examiner's questions about your points
  • Expand with examples, anecdotes, and reflections
  • Use language functions: hypothesise, evaluate, express regrets
  • Ask the examiner at least one question about your topic
  • Handle interruptions by recapping: "As I was saying..."

Step 4: Practice Language Functions

Ensure you can naturally use:

  • Abstract ideas: "This represents a fundamental shift in..."
  • Hypothesising: "If I hadn't made that choice, I would probably..."
  • Evaluating: "Looking back, I should have..." / "In retrospect, I might have..."
  • Paraphrasing: "What I mean is..." / "In other words..."
  • Wishes: "I wish I had realised sooner that..."

Step 5: Interactive Skills

  • Maintain eye contact — don't look at notes
  • Use cohesive devices to link your ideas
  • If interrupted, acknowledge and return to your point
  • Ask the examiner a genuine question to engage them
  • Show enthusiasm and personal investment in the topic
Sample Opening:
"I'd like to talk about my experience of moving from a small village to a big city for university. This was a pivotal moment for me because it challenged many assumptions I'd grown up with. I've prepared six aspects of this transition that I'd like to discuss with you."
Language for Evaluating and Hypothesising
Function Useful Phrases
Evaluating past actions "In retrospect...", "Looking back...", "With hindsight...", "I now realise..."
Hypothesising (present) "Suppose...", "Imagine...", "If that were the case...", "Assuming that..."
Hypothesising (past) "If I had known...", "Had I realised...", "If things had been different..."
Expressing assumptions "I assume that...", "I take it that...", "Presumably...", "I would imagine..."
Paraphrasing "What I'm trying to say is...", "To put it another way...", "What I mean is..."

Examiner Assessment Focus:
  • Range and accuracy of grammatical structures
  • Ability to express abstract ideas clearly
  • Discourse management and turn-taking
  • Interactive communication skills
  • Pronunciation, stress, and intonation

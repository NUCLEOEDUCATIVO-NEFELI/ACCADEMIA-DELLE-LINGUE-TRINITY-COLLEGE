ISE IV (C2) Examination Overview
Important: ISE IV has a different format from ISE I-III. It consists of three separate components taken at different times.
ISE IV Structure: Unlike ISE I-III, ISE IV is not taken in a single session. The three components are taken separately.
Component 1: The Portfolio

Timing: Completed over 6-12 weeks before the Interview

Content: Three writing tasks (one from each section: Correspondence, Factual Writing, Critical/Analytical Writing)

Word limits: 300-350 words (Section 1), 220-250 words (Section 2), 250-300 words (Section 3)

Component 2: Controlled Written Exam

Timing: 3 hours

Content: Two Reading into Writing tasks + One Extended Writing task

Word limits: 250-300 words each (Tasks 1-2), 300-350 words (Task 3)

Component 3: The Interview

Timing: 25 minutes

Content: 5 tasks - Topic Presentation, Topic Discussion, Interactive Task, Listening Task, Conversation Task

Focus: Sophisticated argumentation, abstract concepts, managing discourse

Task 1: Reading into Writing (50 minutes)

Instructions: Read the two texts and write an essay (250-300 words) synthesizing the information to answer the question. Use your own words.

The Epistemology of Artificial Intelligence

Text A — Philosophy of Mind Perspective
The question of whether large language models can be said to "know" anything in a meaningful sense strikes at the heart of epistemological debates that have preoccupied philosophers for centuries. Traditional definitions of knowledge require justified true belief, yet the justification condition presupposes a knower capable of metacognitive reflection—an attribute conspicuously absent in statistical models. These systems process tokens without understanding referents; they manipulate symbols without grasping meaning. The Chinese Room argument, articulated by Searle, gains renewed relevance: syntax is not semantics, and pattern matching is not comprehension. Yet defenders of machine epistemology counter that human cognition itself may be a form of sophisticated statistical inference, rendering the distinction less categorical than it appears. The resolution may lie in recognizing that knowledge is not a binary property but a spectrum of capacities—representation, reasoning, and recursive self-improvement—each present in degrees across biological and artificial systems.
Text B — Cognitive Science Perspective
Contemporary cognitive science suggests that the boundary between human and machine knowledge is more permeable than traditional epistemology assumes. Embodied cognition theory posits that understanding emerges from sensorimotor interaction with the environment—a condition current AI lacks. However, the extended mind hypothesis proposes that cognitive processes routinely incorporate external tools, from notebooks to smartphones, blurring the skin-skull boundary of the knower. If cognition is already hybrid, the integration of AI systems into epistemic practices may represent continuity rather than rupture. Empirical studies demonstrate that human-AI collaborative reasoning often outperforms either alone, suggesting that knowledge production is increasingly distributed across biological and artificial substrates. The pertinent question may not be whether AI knows, but how human epistemic practices are being reconfigured by these technologies, and what normative frameworks should govern their integration into domains previously reserved for human judgment—from scientific discovery to judicial reasoning.
Writing Task

Write an essay (250-300 words) discussing whether artificial intelligence systems can be said to possess knowledge. Evaluate the arguments presented in both texts and develop your own position on the epistemological status of AI.

  • Critical evaluation of competing epistemological frameworks
  • Development of nuanced, non-binary position
  • Use of metacognitive and epistemological terminology
  • Complex sentence structures with embedded clauses

Task 2: Reading into Writing (50 minutes)

Instructions: Read the two texts and write a report (250-300 words) evaluating the proposals and reaching a justified conclusion.

Global Governance in an Age of Crisis

Text A — Cosmopolitan Democracy
The inadequacy of Westphalian sovereignty has become manifest in the face of transnational challenges that respect no borders—climate change, pandemic disease, financial contagion, and cyber warfare. The cosmopolitan democracy model, articulated by scholars such as Held and Archibugi, proposes multilayered governance in which authority is distributed across local, national, regional, and global institutions according to the principle of subsidiarity. Global institutions would be empowered to address genuinely global problems, but only with democratic accountability through cosmopolitan citizenship and transnational deliberative mechanisms. Critics contend that this model underestimates the persistence of national identity and the democratic deficit inherent in supranational bodies. However, proponents argue that the alternative—unilateralism and great power competition—has proven incapable of addressing collective action problems that demand coordinated responses. The European Union, despite its imperfections, offers a partial template for how sovereignty can be pooled without being surrendered entirely.
Text B — Realist Critique
The cosmopolitan vision, however noble, fundamentally misunderstands the nature of international politics. States remain the primary locus of identity, loyalty, and democratic legitimacy. The attempt to transcend sovereignty through global governance mechanisms risks creating unaccountable technocratic structures that lack the solidarity necessary for democratic deliberation. The European Union's democratic deficit—where unelected bureaucrats make decisions affecting millions—exemplifies this danger rather than offering a solution. Furthermore, the assumption that global problems require global solutions ignores the potential for competitive polycentrism, where diverse approaches generate innovation and resilience. Climate action may be more effectively pursued through climate clubs of willing states than through universal but unenforceable agreements. The pandemic demonstrated that nation-states retained both the capacity and legitimacy to take decisive action when international organizations floundered. Rather than aspiring to cosmopolitan governance, we should strengthen the capacity of states to cooperate through flexible, issue-specific arrangements that preserve democratic accountability.
Writing Task

Write a report (250-300 words) evaluating the cosmopolitan democracy proposal and the realist critique. Assess which approach better addresses contemporary global challenges, providing reasoned justification for your evaluation.

Component 3: The Interview (25 minutes) 5 tasks
ISE IV (C2) Interview: Sophisticated argumentation | Abstract conceptual discussion | Full discourse management | Metalinguistic awareness
Interview Structure: Five integrated tasks assessing speaking and listening. The candidate must demonstrate ability to manage abstract discussion with minimal examiner support.
Task 1: Formal Topic Presentation (up to 5 minutes)

Your Task: Present a complex topic with high linguistic formality. Present a clear argument with effective logical structure. Help the examiner notice and remember significant points. Conclude by inviting questions and comments.

Preparation Requirements:
  • Prepare a complex topic of your choice before the exam
  • May bring brief notes or a mind map (not a script)
  • Topic should allow for sophisticated, abstract discussion
  • Must demonstrate high degree of linguistic formality
Sample Topic Areas:
  • The ethical implications of gene editing technologies
  • Post-colonial perspectives on global economic systems
  • The philosophy of consciousness and artificial intelligence
  • Climate justice and intergenerational ethics
  • The future of democratic institutions in the digital age
  • Epistemological challenges in the post-truth era
Language Focus: Formal register, sophisticated cohesive devices, rhetorical strategies, metadiscourse (signposting, emphasizing, summarizing)
Task 2: Topic Discussion (up to 5 minutes)

Your Task: Initiate and maintain discussion of your presentation topic. Actively seek ways to engage the examiner in meaningful exchange. Take full responsibility for maintaining the discussion. Respond effectively to various conversational gambits and handle in-depth questioning.

Examiner Questions May Include:
  • "You mentioned [X]—could you elaborate on how that interacts with [Y]?"
  • "What would be the strongest counterargument to your position?"
  • "How has your thinking on this evolved during your research?"
  • "Can you apply this theoretical framework to a specific current event?"
  • "How does your position account for [apparent contradiction]?"
Language Focus: Hedging and qualification, reformulating for clarity, handling challenges diplomatically, expanding and developing ideas spontaneously
Task 3: Interactive Task (up to 5 minutes)

Scenario: The examiner presents a complex dilemma or opinion. You must control and sustain the discussion through questioning and comment.

Sample Prompt:
"A prestigious university is considering implementing an AI detection system to identify students using language models to complete assignments. The system would analyze writing style patterns and flag inconsistencies. However, there are concerns about false positives, privacy, and whether this fundamentally misunderstands how students should use AI tools."
Your Responsibilities:
  • Ask questions to explore the examiner's perspective
  • Present and defend your own viewpoint
  • Encourage the examiner's collaboration
  • Direct the interaction toward a conclusion
  • Do NOT wait for the examiner to lead
Key Language Functions: Asserting and denying propositions, softening and downplaying claims, contradicting politely, implying without stating directly, affirming with qualification
Task 4: Listening Task (up to 3 minutes)

Procedure: The examiner plays a recording twice. First listening: gist. Second listening: detail (you may take notes).

Sample Recording Script:

"The relationship between technology and human agency has undergone a fundamental reconceptualization. Where early internet theorists celebrated the democratizing potential of digital platforms, contemporary analysis increasingly focuses on what Shoshana Zuboff terms 'surveillance capitalism'—the extraction and commodification of behavioral data. This shift demands we reconsider traditional frameworks of consent and autonomy. However, technological determinism—the view that technology drives social change independently of human choice—remains problematic. Rather, we might conceptualize this as 'sociotechnical co-construction,' where technological affordances and social practices mutually constitute one another. The policy implications are significant: regulation targeting only technical systems without addressing underlying economic incentives may prove ineffective. Conversely, purely economic interventions may fail to account for how platform architectures shape behavior. What emerges is a need for 'holistic governance' that addresses code, commerce, and culture simultaneously."
Questions:
  1. Gist: In one or two sentences, what is the speaker's main argument?
  2. Detail: Explain the speaker's critique of both technological determinism and isolated regulatory approaches.
Skills: Processing academic register, identifying implicit critique, summarizing complex argumentation, inferring speaker stance

Assessment Summary

ISE IV is assessed through three separate components. Unlike lower levels, there is no single numerical score—each component receives a separate grade (Distinction, Merit, Pass, or Fail).

Component 1: Portfolio Assessment

Correspondence Task (Section 1):

  • Appropriate register for genre and audience
  • Effective management of interaction (forum) or relationship (emails/letter)
  • Coherent development of ideas across multiple turns or communications
  • Sophisticated use of formulaic language and conventions

Factual Writing Task (Section 2):

  • Objective, impersonal tone where appropriate
  • Clear organization with effective signposting
  • Critical evaluation and synthesis of information
  • Precise, field-appropriate terminology

Critical/Analytical Writing Task (Section 3):

  • Sophisticated argumentation with nuanced position
  • Effective handling of abstract concepts
  • Critical engagement with multiple perspectives
  • Evidence of independent thinking and intellectual maturity

Component 2: Controlled Written Exam

Tasks 1-2: Reading into Writing (250-300 words each)

  • Sophisticated synthesis of complex source materials
  • Critical evaluation of arguments and evidence
  • Development of coherent, well-supported position
  • Complete transformation of source material (no lifting)

Task 3: Extended Writing (300-350 words)

  • Independent, sophisticated argumentation
  • Engagement with abstract or philosophical questions
  • Evidence of wide reading and intellectual breadth
  • Rhetorical sophistication and precise lexical control

Component 3: Interview

Rating Criteria (all tasks):

  • Communicative Effectiveness: Full task fulfillment with sophisticated turn-taking and natural repair strategies
  • Interactive Listening: Complete understanding, interpretation of implicit meaning, immediate relevant response
  • Language Control: Comprehensive mastery, precise formulation, idiomatic usage, complex structures
  • Delivery: Fully intelligible, effective stress/intonation, fluent with minimal hesitation

Specific Task Requirements:

  • Task 1 (Presentation): High formality, rhetorical sophistication, clear argument structure
  • Task 2 (Discussion): Spontaneous development, handling of abstract questions, maintaining discourse
  • Task 3 (Interactive): Full control of interaction, sophisticated questioning, managing disagreement
  • Task 4 (Listening): Processing complex academic register, inferring stance, summarizing nuance
  • Task 5 (Conversation): Metalinguistic awareness, defending rhetorical choices, abstract discussion

ISE IV Grade Descriptors

Distinction: Exceptional performance demonstrating mastery beyond minimum C2 requirements
Merit: Strong performance fully meeting C2 standards with some evidence of sophistication
Pass: Adequate performance meeting minimum C2 requirements
Fail: Performance below C2 standard

C2 Language Features Expected

Grammar: Mixed conditionals, passive voice, cleft sentences, participle clauses, subjunctive mood, inversion
Discourse: Notwithstanding, albeit, conversely, by the same token, inasmuch as, ergo, prima facie
Vocabulary: Idiomatic usage, collocations, connotation awareness, field-specific terminology, academic phrasing
Pragmatics: Sophisticated hedging, face-threatening act mitigation, implicature

Key Differences from ISE III

  • Longer word counts (250-350 vs 200-230 words)
  • Three components taken separately, not single exam session
  • Portfolio prepared over 6-12 weeks
  • More abstract, philosophical topics
  • Greater emphasis on independent thinking
  • No "Task 1/2" reading comprehension—only reading-into-writing
  • Interview includes formal presentation and portfolio discussion

What is the ISE III Portfolio?

The Portfolio is one of three components of the Trinity ISE III (C1) examination. It consists of three pieces of written work that you prepare before the exam date, allowing you to use reference materials, dictionaries, and receive feedback during the preparation process.

Key Features of the Portfolio:
  • Prepared in advance: You complete these tasks before the exam day
  • Reference materials allowed: Use dictionaries, grammar books, spell-checks, and word processors
  • Teacher feedback permitted: One round of feedback per task using the official form
  • Three sections required: One task from each of the three categories
  • Word count: 200-230 words per task at C1 level
  • Submitted before interview: Must be handed in at least 24 hours before the examiner arrives
Important: The Portfolio is discussed during your Speaking & Listening interview. The examiner will ask you about the content and/or the process of writing your portfolio tasks, so be prepared to discuss your work in detail.

Portfolio Structure & Requirements
Mandatory Requirements:
  • Three tasks total: One from each section (Correspondence, Factual, Creative)
  • Same level only: All tasks must be ISE III (C1) level - do not mix levels
  • Current task list: Use only tasks from the official Trinity list for your exam period
  • Word count: 200-230 words per task (stated at the end of each piece)
  • Task numbers: Write the task question number on each piece of work

Section 1: Correspondence (200-230 words)
Task Overview: Write formal or informal correspondence demonstrating your ability to communicate effectively with different audiences using appropriate conventions, tone, and language at C1 level.

C1 Correspondence Requirements:

  • Highly formal or appropriately informal register
  • Complex sentence structures
  • Sophisticated vocabulary and professional tone
  • Clear purpose and well-organised content
  • Appropriate opening and closing conventions
  • Ability to negotiate, persuade, or discuss complex matters
Sample Task:
"You recently read an article in an academic journal that you disagree with. The editor has invited readers to respond. Write a formal letter (200-230 words) to the editor:
  • summarising the main argument you disagree with
  • presenting counterarguments with evidence
  • suggesting what further research is needed"

Formal Letter Structure at C1:

1 Sender's Address (if required by task)
2 Date
3 Recipient's Address / Salutation (Dear Sir/Madam, Dear Editor, Dear Dr. Smith)
4 Opening Paragraph - State purpose clearly and formally
5 Body Paragraphs - Develop points with sophisticated language
6 Closing Paragraph - Conclude with forward-looking statement
7 Sign-off (Yours faithfully/sincerely, + full name)
C1 Language for Correspondence:
  • Opening: "I am writing with regard to..." / "I wish to express my concern regarding..."
  • Presenting arguments: "It could be argued that..." / "The evidence suggests..."
  • Conceding: "While I acknowledge that..., it is nevertheless the case that..."
  • Persuading: "I would urge you to consider..." / "It is imperative that..."
  • Closing: "I look forward to your response" / "I trust this matter will receive due consideration"
Section 2: Factual Writing (200-230 words)
Task Overview: Write factual pieces such as reports, articles, reviews, or discursive essays. Demonstrate your ability to convey information objectively, structure arguments logically, and use formal academic language at C1 level.

Genres in Section 2:

1. Report

Objective, analytical tone with clear sections. Includes introduction, findings, analysis, and recommendations.

Example: "Write a report on the impact of social media on young people's mental health."

2. Article

Engaging title, sophisticated vocabulary, rhetorical devices, authoritative but accessible tone.

Example: "Write an article for a business magazine about the gig economy."

3. Review

Sophisticated evaluation, nuanced opinion, cultural context, balanced critique with recommendation.

Example: "Review a recent documentary about climate change, evaluating its effectiveness."

4. Discursive Essay

Balanced discussion of different viewpoints, objective tone, weighing evidence, conclusion.

Example: "Discuss the extent to which technology has improved education."

C1 Factual Writing Features:
  • Nominalisation: "The implementation of the policy resulted in..."
  • Passive voice: "It has been suggested that..." / "Evidence indicates that..."
  • Hedging language: "It appears that..." / "This may indicate..." / "Potentially..."
  • Discourse markers: "Furthermore," "Conversely," "Notwithstanding," "Consequently"
  • Complex conditionals: "Were this trend to continue, the implications would be..."
Sample Structure for a Report:

Title: The Impact of Remote Working on Employee Productivity

Introduction (30-35 words): Purpose and scope of the report
Current Situation (50-60 words): Overview of remote working trends
Analysis (50-60 words): Evidence of productivity impacts, both positive and negative
Recommendations (40-50 words): Practical suggestions for employers
Conclusion (30-35 words): Summary of key findings
Section 3: Creative & Descriptive Writing (200-230 words)
Task Overview: Write creative and descriptive pieces including stories, narratives, and descriptive essays. Demonstrate your ability to use imaginative language, create atmosphere, develop character, and engage the reader at C1 level.

C1 Creative Writing Requirements:

  • Sophisticated descriptive vocabulary and figurative language
  • Complex narrative structures and pacing
  • Psychological depth and character development
  • Thematic complexity (ambition, identity, freedom, etc.)
  • Atmospheric and sensory detail
  • Subtle emotional resonance
Sample Task:
"Write a story (200-230 words) that explores the theme of ambition and its consequences. Your story should:
  • include a character whose ambition leads to unexpected results
  • use description to convey the character's emotional state
  • end with a reflection on the nature of success"

C1 Creative Techniques:

Technique C1 Application Example
Figurative Language Sophisticated metaphors and symbolism "The silence was a heavy blanket, smothering her ambitions"
Varied Syntax Inversion for emphasis and effect "Never had she anticipated such a profound transformation"
Sensory Detail Abstract and emotional sensory description "The air tasted of regret and unfulfilled promises"
Internal Monologue Complex psychological insight "Had he been wrong to sacrifice everything for recognition?"
Atmospheric Setting Setting as reflection of theme "The decaying mansion mirrored his deteriorating values"
Common Themes at ISE III:
  • Ambition and its costs
  • Identity and self-discovery
  • Freedom vs. constraint
  • The individual vs. society
  • Memory and nostalgia
  • Loss and transformation
Portfolio Rules & Feedback Process
Critical Rules - Read Carefully:
  • Original work only: The portfolio must contain your own work and no one else's
  • No external corrections: Nobody except you personally should correct, add to, or remove anything from your tasks
  • Teacher feedback limited: Teachers may give only ONE piece of feedback per task using the official Student Portfolio Feedback Form
  • No specific corrections: Teachers must not correct or comment upon specific points of language or grammar
  • No writing on drafts: Teachers must not write on any draft, whether preparatory or final
  • Plagiarism warning: Tasks considered plagiarised or cheated will be marked as 'Ungraded'

The Feedback Process:

1 First Draft

You write your first draft using reference materials as needed.

2 Teacher Review

Teacher completes ONE Student Portfolio Feedback Form per task, ticking relevant boxes about task fulfilment, organisation, and language.

3 Revision

You revise your work based on the general feedback provided. You may use reference materials again.

4 Final Version

You submit the final version with the feedback form attached.

Permitted Resources:
  • Dictionaries (monolingual and bilingual)
  • Grammar reference books
  • Spell-check and grammar-check software
  • Word processing packages
  • The internet for research (but not for copying text)

Submission Requirements:

  • Present in the colour-coded plastic wallet provided by Trinity
  • Include completed Portfolio Cover Sheet
  • Attach Student Portfolio Feedback Form for each task
  • Write task question number on each piece of work
  • Write word count at the end of each task
  • Submit to teacher at least 24 hours before examiner's arrival
  • No access to portfolio from submission until after examiner's departure
Portfolio Discussion in the Interview

During your Speaking & Listening interview, the examiner will discuss your Portfolio with you. Be prepared to:

  • Explain why you chose particular tasks
  • Discuss your writing process and challenges
  • Talk about how you incorporated feedback
  • Explain your research or preparation for factual tasks
  • Discuss themes and ideas in your creative writing
  • Reflect on what you learned from the process
Sample Interview Questions about Portfolio:
  • "Why did you choose this particular task from Section 1?"
  • "What was the most challenging aspect of writing your report?"
  • "How did you research the topic for your factual writing?"
  • "What feedback did your teacher give you, and how did you respond to it?"
  • "Where did you get the idea for your creative writing piece?"
  • "If you were to rewrite one of your tasks, what would you change?"

Writing Practice Activity

‚úçÔ∏è Practice Task: Plan a Portfolio Piece

Task: You are planning to write a formal letter to a local government official about traffic congestion in your area.

Plan your letter by answering these questions:

  1. What is the purpose of your letter? (complaint, suggestion, request for information?)
  2. What are three specific points you will include?
  3. What sophisticated vocabulary could you use? (e.g., "mitigate," "congestion," "infrastructure")
  4. How will you structure your paragraphs?
  5. What formal opening and closing will you use?
Sample Plan Structure:
Opening: State purpose formally
Para 1: Describe current situation with specific examples
Para 2: Present proposed solutions with justification
Para 3: Request specific action and offer further cooperation
Closing: Formal sign-off with full name

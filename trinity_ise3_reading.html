ISE III Reading Exam Overview

The Reading component of ISE III (C1) consists of two tasks to be completed in 40 minutes. The exam tests your ability to understand complex, demanding texts and identify implicit meaning at C1 level.

Key Features at C1 Level:
  • Longer texts: 700 words (vs. 500 words at B2)
  • Complex vocabulary: Abstract concepts, academic language, idiomatic expressions
  • Implicit meaning: Understanding what is suggested but not directly stated
  • Opinion and attitude: Identifying tone, bias, and perspective
  • Text structure: Following complex argumentation and discourse
  • Synthesis: Comparing and contrasting multiple sources
Important: The reading texts at ISE III relate to the 12 subject areas. Familiarity with these topics will help you understand context and vocabulary.

C1 Reading Skills Required

At ISE III (C1), you must demonstrate the following reading skills:

1 Reading for Gist

Understanding the main idea, purpose, and overall message of complex texts.

2 Reading for Detail

Identifying specific information, facts, and supporting evidence.

3 Reading for Inference

Understanding implied meaning, reading between the lines, recognising what is suggested but not explicitly stated.

4 Understanding Opinion and Attitude

Identifying the writer's stance, tone, bias, and evaluating their perspective.

5 Understanding Text Structure

Following complex argumentation, recognising discourse markers, understanding how ideas are organised and developed.

6 Synthesising Information

Comparing and contrasting information from multiple sources, identifying agreement and contradiction.

Task 1: Long Reading (20 minutes)
Task Overview: Read one complex text of approximately 700 words (usually divided into 5 paragraphs) and answer 15 questions testing different reading skills at C1 level.

Question Types (15 questions total):

1 Questions 1-5: Title Matching

Match the correct title to each of the 5 paragraphs. You have 6 titles to choose from (one is a distractor).

Example: Paragraph discusses economic inequality solutions. Correct title: "Addressing the Wealth Gap" (not "Global Economic Trends")

Skill tested: Reading for gist, main ideas, and understanding paragraph focus

2 Questions 6-10: True Statements Selection

Choose 5 true statements from a list of 8. Three statements are false according to the text.

Example statements:
  • "Economic inequality has decreased in developed nations" (True/False based on text)
  • "The author supports progressive taxation policies" (True/False based on text)
  • "Wealth gaps are primarily caused by educational disparities" (True/False based on text)

Skill tested: Reading for specific factual information and inference

3 Questions 11-15: Sentence Completion

Complete sentences with exact words from the text (maximum 3 words per gap).

Example: "The author suggests that _______ policies are necessary to address climate change."
Answer from text: "comprehensive environmental" (exact words from the passage)

Skill tested: Reading for detail and precise information

C1 Reading Strategies for Task 1:
  • Skim first: Read quickly for overall structure and main idea
  • Identify tone and attitude: Is the author critical, supportive, neutral, or ironic?
  • Recognise implicit meaning: What is suggested but not directly stated?
  • Track argument development: How does the writer build their case?
  • Watch for hedging language: "may," "could," "potentially," "arguably" indicate uncertainty
  • Understand discourse markers: "however," "furthermore," "consequently," "nevertheless"
  • Note paragraph focus: Each paragraph usually has one main idea
Common Mistakes to Avoid:
  • Matching titles based on similar words rather than meaning
  • Using your own knowledge instead of the text (true/false questions)
  • Changing the form of words in sentence completion (must be exact)
  • Spending too long on one question - move on and return
  • Not checking that answers make grammatical sense
Sample Task 1 Layout

Text: The Future of Remote Work

Paragraph A
The shift towards remote working, accelerated by global events, has fundamentally transformed the modern workplace. What began as a temporary necessity has evolved into a permanent feature of corporate culture, with profound implications for productivity, employee wellbeing, and urban development...

Paragraph B
Proponents argue that flexibility enhances productivity by eliminating commuting time and allowing employees to work during their most productive hours. However, critics contend that the blurring of boundaries between professional and personal life has led to increased burnout and decreased job satisfaction...

Paragraph C
The economic implications extend beyond individual companies. Commercial real estate markets in major cities have experienced significant declines in demand, while suburban and rural areas have seen property values surge as workers relocate...

Paragraph D
From an environmental perspective, reduced commuting has contributed to lower carbon emissions in urban centres. Nevertheless, the increased energy consumption of home offices and the rise in domestic deliveries partially offset these gains...

Paragraph E
Ultimately, the future of work likely lies in hybrid models that combine the benefits of both approaches. Organisations must develop policies that maximise flexibility while maintaining corporate culture and employee connection...

Sample Questions:

1-5: Match titles (a-f) to paragraphs A-E. One title is extra.

6-10: Choose 5 true statements from 8 options.

11-15: Complete sentences with exact words from the text (max 3 words).

Task 2: Multi-text Reading (20 minutes)
Task Overview: Read 4 short texts (approximately 700 words total) on the same theme. One text may be an infographic (diagram, table, or chart with text). Answer 15 questions.
Important: The information in these texts is used for Task 3 (Reading into Writing) in the Written Exam. Pay attention to facts, statistics, opinions, and examples you can use in your writing.

Question Types (15 questions total):

1 Questions 16-20: Text Matching

Match descriptions to the correct text (A, B, C, or D). Tests understanding of main idea or purpose of each text.

Example: "Which text presents statistical evidence?" ‚Üí Match to correct letter
Example: "Which text expresses a personal opinion?" ‚Üí Match to correct letter

Skill tested: Understanding purpose, tone, and content of different texts

2 Questions 21-25: True Statements Selection

Choose 5 true statements from 8 options based on information across all four texts.

Strategy: You may need to synthesise information from multiple texts. One statement might be true based on combining Text A and Text C.

Skill tested: Reading for specific information across multiple sources

3 Questions 26-30: Summary Notes Completion

Complete summary notes using exact words from the texts (maximum 3 words per gap). The notes synthesise key information from all four texts.

Example:
"Advantages of remote working:
‚Ä¢ Increased __________ (Text A)
‚Ä¢ Reduced __________ (Text B)
‚Ä¢ Improved __________ (Text C)"

Skill tested: Synthesising information from multiple texts, reading for detail

C1 Strategies for Multi-text Reading:
  • Skim all texts first: Get an overview of the different perspectives
  • Identify text types: Is it an article, statistics, opinion piece, or report?
  • Note the source: Academic journal? Newspaper? Blog? This affects tone and reliability
  • Look for agreement and contradiction: Do texts say the same thing or disagree?
  • Track information across texts: You may need to combine information from multiple sources
  • Read the questions carefully: Note whether they ask about specific texts or all texts

C1 Reading Strategies

Time Management (40 minutes total):

1 Task 1: Long Reading (20 minutes)
  • 2 minutes: Skim the text for gist and structure
  • 14 minutes: Answer questions 1-15
  • 4 minutes: Check answers, ensure sentence completion uses exact words
2 Task 2: Multi-text Reading (20 minutes)
  • 3 minutes: Skim all four texts
  • 14 minutes: Answer questions 16-30
  • 3 minutes: Check answers, verify text references

Step-by-Step Approach:

1 Read the Instructions Carefully

Note word limits (especially "maximum 3 words"), whether to use exact words from the text, and how many options to select.

2 Skim the Text(s)

Read quickly for main idea, structure, and tone. Don't worry about every word.

3 Analyse the Questions

Understand what each question is asking before you search for the answer.

4 Scan for Specific Information

Use key words from the question to locate relevant parts of the text.

5 Check Your Answers

Ensure sentence completion answers are grammatically correct and use exact words from the text.

Understanding C1 Level Vocabulary:
  • Abstract nouns: implementation, consideration, phenomenon, implications
  • Academic verbs: demonstrate, indicate, suggest, imply, acknowledge
  • Hedging language: arguably, potentially, seemingly, to some extent
  • Discourse markers: furthermore, nevertheless, consequently, in contrast
  • Idiomatic expressions: "the tip of the iceberg," "a double-edged sword"
  • Collocations: "raise awareness," "address issues," "implement policies"
Question Type Strategies

Title Matching (Questions 1-5):

  • Read the paragraph first, then look at the titles
  • Identify the main idea, not just a detail mentioned
  • Eliminate titles that are too broad or too narrow
  • Watch out for distractors that use similar words but different meanings
  • Check that your chosen title covers the whole paragraph

True Statements (Questions 6-10 & 21-25):

  • Read all 8 statements before checking the text
  • Look for specific evidence in the text - don't use your own knowledge
  • Be careful with extreme words: "always," "never," "all," "none" are often false
  • Watch for paraphrasing - the text may use different words for the same idea
  • Check that the statement matches the text exactly, not just partially

Sentence Completion (Questions 11-15 & 26-30):

  • Read the sentence carefully to understand what information is needed
  • Scan the text for key words from the sentence
  • Check the word limit (usually maximum 3 words)
  • Copy words exactly as they appear in the text - do not change form
  • Ensure your completed sentence makes grammatical sense
  • Check spelling (British or American, but be consistent)

Text Matching (Questions 16-20):

  • Identify the unique features of each text (statistics, opinion, history, etc.)
  • Note the source and tone of each text
  • Some questions may require synthesising information from multiple texts
  • Be careful - the same topic may appear in multiple texts but from different angles
Subject Areas for ISE III Reading

All reading texts relate to these 12 subject areas. Familiarity with vocabulary and concepts in these areas will help your comprehension.

1. Ambitions

Career goals, personal development, motivation, success and failure

2. Independence

Autonomy, self-reliance, leaving home, financial independence

3. Stereotypes

Gender roles, cultural assumptions, prejudice, challenging norms

4. Role Models

Influence, inspiration, leadership, celebrity culture, mentors

5. Competitiveness

Sports, business, education, cooperation vs. competition

6. Young People's Rights

Education, voting, participation, protection, voice

Journalism, social media, bias, fake news, influence

Marketing, consumerism, manipulation, regulation, ethics

Health, work-life balance, consumer choices, trends

10. The Arts

Culture, creativity, funding, accessibility, value

11. Rights of the Individual

Privacy, freedom of speech, equality, discrimination

12. Economic Issues

Inequality, globalisation, unemployment, sustainability

Reading Practice Activities

Develop your C1 reading skills with these practice activities:

üìñ Activity 1: Skimming Practice

Task: Find an article of approximately 700 words on a subject area topic (e.g., from The Guardian, BBC, or The Economist).

Steps:

  1. Give yourself 2 minutes to skim the text
  2. Close the article and write down:
    • The main topic
    • The writer's opinion/stance
    • Three key points
    • The overall structure
  3. Re-open the article and check your answers
üìñ Activity 2: Title Matching Practice

Task: Take a text with 4-5 clear paragraphs and create 6 possible titles (including one distractor).

Example paragraph:

"The proliferation of social media platforms has fundamentally altered how individuals consume information. Unlike traditional media, which operated through established editorial processes, social media enables anyone to disseminate content instantly. This democratisation of information has coincided with a marked decline in public trust of established news sources."

Possible titles:

  • Correct: "The Changing Landscape of Information Consumption"
  • Distractor: "The History of Traditional Media" (too narrow, focuses on wrong aspect)
üìñ Activity 3: Inference Practice

Task: Practice reading between the lines with these statements:

Text: "The policy, while well-intentioned, has yielded mixed results thus far."

What can you infer?

  • The policy has been completely successful? (No)
  • The writer is cautiously critical? (Yes)
  • Some aspects work, others don't? (Yes)

Practice with hedging language:

  • "arguably" = the writer thinks this but acknowledges others may disagree
  • "to some extent" = partially true, but not completely
  • "it could be suggested that" = the writer is presenting a possible interpretation
üìñ Activity 4: Vocabulary Building

ISE III Interview Overview (C1)

The Interview is the Speaking & Listening component of the Trinity ISE III (C1) exam. It is a one-to-one oral examination with a Trinity examiner lasting approximately 25 minutes. The interview assesses your ability to understand and produce spoken English at C1 level, demonstrating fluency, spontaneity, and sophisticated language use.

Key Differences from ISE II (B2):
  • Longer duration: 25 minutes vs. 20 minutes at B2
  • Formal presentation: 4-minute prepared presentation with handout (vs. discussion only at B2)
  • More abstract topics: Ambitions, stereotypes, economic issues, rights of the individual
  • C1 language functions: Deducing, inferring, hypothesising, staging, evaluating
  • Academic register: More formal, sophisticated vocabulary and structures
  • Independent listening: Longer, more complex recordings

Topic Task (8 minutes)
Task Overview: You prepare a topic before the exam and deliver a formal presentation (4 minutes) with a handout. This is followed by a discussion (4 minutes) where the examiner asks questions about your presentation. You must ask the examiner for their comments.

Preparing Your Topic:

  • Choose a discursive topic (arguments for/against, benefits/drawbacks, analysis of complex issues)
  • Prepare a formal presentation with clear structure (introduction, arguments, evaluation, conclusion)
  • Create a handout for the examiner with brief notes (main arguments, key statistics, sources)
  • Bring brief notes for yourself (not a full script)
  • Do NOT choose from the 12 subject areas (these are reserved for the Conversation task)
  • Choose something you are genuinely interested in and can discuss at length
Sample Topic: "To what extent has social media benefited society?"

Structure:
  1. Introduction: Define social media and its prevalence; state your approach
  2. Arguments for: Connectivity, information access, business opportunities, political engagement
  3. Arguments against: Privacy concerns, mental health impacts, misinformation, echo chambers
  4. Evaluation: Balanced view with evidence; consider context and nuance
  5. Conclusion: Personal stance with justification; future outlook

Handout content: Main arguments, key statistics, sources, thought-provoking questions

C1 Language Functions to Demonstrate:

Function Example Language
Developing and justifying arguments "The primary argument in favour is..." / "This is supported by the fact that..." / "Evidence suggests that..."
Summarising "To summarise the key points..." / "In essence, the evidence suggests..." / "Broadly speaking..."
Evaluating options/standpoints "From one perspective... however, conversely..." / "The merits of X are... whereas the drawbacks include..."
Deducing and inferring "This implies that..." / "It can be deduced from this that..." / "One might infer that..."
Staging "Having considered X, I shall now examine Y..." / "Turning to the next point..." / "Before concluding, I would like to address..."
Hypothesising "Were this situation to continue..." / "Should X occur, the consequences would be..." / "Had Y been implemented earlier..."
Key Strategies for Success:
  • Structure your presentation clearly with signposting language
  • Use formal, academic language throughout
  • At the end of your presentation, ask: "Do you have any questions or comments?"
  • Be prepared to defend your arguments and consider alternatives
  • Use your handout to guide the discussion
  • Elaborate on your points with examples and evidence
  • Show critical thinking by acknowledging complexity and nuance
Common Mistakes to Avoid:
  • Reading directly from a script (use notes only)
  • Choosing a topic from the 12 subject areas
  • Being too descriptive rather than discursive
  • Failing to ask for examiner comments
  • Not preparing a handout
  • Running over or under time (practice to get exactly 4 minutes)
Collaborative Task (4 minutes)
Task Overview: The examiner reads you a prompt describing a situation or problem. You must initiate, lead, and maintain the interaction by asking questions and making suggestions. This tests your ability to collaborate and take control of a conversation at C1 level.

Task Structure:

Phase 1 - Prompt: Examiner reads a scenario (e.g., ethical dilemma, complex problem, policy decision, personal conflict)

Phase 2 - Your Role: You must immediately start asking questions and making suggestions. Do NOT wait for the examiner to ask you questions.

Phase 3 - Development: Build on the examiner's responses, offer alternatives, negotiate, and work towards a conclusion.
Sample Prompt:
"A friend of mine has been offered a promotion at work which would mean moving to another country. She's excited about the opportunity but worried about leaving her elderly parents behind. She's asked me for advice but I'm not sure what to suggest."

Your opening (take control immediately):
"That's a complex situation with no easy answer. Shall we start by considering what factors she should weigh up? For instance, how serious are her parents' health concerns, and what support do they currently have?"

Useful Collaborative Language at C1:

Function Example Language
Opening "Shall we start by...?" / "Where shall we begin?" / "I'd suggest we first consider..." / "Perhaps we could look at..."
Making suggestions "How about...?" / "Why don't we...?" / "Perhaps we could..." / "One option might be to..."
Asking for opinions/information "What do you think about...?" / "Do you think...would work?" / "What's your view on...?" / "How do you feel about...?"
Responding "That's a good point" / "I hadn't thought of that" / "I'm not sure that would work because..." / "I see what you mean, but..."
Negotiating "We could compromise by..." / "What if we combined both ideas?" / "Let's settle on..." / "Perhaps a middle ground would be..."
Closing "So we've agreed on..." / "To summarise our decisions..." / "Shall we move on to...?" / "In conclusion, we think..."
Mandatory Requirements:
  • Take control immediately - don't wait for examiner questions
  • Ask multiple questions to gather information
  • Make suggestions and offer alternatives
  • Respond to examiner contributions
  • Work towards a conclusion or decision
  • Maintain the interaction throughout the 4 minutes
Tip: The examiner will play a role (e.g., the friend, a colleague, a committee member). Respond to them in character and keep the conversation moving forward.
Conversation Task (3 minutes)
Task Overview: The examiner chooses one subject area from the 12 listed areas and initiates conversation. You must respond with extended, sophisticated answers, ask questions, and maintain interaction at C1 level.

Key Requirements:

  • Respond to questions with extended, sophisticated answers (not just yes/no)
  • Demonstrate knowledge and critical thinking about the subject area
  • Ask the examiner questions about the subject area
  • Use C1 language functions (deducing, evaluating, hypothesising)
  • Show ability to discuss abstract concepts
  • Maintain natural turn-taking
Sample Conversation about "The Media":

Examiner: "How do you think the media influences public opinion?"
Candidate: "The media's influence is profound and multifaceted. On one hand, it serves an essential democratic function by informing citizens and facilitating public discourse. However, the concentration of media ownership raises significant concerns about bias and agenda-setting. Were we to have truly independent and diverse media ownership, public discourse might be considerably more balanced. What is your perspective on current media ownership regulations?"

Examiner: "I think there should be more diversity in media ownership."
Candidate: "I concur entirely. The current consolidation of media conglomerates inevitably limits viewpoint diversity and can lead to the marginalisation of minority perspectives. This implies that policy intervention may be necessary. Would you support stricter antitrust measures in the media sector, or do you favour alternative approaches?"

C1 Conversation Strategies:

1 Expand Your Answers

Don't just answer the question; explore implications, give examples, consider different perspectives.

2 Use Abstract Language

Discuss concepts like "democratic accountability," "social cohesion," "ethical implications."

3 Ask Sophisticated Questions

Return questions to the examiner that show critical thinking: "To what extent do you think...?" / "How might we address...?"

4 Hypothesise and Speculate

Use conditional and hypothetical language: "Were X to happen..." / "Should Y be implemented..."

Sample Questions by Subject Area:
  • Ambitions: "To what extent is personal ambition compatible with social responsibility?"
  • Stereotypes: "How do stereotypes perpetuate social inequality?"
  • Economic Issues: "What are the ethical implications of wealth inequality?"
  • Rights of the Individual: "Should individual privacy be sacrificed for national security?"
  • The Arts: "What role should the arts play in addressing social issues?"
Independent Listening Task (8 minutes)
Task Overview: Listen twice to a complex recording (approximately 3 minutes 30 seconds). First, report the gist (main idea). Second, answer specific questions from the examiner. Finally, engage in discussion about the content.

Task Structure:

Phase 1 - First Listening:
Listen for gist. The examiner will ask: "What is the speaker talking about?" or "What is the main topic?"

Your response: A comprehensive summary in 2-3 sentences showing understanding of the main argument and perspective.
Example: "The speaker is examining the ethical implications of artificial intelligence in healthcare, arguing that while AI offers significant benefits for diagnosis and treatment efficiency, it raises serious concerns about patient privacy, data security, and the potential dehumanisation of care."
Phase 2 - Second Listening:
Listen for specific information. Take detailed notes on main points, examples, and opinions.

After listening: Answer examiner's questions about specific content, opinions, and implications.
Example: "According to the speaker, what are the main advantages of AI in healthcare?" / "What concerns does the speaker raise about privacy?"
Phase 3 - Discussion:
The examiner asks follow-up questions requiring you to discuss the implications and relate to broader issues.

Example: "To what extent do you think the benefits of AI outweigh the risks?" / "How might these issues affect your country specifically?" / "What safeguards would you recommend?"

Assessment Criteria (Independent Listening):

Score Description
4 Identifies gist accurately, understands detailed information, responds to questions with sophistication, shows critical engagement
3 Identifies gist, understands most details, responds appropriately with minor hesitation
2 Identifies partial gist, understands some details, requires repetition of some questions
1 Identifies minimal information, struggles with detailed questions
0 No performance to assess or task not attempted
Note-Taking Tips:
  • Use abbreviations and symbols (&, +, ‚Üí, ‚Üë, ‚Üì, ?, =, ‚â†)
  • Write keywords only - don't try to write full sentences
  • Organise notes spatially (left for main ideas, right for details)
  • Leave space between points to add information on second listening
  • Notes are not assessed - only your oral responses count
  • Practice listening to academic lectures, podcasts, and discussions
Listening Topics at C1: Recordings may cover academic subjects, professional contexts, social issues, cultural topics, or ethical debates. The speaker may have an accent (British, American, Australian, etc.).
Assessment Criteria at ISE III

Your interview performance is assessed against four criteria. At C1 level, examiners expect sophisticated, fluent, and accurate language use.

1 Communicative Effectiveness

What examiners look for:

  • Task fulfilment - addressing all parts of tasks appropriately
  • Appropriate turn-taking and interaction management
  • Initiating and maintaining interaction
  • Elaborating and extending responses
  • Using C1 language functions (deducing, hypothesising, evaluating)
2 Interactive Listening

What examiners look for:

  • Understanding examiner questions and prompts
  • Responding appropriately to feedback
  • Asking for clarification when needed
  • Showing engagement with examiner contributions
  • Independent listening task performance
3 Language Control

What examiners look for:

  • Range and accuracy of grammar
  • Vocabulary precision and sophistication
  • C1 structures: inversion, subjunctive, advanced conditionals
  • Complex sentence structures
  • Appropriate register and tone
4 Delivery

What examiners look for:

  • Pronunciation and intonation
  • Fluency and natural pace
  • Clear articulation
  • Appropriate stress and rhythm
  • Minimal hesitation
C1 Grammar Structures to Demonstrate:
  • Inversion: "Never before has..." / "Rarely do we see..." / "Should you need..."
  • Subjunctive: "It is essential that he be..." / "I suggest that she go..."
  • Mixed conditionals: "If I had studied harder, I would be..."
  • Advanced modals: "The situation might have been avoided had..."
  • Cleft sentences: "What concerns me most is..." / "It was only when... that..."
  • Participle clauses: "Having considered all options..." / "Faced with this dilemma..."
Portfolio Discussion in the Interview

The examiner will discuss your Portfolio tasks with you. This is integrated throughout the interview and tests your ability to reflect on your own writing.

Sample Portfolio Discussion Questions:
  • "Why did you choose this particular task from Section 1?"
  • "What was the most challenging aspect of writing your report?"
  • "How did you research the topic for your factual writing?"
  • "What feedback did your teacher give you, and how did you respond to it?"
  • "Where did you get the idea for your creative writing piece?"
  • "If you were to rewrite one of your tasks, what would you change?"
  • "How did you ensure your formal letter had the appropriate tone?"
  • "What techniques did you use to make your article engaging?"
Preparation Tip: Review your Portfolio tasks before the interview. Be ready to discuss your writing process, challenges, research, and what you learned.

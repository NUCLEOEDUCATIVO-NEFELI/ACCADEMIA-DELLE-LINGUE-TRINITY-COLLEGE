ISE Foundation Interview Guide

Master the Topic Task, Conversation Task, and Independent Listening

ISE Foundation Speaking & Listening Exam Overview

The Speaking & Listening module of ISE Foundation is a 13-minute one-to-one interview with a Trinity examiner. It consists of three tasks that test your ability to communicate effectively in English at A2 level.

Key Facts:
  • Total time: 13 minutes (12 minutes exam + 1 minute administration)
  • Format: One-to-one interview with Trinity examiner
  • Tasks: 3 tasks (Topic, Conversation, Independent Listening)
  • Assessment: Speaking (16 marks) + Listening (8 marks)
  • Preparation: You must prepare a Topic Form before the exam
Task 1: Topic Task (4 minutes)

You discuss your prepared topic with the examiner. You bring a Topic Form with 4 points.

Skills: Giving personal information, describing routines, expressing likes/dislikes

Task 2: Conversation Task (2 minutes)

The examiner chooses one subject area and asks you questions. You must ask the examiner one question.

Skills: Responding to questions, asking for information, maintaining conversation

Task 3: Independent Listening (8 minutes)

You listen to two recordings and respond to questions in writing and orally.

Skills: Listening for gist and detail, note-taking, reporting information

Assessment Criteria

Your speaking is assessed on four criteria across all tasks:

Criterion What the Examiner Looks For Score
Communicative Effectiveness Task completion, appropriate responses, interaction management 0-4
Interactive Listening Understanding questions, relevant responses, speed of reaction 0-4
Language Control Grammar accuracy, vocabulary range, appropriate language functions 0-4
Delivery Fluency, pronunciation, intelligibility, pace 0-4
Pass Requirements:
  • Speaking: Total 8+ out of 16 (Pass), 12+ (Merit), 15+ (Distinction)
  • Listening: Total 4+ out of 8 (Pass), 5+ (Merit), 7+ (Distinction)
Language Functions at A2 Level

You must demonstrate these language functions during the exam:

  • Giving personal information about present and past circumstances
  • Describing routines (present simple)
  • Describing ability and inability (can/can't, could/couldn't)
  • Describing future plans (going to, would like to)
  • Expressing likes and dislikes
  • Describing people, objects and places
  • Expressing simple comparisons (comparative adjectives)
  • Asking for clarification (What do you mean?)
  • Responding to requests for clarification
  • Asking for information (simple questions)
Task 1: Topic Task (4 minutes)

Preparation: You must prepare a Topic Form with 4 discussion points before the exam

Duration: 4 minutes

Interaction: Examiner asks questions about your topic using your form

Requirement: You must ask the examiner at least one question about your topic

How to Choose Your Topic

Good topics are:
  • Something you know well and can talk about easily
  • Something you are genuinely interested in
  • Complex enough to discuss for 4 minutes
  • NOT from the Conversation subject areas (Holidays, Shopping, etc.)
Good Topic Examples:
• My favourite sport - swimming
• Learning to play the guitar
• My experience moving to a new city
• My hobby of photography
• My pet and how I care for it

The Topic Form

You must bring a completed Topic Form with 4 discussion points. The examiner will use this to ask you questions.

Sample Topic Form: "My Favourite Sport - Swimming"
1. When I started swimming and why I chose it
2. My training schedule and routine
3. A competition I participated in last year
4. My future swimming goals and plans
Tips for the Topic Form:
  • Write short phrases, not full sentences
  • Make sure points are different (not repetitive)
  • Include past, present, and future aspects
  • Prepare to talk about each point for about 1 minute
Sample Topic Task Dialogue

Examiner: Good morning. Can you tell me about your topic?

Candidate: Good morning. I'd like to talk about swimming, which is my favourite sport. I've prepared four points about my swimming experience.

Examiner: Great. Can you tell me when you started swimming and why?

Candidate: I started swimming when I was eight years old. My parents took me to the local pool and I really enjoyed it. I chose swimming because my older sister was already swimming and I wanted to be like her. At first, I was a bit scared of the deep water, but now I'm completely comfortable.

Examiner: Tell me about your training schedule.

Candidate: I train three times a week. On Mondays and Wednesdays, I go to the pool after school for one hour. On Saturday mornings, I have a longer training session for two hours. I usually swim about 2 kilometres each time. I can swim freestyle and backstroke very well, but I'm still learning butterfly.

Examiner: Can you tell me about a competition you participated in?

Candidate: Last year, I participated in a regional competition. It was my first big competition and I was very nervous. I competed in the 100-metre freestyle and I won a bronze medal! My time was 1 minute 15 seconds, which was my personal best. My family came to watch and they were very proud. It was an amazing experience.

Examiner: What are your future goals?

Candidate: In the future, I would like to compete at the national level. I'm going to train harder and improve my technique. Next year, I want to qualify for the junior championships. Eventually, I would like to become a swimming instructor and teach children to swim. Do you enjoy swimming, examiner?

Examiner: Yes, I do. Thank you. Now let's move on...

Why this is good:
  • Covers all 4 topic points with details and examples
  • Uses A2 language functions: past simple, present simple, going to, can/can't
  • Asks the examiner a question at the end
  • Natural, conversational style (not memorized)
  • Personal and specific details (times, dates, feelings)

Task 2: Conversation Task (2 minutes)

Duration: 2 minutes

Format: Examiner chooses one subject area and asks questions

Requirement: You must ask the examiner at least one question

Preparation: None needed (but practice the subject areas)

Subject Areas (Examiner chooses one)

Subject Area Possible Questions
Holidays Where did you go? Who with? What did you do?
Shopping Where do you shop? What did you buy last?
School and Work What subjects do you study? What job would you like?
Hobbies and Sports What do you do in your free time? Can you...?
Food What do you like eating? Can you cook?
Weekend and Seasonal Activities What do you do at weekends? What do you do in summer?
Important: You must ask the examiner a question in this task. Prepare some questions in advance for each subject area.
Sample Conversation Dialogues

Conversation about "Food"

Examiner: Let's talk about food. Can you cook?

Candidate: Yes, I can cook some simple dishes. I can make pasta and omelettes. I usually cook dinner for my family twice a week. My mother taught me when I was twelve.

Examiner: What did you have for breakfast this morning?

Candidate: I had toast with jam and a cup of tea. I usually don't eat much in the morning because I'm not very hungry. What about you, do you prefer sweet or savoury breakfast?

Examiner: I prefer savoury. What's your favourite meal?

Candidate: My favourite meal is dinner because my whole family eats together. We usually have meat or fish with vegetables. I really enjoy talking about our day while we eat.

Conversation about "Holidays"

Examiner: Tell me about your last holiday.

Candidate: Last summer, I went to the beach with my family. We stayed in a small hotel near the sea for one week. The weather was fantastic - sunny every day!

Examiner: What did you do there?

Candidate: We went swimming in the sea every morning. In the afternoons, we walked along the beach and ate ice cream. One day, we visited a nearby town. It was very relaxing. Do you prefer beach holidays or city holidays?

Examiner: I like beach holidays. Where would you like to go next?

Candidate: Next year, I would like to visit Italy because I love Italian food and I want to see Rome. I'm going to save money so I can stay for two weeks.

Questions to Ask the Examiner

You must ask the examiner a question. Here are some examples for each subject area:

Subject Area Questions You Can Ask
Holidays Do you like going on holiday? / Where do you usually go? / Do you prefer summer or winter holidays?
Shopping Do you like shopping? / Where do you usually shop? / Do you prefer shopping online or in shops?
School/Work What did you study at school? / Do you like your job? / What subject did you like best?
Hobbies/Sports Do you play any sports? / What do you do in your free time? / Can you swim/play tennis?
Food Do you like cooking? / What's your favourite food? / Do you prefer sweet or savoury food?
Weekend Activities What do you do at weekends? / Do you prefer relaxing or being active? / What did you do last weekend?
Task 3: Independent Listening (8 minutes)

Duration: 8 minutes total

Format: Two separate listening tasks

Recordings: You hear each recording twice

Assessment: Listening score (8 marks total)

Independent Listening Task 1

Format:
  • You see a map, plan, or diagram with pictures labelled A-F
  • You listen to someone describing locations
  • You match letters to numbered places on the map
  • One picture is NOT used (distraction)
  • You write your answers on paper
Example Task:
You see a map of an airport waiting room with 5 numbered circles (1-5) and 6 pictures (A-F).

You hear: "The information desk is on the left side of the room, next to the entrance."

You write the correct letter in circle 1 on your map.

Key language to listen for: left, right, opposite, next to, between, in the corner, in the centre

Independent Listening Task 2

Format:
  • You listen to a monologue (one person speaking)
  • Recording is approximately 1 minute long
  • You can take notes while listening (optional)
  • Examiner asks you 6 questions
  • You answer in one or two words or short phrases
Example:
You hear a woman talking about her weekend.

Examiner asks: "What did she do on Saturday morning?"
You answer: "Went shopping" or "She went shopping."

Examiner asks: "Who did she go with?"
You answer: "Her sister" or "With her sister."
Listening Strategies:
  • First listening: Get the general idea, don't worry about details
  • Second listening: Focus on specific information
  • For maps: Look at the layout before listening
  • For facts: Note down numbers, names, times, places
  • Don't worry if you miss something - you hear it twice!
Useful Language for Directions (Task 1)
Language Meaning
On the left/right Position on one side
Next to / Beside Very close, adjacent
Opposite / Across from Facing, on the other side
In the corner Where two walls meet
In the centre/middle Middle of the space
Between In the middle of two things
At the back/front Rear or entrance area
Note-Taking for Task 2

While listening the second time, quickly write down key words:

WHO: ________________
WHERE: ________________
WHEN: ________________
WHAT (activity): ________________
TRANSPORT: ________________
FEELING: ________________

This helps you answer the examiner's questions accurately!

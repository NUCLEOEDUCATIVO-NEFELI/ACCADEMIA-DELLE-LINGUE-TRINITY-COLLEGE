Grade 12 Mastery Grammar and Discourse

Hypothesizing, Evaluating Complex Arguments, and Eloquent Expression

Grade 12 Overview (C2 Level - Mastery)

Grade 12 of the Trinity College London GESE exam corresponds to level C2 (mastery/proficiency) of the CEFR. At this level, candidates demonstrate exceptional command of the language with precision, nuance, and sophistication in complex academic and professional contexts.

Key Features of Grade 12:
  • Duration: 25 minutes (fully candidate-led discussion)
  • Topic Presentation: Formal presentation on sophisticated discursive topic (5 minutes)
  • Topic Discussion: Handling abstract concepts and defending complex arguments (5 minutes)
  • Interactive Task: Sophisticated questioning and discussion management (5 minutes)
  • Listening Task: Inferring attitude, persuading, and third-party referencing (3 minutes)
  • Conversation: Two subject areas from selected lists (6 minutes)
C2 Language Functions:
  • Hypothesizing and speculating with precision
  • Evaluating and challenging complex arguments
  • Identifying and exploiting ambiguity
  • Distinguishing between fact and opinion elegantly
  • Expressing subtle distinctions of meaning
  • Third-party referencing (reporting others' views)
  • Emphasizing and highlighting key points
Exam Tip: At Grade 12, you must demonstrate complete linguistic mastery. You are expected to handle abstract, hypothetical, and philosophical discussions with ease, using language that is both precise and elegant.

Third-Party Referencing

At C2 level, you must demonstrate the ability to report, attribute, and distance yourself from others' opinions elegantly. This involves sophisticated reporting verbs and complex structures.

Structure Function Example
It has been suggested that... Impersonal attribution It has been suggested that economic factors are paramount.
According to + source... Direct attribution According to recent studies, the trend is accelerating.
X takes the view that... Attributing specific positions Smith takes the view that traditional methods are obsolete.
The prevailing wisdom holds that... Reporting consensus The prevailing wisdom holds that early intervention is crucial.
Critics argue/maintain/contend... Reporting opposition Critics argue that this approach lacks empirical support.
Proponents claim/assert... Reporting support Proponents assert that the benefits outweigh the risks.
Advanced Third-Party Referencing:
• It is widely held that climate change represents the greatest challenge of our time.
• Opponents of this view maintain that economic growth should take precedence.
• As Jones (2023) cogently argues, the data does not support such conclusions.
• Common perception would have it that technology improves efficiency, yet the evidence is mixed.
Complex Hypothesizing and Speculation

C2 level requires sophisticated expression of hypothetical scenarios, including impossible pasts, counterfactual presents, and theoretical futures.

Mixed Conditionals (Advanced):
• If he were to have accepted the position, the company would be in a different position today.
• Had we known then what we know now, our approach would have been radically different.
• Were it not for his intervention, the project would have collapsed.
Advanced Speculative Structures:
• Supposing (that) the theory proves correct, we would need to revise our understanding.
• Imagine a world where energy is freely available.
• Even if we were to accept that premise, the conclusion does not necessarily follow.
• But for his courage, the mission would have failed.
Formal Conditional Inversion:
• Were he to agree, we would proceed immediately.
• Had they been aware of the risks, they would never have proceeded.
• Should the situation change, we will notify you accordingly.
Subtle Distinctions and Nuanced Meaning

At C2, you must demonstrate the ability to express fine shades of meaning, distinguishing between similar concepts with precision.

Distinction Expression Example
Complete vs. Partial agreement I wholeheartedly accept... / I am broadly sympathetic to... I am broadly sympathetic to that view, with certain caveats.
Strong vs. Weak disagreement I find that argument unconvincing... / I am not entirely persuaded... I find that argument unconvincing in light of recent evidence.
Certainty vs. Probability It is indisputable that... / It is reasonable to assume... It is indisputable that the trend has accelerated.
Direct vs. Indirect causation directly led to... / contributed to... / was a factor in... While not directly causing the crisis, it was undoubtedly a contributing factor.
Expressing Subtle Distinctions:
• It is not so much that the theory is wrong, but rather that it is incomplete.
• The distinction between correlation and causation is crucial here.
• While ostensibly similar, the two phenomena differ in substance.
• The effect is negligible at best and potentially counterproductive at worst.

    • Depending on how one defines "success," the outcome could be interpreted variously.
    • The concept is fraught with ambiguity; it resists simple definition.
    Clarifying Meanings:
    • When I say "efficient," I mean not in the narrow sense of speed, but rather optimal resource allocation.
    • To be precise, I am referring to the systemic causes, not the proximate triggers.
    • By X, I do not mean Y; rather, I intend to convey Z.
    Advanced Emphasis and Highlighting
    Cleft Sentences (Advanced Variations):
    • What is particularly striking is the rapidity of the change.
    • It is not until X that we appreciate the full significance of Y.
    • The very idea that such a thing is possible is, in itself, remarkable.
    Fronting for Emphasis:
    • Most significant of all is the impact on future generations.
    • Particularly noteworthy are the findings regarding climate adaptation.
    • Equally compelling is the argument from economic sustainability.
    Reinforcement Structures:
    • Indeed, it could be argued that...
    • In fact, the evidence suggests precisely the opposite.
    • More importantly still, we must consider the ethical implications.

    How to Write the Grade 12 Discursive Essay

    Requirements: At Grade 12, you must prepare a Formal Topic Presentation - a sophisticated discursive essay (approximately 500-600 words) that demonstrates mastery of abstract reasoning, complex argumentation, and elegant expression. You must provide a written copy to the examiner.

    Step 1: Select a Sophisticated Discursive Topic

    Your topic must be philosophically or conceptually sophisticated, allowing for nuanced exploration of abstract ideas.

    Examples of C2-Level Topics:

    • "To what extent does the pursuit of scientific progress justify ethical compromise?"
    • "Is the concept of universal human rights a cultural imposition or a moral imperative?"
    • "Does technological connectivity enhance or diminish authentic human experience?"
    • "Is objectivity in historical narrative achievable or illusory?"
    • "To what extent should individual liberty be sacrificed for collective security?"

    Avoid: Topics that are purely descriptive or require only factual recounting.

    Step 2: Structure Your Argument

    Introduction (80-100 words):

    • Contextualize the debate within broader philosophical or theoretical frameworks
    • Identify the tension or paradox at the heart of the issue
    • Present a nuanced thesis that acknowledges complexity
    • Outline the structure of your argument

    Body Paragraph 1 - Opposing View (120-140 words):

    • Present the strongest version of the counter-argument (steel-manning)
    • Use third-party referencing: "Proponents maintain that..."
    • Demonstrate understanding of underlying assumptions
    • Concede validity where appropriate

    Body Paragraph 2 - Your Position (120-140 words):

    • Present your argument with sophisticated connectors
    • Distinguish between different types of evidence or reasoning
    • Use conditional and hypothetical structures
    • Reference specific examples or thought experiments

    Body Paragraph 3 - Synthesis/Critique (100-120 words):

    • Identify false dichotomies or oversimplifications
    • Propose a nuanced middle position or reconceptualization
    • Exploit ambiguity productively
    • Demonstrate meta-awareness of the argumentation process

    Conclusion (80-100 words):

    • Synthesize without merely repeating
    • Highlight broader implications or unanswered questions
    • End with an elegant, thought-provoking final statement
    • Avoid absolute statements; maintain appropriate hedging

    Step 3: Employ C2-Level Discourse Markers

    Replace standard connectors with sophisticated alternatives:

    • Instead of "but" → that being said, conversely, on the contrary, nevertheless
    • Instead of "also" → furthermore, moreover, in addition, what is more, equally
    • Instead of "so" → consequently, as a result, therefore, thus, hence
    • Instead of "for example" → by way of illustration, to take a specific instance, consider the case of
    • Instead of "I think" → it is reasonable to contend, the evidence suggests, one might argue

    Step 4: Master the Language of Evaluation

    • Highlighting significance: "What is crucial here is...", "The pivotal issue is...", "Of paramount importance is..."
    • Expressing reservations: "This is not without its difficulties...", "One might question whether...", "This presupposes that..."
    • Making distinctions: "It is essential to distinguish between...", "We must differentiate...", "The contrast between X and Y is not merely semantic but substantive"
    • Conceding gracefully: "While there is merit in the view that...", "I am sympathetic to the argument that..., however..."
    Sample C2 Essay Opening:
    "The rapid proliferation of artificial intelligence technologies has precipitated a fundamental reconceptualization of human uniqueness and cognitive superiority. While the prevailing wisdom holds that technological augmentation enhances human capability, this presentation contends that uncritical embrace of AI integration risks eroding the very faculties—critical reasoning, creative intuition, and ethical judgment—that constitute distinctively human intelligence. Through an examination of both the instrumental benefits and the existential costs, I shall argue that sustainable technological development requires not merely efficiency optimization, but the preservation of human agency as an independent, non-instrumental value."
    Key C2 Essay Characteristics:
    • Use of abstract nouns: reconceptualization, augmentation, optimization, agency
    • Complex sentence structures with multiple subordinate clauses
    • Philosophical vocabulary: existential, instrumental, distinctively, sustainable
    • Self-referential meta-discourse: "this presentation contends," "I shall argue"
    • Balanced, non-dogmatic tone with appropriate hedging
    Language for Evaluating Complex Arguments
    Function Advanced Expressions
    Identifying logical flaws "This commits the fallacy of...", "The reasoning is circular in that...", "This begs the question..."
    Recognizing hidden assumptions "This presupposes that...", "The underlying premise appears to be...", "This rests on the questionable assumption..."
    Evaluating evidence quality "The empirical basis for this claim is...", "While anecdotal evidence suggests..., systematic studies indicate..."
    Highlighting implications "The ramifications extend beyond...", "This entails that...", "If taken to its logical conclusion..."
    Proposing alternatives "A more nuanced approach would...", "Rather than viewing this as..., we might conceptualize it as..."

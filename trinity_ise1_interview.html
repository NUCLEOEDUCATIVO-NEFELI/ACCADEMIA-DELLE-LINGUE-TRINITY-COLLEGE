ISE I Interview Complete Guide

Speaking & Listening Tasks, Assessment & Preparation Strategies

Interview Structure Overview

The ISE I Interview is a one-to-one speaking and listening exam with a Trinity examiner. It lasts 18 minutes (16 minutes exam + 2 minutes administration) and consists of 4 assessed tasks.

Exam Breakdown:
  • Topic Discussion Task: 4 minutes (speaking)
  • Conversation Task: 4 minutes (2 min portfolio + 2 min subject area)
  • Independent Listening Task 1: 4.5 minutes
  • Independent Listening Task 2: 5.5 minutes

Before the Exam (2 minutes)

Examiner greets candidate, checks identification, and explains procedure. Candidate hands over Topic Form.

Topic Task (4 minutes)

Discussion of candidate's prepared topic using the Topic Form. Examiner asks questions about all 4 points.

Conversation Task (4 minutes)

2 minutes discussing portfolio + 2 minutes on one subject area chosen by examiner.

Independent Listening Task 1 (4.5 minutes)

Listen twice to recording, answer 6 questions with 1-2 words each.

Independent Listening Task 2 (5.5 minutes)

Listen twice, report gist, report 6 facts, answer 4 follow-up questions.

Key Differences from ISE Foundation (A2)
Feature ISE Foundation (A2) ISE I (B1)
Total Time 13 minutes 18 minutes
Topic Task 4 minutes 4 minutes (more abstract discussion)
Conversation 2 minutes (subject area only) 4 minutes (portfolio + subject area)
Listening Tasks 2 tasks (8 minutes) 2 tasks (10 minutes, more complex)
Language Concrete, everyday topics Abstract ideas, predictions, opinions
Portfolio Discussion Not included 2 minutes mandatory discussion
B1 Language Functions Required

You must demonstrate these functions throughout the interview:

Describing past actions
Indefinite & recent past
"I've been interested in..."
Describing the future
Intentions & predictions
"I'm planning to...", "I think..."
Expressing certainty/uncertainty
"Definitely", "probably", "might"
Giving reasons & opinions
"The reason is...", "In my opinion..."
Expressing obligation
"I have to...", "I need to..."
Asking for information
"What do you think about...?"
Topic Discussion Task 4 minutes
Task Overview: You prepare a topic before the exam and complete a Topic Form with 4 discussion points. The examiner uses this form to ask questions in random order. You must ask the examiner at least one question about your topic.

The Topic Form:

INTEGRATED SKILLS IN ENGLISH — TOPIC FORM (ISE I)

Candidate name: _______________
Title of topic: CRICKET

Discussion Points (4 required):

1. How long I've been interested in cricket
2. Why I love my team
3. My next match
4. What's better – playing or watching?

The examiner will use these points to ask questions. Order is random.

Choosing Your Topic:

✓ Good Topic Choices:
  • Something you genuinely enjoy talking about
  • Allows discussion of past, present, and future
  • Enables demonstration of B1 language functions
  • Not from the 6 conversation subject areas
  • You can bring a small item (photo, ticket, medal) to support discussion
✗ Avoid These Topics:
  • Travel, Money, Fashion, Rules & Regulations, Health & Fitness, Learning a Foreign Language (these are for Conversation task)
  • Topics you can't discuss for 4 minutes
  • Topics requiring specialized knowledge
  • Memorized speeches or presentations

Sample Topic Discussion:

Examiner: "So, tell me about how you became interested in cricket."

Candidate: "Well, I've been interested in cricket since I was about eight years old. My father used to play in a local team, and he took me to watch my first match. I remember being fascinated by the atmosphere in the stadium. Since then, I've been following cricket regularly. In fact, I've been playing in a local team for the past three years."

Examiner: "What about your next match?"

Candidate: "We're playing against our biggest rivals next Saturday. I'm really looking forward to it because we lost to them last season, so we need to win this time. I'm going to practice every evening this week to prepare. Do you enjoy watching cricket, examiner?"
Key Strategies:
  • Speak in sustained turns (don't give one-word answers)
  • Elaborate with examples and details
  • Refer back to previous points and forward to upcoming ones
  • Use a range of tenses: past experiences, current habits, future plans
  • Ask the examiner a genuine question about your topic
  • Don't memorize a script - the examiner will interrupt to encourage spontaneity
Conversation Task 4 minutes
Task Overview: Two parts: (1) 2 minutes discussing your portfolio, and (2) 2 minutes on one subject area chosen by the examiner. You must ask at least one question about the portfolio and one about the subject area.

Part A: Portfolio Discussion (2 minutes)

The examiner has already assessed your portfolio and will ask about your written work. Be prepared to:

  • Explain what you wrote and why you chose those tasks
  • Discuss challenges you faced while writing
  • Talk about what you learned from the process
  • Give examples from your portfolio pieces
  • Ask the examiner at least one question about your portfolio (e.g., "What did you think of my conclusion in the article?")
Sample Exchange:

Examiner: "I see you wrote about learning languages. Why did you choose that topic?"

Candidate: "I chose it because I'm currently learning English and I find the process really interesting. In my article, I discussed different methods like using apps and watching films. I found it challenging to organize my ideas clearly. What did you think of my suggestions for language learning, examiner?"

Part B: Subject Area Conversation (2 minutes)

The examiner chooses one of these 6 subject areas:

Travel
Money
Fashion
Rules & Regulations
Health & Fitness
Learning a Foreign Language
Important: You must ask the examiner at least one question about the subject area. This is mandatory for a pass.

Sample Subject Area Questions:

Travel:
"What's the most interesting place you've visited?"
"Do you prefer traveling alone or with others?"
"How do you think travel will change in the future?"
Money:
"Do you think young people should learn about money management at school?"
"Is it better to save money or spend it on experiences?"
Learning a Foreign Language:
"What do you think is the most difficult thing about learning English?"
"Do you think it's easier to learn a language when you're young?"
Conversation Strategies:
  • Initiate conversation - don't just wait for questions
  • Give opinions with reasons ("I think... because...")
  • Show interest in the examiner's responses
  • Use repair strategies if you don't understand ("Sorry, do you mean...?")
  • Maintain eye contact and use natural body language
Independent Listening Task 1 4.5 minutes
Task Overview: Listen twice to a recording (approximately 1 minute) of basic information (descriptive or narrative). Answer 6 questions with one or two words. You may take notes.

Task Structure:

  • Recording type: Descriptive or narrative (e.g., describing a process, telling a short story)
  • Length: Approximately 1 minute
  • Plays: Twice
  • Questions: 6 factual questions requiring specific information
  • Answers: 1-2 words only (phrases not accepted)
  • Scoring: 1 mark per correct answer (total 6 marks)
Sample Recording (about emus):

"Emus are large birds that live in Australia. They prefer open country rather than forests. They can run very fast - up to 50 kilometres per hour. Emus eat plants and insects. They are brown in color and have very good eyesight."

Sample Questions:
1. "Where do emus live?" → "Australia" (1 word)
2. "What kind of places do they prefer?" → "Open country" (2 words)
3. "How fast can they run?" → "50 kilometres per hour" (too long - just "50 km/h" or "50 kilometres")
4. "What do they eat?" → "Plants" or "Insects" (1 word)
1 First Listening - Get the Gist

Listen for overall understanding. Don't worry about details. Identify the topic and general context.

2 Second Listening - Extract Details

Focus on specific information: numbers, times, dates, names, places, adjectives. Write key words quickly.

Key Strategies:
  • Listen for content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives, numbers) - ignore grammar words
  • Write abbreviations during listening (e.g., "7pm" instead of "seven o'clock")
  • Check spelling if you write full words
  • Answer all 6 questions - no penalty for wrong answers
  • Stick to 1-2 words - longer answers may not be accepted
Independent Listening Task 2 5.5 minutes
Task Overview: Listen twice to a factual exposition (approximately 1 minute 15 seconds). First, report the gist (main idea). Then report 6 facts from the recording. Finally, answer 4 follow-up questions from the examiner.

Task Structure:

Phase 1 - First Listening:
Listen for gist. The examiner will ask: "What is the speaker talking about?" or "What is the main topic?"

Your response: A general statement in 1-2 sentences (e.g., "The speaker is talking about the benefits of learning foreign languages and how it affects brain development.")
Phase 2 - Second Listening:
Listen for specific facts. Take notes on the provided paper.

After listening: Report 6 facts to the examiner. These can be simple statements.
Example: "The speaker said that learning languages improves memory." / "He mentioned that bilingual people are better at multitasking."
Phase 3 - Follow-up Questions:
The examiner asks 4 questions about the recording content.

Example: "According to the speaker, why do children learn languages faster than adults?"

Assessment Criteria (Task 2 only):

Score Description
4 Identifies main points and specific details accurately. Responds promptly and accurately to questions.
3 Identifies main points and most specific details. Responds to questions with minor hesitation or inaccuracy.
2 Identifies some main points and details. Requires repetition of questions or provides incomplete answers.
1 Identifies few points. Has difficulty responding to questions even when repeated.
0 No performance to assess or task not attempted.
Note-Taking Tips:
  • Use abbreviations and symbols (&, +, →, ↑)
  • Write keywords only - don't try to write full sentences
  • Organize notes spatially (left side for main ideas, right for details)
  • Number your facts 1-6 as you hear them
  • Notes are not assessed - only your oral responses count
How the Interview is Assessed

The interview is assessed using different methods for speaking and listening tasks:

Speaking Assessment (Topic + Conversation Tasks)

Both speaking tasks are assessed together using four criteria on a scale of 1-4:

Criterion What Examiners Look For Weight
Communicative Effectiveness Task fulfilment, appropriate turn-taking, initiating and maintaining interaction, elaborating responses 25%
Interactive Listening Understanding examiner questions, responding appropriately, asking for clarification when needed 25%
Language Control Range and accuracy of grammar, vocabulary precision, B1 structures (conditionals, passives, modals) 25%
Delivery Pronunciation, intonation, fluency, appropriate speed, clear articulation 25%

Score Interpretation:

Score Meaning
4 Excellent achievement - at the upper end of B1
3 Appropriate achievement - at the middle of B1
2 Acceptable achievement - of B1 level, possibly newly qualified
1 Non-achievement - not at B1 level
0 No performance to rate
Listening Assessment

Task 1 Scoring:

Objective scoring: 1 mark per correct answer (6 marks total)

Task 2 Scoring:

Rating scale 0-4: Based on number of facts reported correctly and promptness of response

Combined Listening Score (out of 8):
  • Task 1: 6 marks (objective)
  • Task 2: Rated 0-4, then weighted
  • Total converted to score out of 8

Pass Requirements:

Skill Total Marks Pass Merit Distinction
Speaking 16 8 12 15
Listening 8 4 5 7
Sample Performance Descriptors
Score 3 (Appropriate achievement) - Communicative Effectiveness:
"Fulfils the task appropriately. Initiates and responds appropriately. Maintains and develops the interaction appropriately (e.g., expanding and developing ideas, and showing understanding of what the examiner said). Deals with communication problems well."
Score 2 (Acceptable achievement) - Language Control:
"Uses an acceptable range of grammatical structures/lexis to deal with topics at this level. Shows a relatively acceptable level of grammatical accuracy and lexical precision. Errors do not impede communication."
Score 4 (Excellent achievement) - Delivery:
"Clearly intelligible. Uses focal stress and intonation effectively. Speaks promptly and fluently. Requires no careful listening."
Topic Task Preparation
1 Choose Your Topic Wisely

Select something you genuinely enjoy and can talk about for 4 minutes. Avoid the 6 conversation subject areas. Consider: hobbies, sports, music, films, books, personal experiences.

2 Plan Your 4 Discussion Points

Each point should allow you to demonstrate B1 language functions:

  • Point 1: Past experiences ("How I started...")
  • Point 2: Present habits/routines ("What I do now...")
  • Point 3: Future plans ("What I'm going to do...")
  • Point 4: Opinions/comparisons ("What I think about...")
3 Practice with a Partner

Give your Topic Form to a friend/teacher. Have them ask questions in random order. Practice:

  • Speaking for 30-45 seconds per answer
  • Using varied tenses and vocabulary
  • Asking questions back to the examiner
  • Recovering from interruptions
4 Prepare a Small Item

You can bring a photo, ticket, medal, or small object to support your topic. This can help if you get nervous - it gives you something concrete to refer to.

Conversation Task Preparation

Portfolio Discussion:

  • Remember what you wrote in your portfolio
  • Prepare to explain why you chose each task
  • Think about difficulties you faced and how you solved them
  • Prepare at least 2 questions to ask the examiner about your work

Subject Area Preparation:

Practice discussing all 6 subject areas. For each, prepare to:

  • Give personal experiences
  • Express opinions with reasons
  • Make predictions about the future
  • Ask the examiner questions
Sample Questions to Ask the Examiner:
  • "Do you think...?"
  • "Have you ever...?"
  • "What do you think about...?"
  • "Do you prefer... or...?"
  • "Why do you think...?"
Listening Task Preparation

Task 1 Preparation:

  • Practice listening for specific information (numbers, dates, names)
  • Train yourself to write abbreviations quickly
  • Practice answering in exactly 1-2 words
  • Listen to short recordings about everyday topics

Task 2 Preparation:

  • Practice identifying gist (main topic) vs. details
  • Develop note-taking skills using abbreviations
  • Practice reporting facts in your own words
  • Listen to short talks/lectures and summarize
Recommended Practice Resources:
  • Trinity sample audio files at trinitycollege.com/ISEI
  • BBC Learning English - 6 Minute English
  • Short documentaries or educational podcasts
  • Practice with sample questions from Trinity website
Exam Day Tips

Before the Exam

  • Arrive early and bring your ID
  • Bring your Topic Form (completed)
  • Bring a pen for note-taking
  • Wear comfortable clothes
  • Stay calm - the examiner wants you to succeed

During the Speaking Tasks

  • Speak clearly and at a natural pace
  • Don't memorize scripts - be spontaneous
  • If you don't understand, ask for repetition
  • Make eye contact with the examiner
  • Remember to ask your mandatory questions

During the Listening Tasks

  • Use the preparation time to read questions
  • Take notes even if you're not sure - you hear it twice
  • Don't leave answers blank - guess if unsure
  • For Task 2, number your facts 1-6 as you hear them

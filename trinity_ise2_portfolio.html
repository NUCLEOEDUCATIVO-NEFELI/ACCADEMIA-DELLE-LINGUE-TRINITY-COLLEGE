Portfolio Component Overview (B2)

The Portfolio is a collection of your best written work submitted before the Speaking & Listening interview. At ISE II (B2), you must complete 3 tasks selected from a published list of 15 tasks, divided into three sections.

Key Points:
  • One task from each section (you cannot choose two from the same section)
  • Word counts are strict - any text over the maximum will not be marked
  • Best work - you have time to draft, edit, and use reference materials
  • Preparation period - typically 6-12 weeks recommended
  • 20% of total exam score - weighted equally with Controlled Written exam
Important: Tasks must be selected from the current year's official Trinity task list (January-December). No other tasks are acceptable. Check the Trinity website for the current list.

Portfolio Preparation Rules
Critical Rules - Breaking these results in ZERO marks:
1 Original Work Only

The portfolio must contain the candidate's own work and no one else's. Plagiarism (copying from any source and presenting it as your own) results in an 'Ungraded' (U) mark and zero score.

2 No Teacher Correction

Nobody except the candidate personally should correct, add to, or take anything out of the task. Teachers must NOT correct the work - only provide one feedback form per task.

3 One Feedback Form Only

Teachers are allowed to give one piece of feedback per task only, using the official Student Portfolio Feedback Form. This must be the only form of written feedback.

4 Word Count Strictly Enforced

Any text over the stated maximum word length range will NOT be marked and will affect the grade awarded. Write the word count at the end of each task.

5 Current Task List Only

Tasks must be selected from the current year's published list (January-December). No other tasks are acceptable. Do not mix levels (e.g., don't use ISE I tasks for ISE II).

Submission Requirements

Required Documents:

  • Portfolio Cover Sheet - Completed with candidate details, task numbers, and word counts
  • Three Student Portfolio Feedback Forms - One for each task (completed by teacher)
  • Colour-coded plastic wallet - Provided by Trinity (green for ISE II)
  • Three final tasks - One from each section, in section order (1, 2, 3)
Submission Timeline:
  • Portfolio must be submitted to teacher at least 24 hours before the examiner's arrival
  • Candidate must NOT have access to portfolio between submission and interview
  • Results provided on Results Summary Slip (not on day of interview)
Section 1: Correspondence (120-150 words)
Task Overview: Write a letter or email demonstrating your ability to produce personal or formal correspondence using appropriate language styles and conventions.

Genres in Section 1:

  • Formal Letters - To councils, editors, ministers, companies
  • Informal Emails - To friends, colleagues, family
  • Letters of Application - For jobs, volunteer positions
  • Reference Letters - Recommending someone

Sample Task Examples:

Example 1 (Formal):
"Your local council is planning to close the market which sells local produce and products. Write a letter to the council expressing your disagreement with the plan, highlighting the advantages of the market and persuading the council not to close it."
Example 2 (Informal):
"You have received an email from a friend who has moved to a new city and is very homesick. Reply to your friend and give her some advice on how she can make new friends and settle into her new surroundings."
Example 3 (Persuasive):
"Your country is taking part in an international food fair. Write a letter to the organisers suggesting what foods from your area to include and explaining how this event could benefit your area. Persuade the organisers to send you some free tickets for the fair."

B2 Language Requirements for Correspondence:

Feature Formal Letter Informal Email
Opening Dear Sir/Madam, Dear Editor, Hi [name], Hello [name],
Closing Yours faithfully/sincerely, Best wishes, Take care,
Language "I am writing to express...", "I would like to highlight..." "I was thinking...", "You might want to..."
Modals "would," "could," "should" (polite) "can," "might," "could" (casual)
Functions Persuading, complaining, recommending Advising, empathising, sharing news
Success Criteria for Section 1:
  • ‚úì Appropriate format (addresses, dates if required)
  • ‚úì Consistent register throughout (formal OR informal)
  • ‚úì Clear purpose stated early
  • ‚úì All bullet points from task addressed
  • ‚úì Range of B2 grammar (conditionals, passives, relative clauses)
Section 2: Factual Writing (170-200 words)
Task Overview: Write a report, article, or review that conveys information and ideas clearly, with the type of text that might appear in an English language newspaper or magazine.

Genres in Section 2:

  • Articles - For magazines, newspapers, websites (informative or opinion-based)
  • Reports - For managers, principals, organisations (objective and structured)
  • Reviews - Of films, books, documentaries, events (balanced evaluation)

Sample Task Examples:

Example 1 (Article):
"Write an article for a local newspaper describing how the region where you live has changed since you were born. Describe how life used to be different, with examples from your own experience and comment on whether or not life has changed for the better."
Example 2 (Report):
"The principal of your college has said that there is some money available to improve one of the facilities at your college. Write a report for the principal highlighting the advantages of improving the facility of your choice. Explain how it should be improved and express the feelings of the other students on this change."
Example 3 (Review):
"Write a review for a website of a film that reflects life in a particular section of society in your country. Outline what aspects of society the film represents, how realistic you feel this image of society is and if you would recommend this film to someone from another country."

Structure by Genre:

Genre Structure Key Features
Article Title ‚Üí Introduction ‚Üí Body (2-3 paras) ‚Üí Conclusion Engaging headline, direct address to reader, personal opinion mixed with facts
Report Title ‚Üí Introduction ‚Üí Findings ‚Üí Conclusions ‚Üí Recommendations Objective tone, headings/subheadings, formal language, evidence-based
Review Introduction ‚Üí Description ‚Üí Evaluation ‚Üí Recommendation Balanced opinion, specific examples, clear recommendation, appropriate register

B2 Language for Factual Writing:

  • Discourse markers: "Furthermore," "However," "Consequently," "In contrast"
  • Passive voice: "It has been suggested that...", "The report was written..."
  • Reporting verbs: "claim," "argue," "suggest," "acknowledge," "emphasise"
  • Evaluation language: "significant," "notable," "problematic," "effective"
  • Conditional structures: "If this trend continues...", "Were this to happen..."
Success Criteria for Section 2:
  • ‚úì Clear organisation with appropriate paragraphs
  • ‚úì Evidence and examples to support points
  • ‚úì Evaluation and analysis (not just description)
  • ‚úì Appropriate register for target audience
  • ‚úì Cohesion through linking devices
Section 3: Creative/Descriptive Writing (170-200 words)
Task Overview: Use language imaginatively to write stories, diary entries, or descriptions that demonstrate your ability to convey emotion, atmosphere, and narrative effectively.

Genres in Section 3:

  • Stories - With opening/closing lines provided or free choice
  • Diary Entries - Personal reflections on experiences
  • Descriptions - Of places, people, memories, imagined scenarios

Sample Task Examples:

Example 1 (Story with prompt):
"Write a story for a writing competition beginning with the words, 'I had only lived there a few weeks when I started noticing strange things happening'."
Example 2 (Diary):
"A childhood friend who you have not seen for a long time has become a politician. Recently you met him/her by chance. Write a diary entry describing your feelings when you met, saying what your friend told you about life as a politician and saying whether you would like to change places with him/her."
Example 3 (Description):
"If you were stranded on a desert island and could only bring three things, what would they be? Describe the three items and explain why you wouldn't want to be without them."

B2 Creative Writing Techniques:

Technique Example Effect
Sensory details "The air smelled of salt and decay" Creates atmosphere
Varied sentence length Short sentences for tension; long for description Controls pace
Figurative language "The waves were mountains of liquid glass" Creates vivid imagery
Internal monologue "What was I thinking? This couldn't end well." Reveals character
Speculation "If only I had known...", "Little did I realise..." Adds depth/reflection

Narrative Tenses at B2:

  • Past Simple: Main events of the story
  • Past Continuous: Setting the scene, interrupted actions
  • Past Perfect: Events before the main narrative
  • Would for past habits: "We would spend summers at the beach"
  • Conditionals: "If I had known, I would have acted differently"
Success Criteria for Section 3:
  • ‚úì Engaging opening that captures attention
  • ‚úì Clear narrative arc (beginning, middle, end) or coherent description
  • ‚úì Show don't tell (use sensory details, not just statements)
  • ‚úì Appropriate use of narrative tenses
  • ‚úì Consistent tone and voice throughout
Portfolio Assessment Criteria
Grading System: Portfolios are assessed using a five-point scale (A-E) by the examiner before the interview. There is one overall assessment criterion: Task Fulfilment.

Grade Descriptors:

Grade Descriptor Characteristics
A Excellent All task requirements met; sophisticated use of language; very well organised; wide range of vocabulary and structures; minimal errors; highly appropriate register
B Very Good All task requirements met; good range of language; well organised; appropriate register; minor errors that don't impede communication
C Good Most task requirements met; adequate range of language; generally well organised; some errors but meaning clear
D Satisfactory Basic task requirements met; limited range of language; some organisational issues; errors may impede communication at times
E Unsatisfactory Task requirements not fully met; limited language control; poor organisation; frequent errors that impede communication

Factors Considered in Task Fulfilment:

1 Overall Achievement of Communicative Aim

Does the text achieve its purpose? Is the message clear? Does it address all parts of the task?

2 Text Organisation

Are paragraphs well-structured? Is there logical progression? Are linking devices used effectively?

3 Appropriacy of Format, Style and Register

Is the format correct (letter layout, report headings)? Is the register consistent and appropriate for the audience?

4 Accuracy and Range of Language

Is there a range of B2 grammatical structures (conditionals, passives, relative clauses)? Is vocabulary precise and varied? How accurate is the language?

5 Effect on Reader

Is the text engaging? Is the message communicated effectively? Does it hold the reader's attention?

Important Notes on Assessment:
  • Examiners expect a very high degree of accuracy in portfolios (you've had time to draft and use resources)
  • Spelling and punctuation should be thoroughly checked (use spell-checkers)
  • Word count over the maximum means that text is not marked
  • Tasks do not have equal weighting: Section 1 = 20%, Sections 2 & 3 = 40% each
Common Mistakes to Avoid
Mistake Why It Matters How to Avoid
Exceeding word count Excess text not marked; affects grade Use word count tool; edit ruthlessly; check before submission
Teacher correction on draft Results in zero (Ungraded) Teacher uses feedback form only; student makes all corrections
Mixing registers Inconsistent tone; lower grade Decide formal/informal and stick to it throughout
Missing bullet points Incomplete task fulfilment Underline key requirements; checklist before final draft
Plagiarism Zero marks; possible disqualification All work must be original; quote and attribute if using sources
Poor paragraphing Organisation issues; affects coherence Plan structure; one main idea per paragraph; use topic sentences

Portfolio Preparation Checklist

Use this checklist to ensure your portfolio is ready for submission:

  • I have selected one task from each section (1, 2, and 3)
  • All tasks are from the current year's official Trinity list
  • I have not mixed levels (all tasks are ISE II)
  • Each task has the correct word count (120-150 for Section 1; 170-200 for Sections 2 & 3)
  • I have written the word count at the end of each task
  • I have written the task question number on each piece of work
  • I have completed the Portfolio Cover Sheet
  • I have attached one Student Portfolio Feedback Form to each task
  • All work is my own original writing (no plagiarism)
  • No one except me has corrected the tasks
  • I have used spell-checkers and dictionaries
  • I have checked formatting (paragraphs, headings, addresses)
  • I have placed tasks in section order (1, 2, 3) in the colour-coded wallet
